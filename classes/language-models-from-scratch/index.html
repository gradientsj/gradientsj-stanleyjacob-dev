Building a language model from scratch: tokenizer to trained checkpoint

Every frontier language model since roughly 2020 is the same object: a byte-pair-encoded token stream fed through a stack of pre-norm decoder blocks, trained with next-token cross-entropy on a few trillion deduplicated tokens, sharded across a few thousand accelerators, then served through a paged KV cache. This page walks that pipeline end to end and derives each piece rather than naming it: the BPE merge rule with a real counted trace, the rotation algebra that makes RoPE encode relative position, the online-softmax recurrence that makes FlashAttention exact, the 6ND FLOPs approximation component by component, the communication arithmetic of ZeRO, tensor, and pipeline parallelism, the Chinchilla allocation as a constrained optimization, and the rejection-sampling argument that makes speculative decoding lossless. Measured numbers come from an H100 80GB in this repository. A reader who finishes it should be able to budget, build, train, and serve a small model without consulting anything else.

Why this subject matters now

Ten years ago, "training a language model" meant fitting an LSTM on a single GPU and the hard parts were optimization tricks. Today the model itself is almost boringly standardized: nearly every serious system, from Llama to Qwen to DeepSeek to the closed frontier models, is a decoder-only transformer with rotary positions, RMS normalization, a gated MLP, and some form of grouped or latent attention for the KV cache. What changed is where the difficulty lives. The open problems a practitioner actually faces are quantitative and systemic: how many parameters and how many tokens a fixed compute budget should buy, whether a kernel is bound by arithmetic or by memory bandwidth, how much each parallelism strategy communicates per step, how much HBM a KV cache eats at a given batch and context, and what an inference token costs at serving time. None of these questions are answered by knowing what attention is; all of them are answered by arithmetic that fits on one page once the right approximations are derived.

The economics force the issue. A single H100 80GB in this repository measures 744.6 TFLOPS on a 4096-square bf16 matmul and 2992.4 GB/s of memory bandwidth; a pretraining run at the scale of a 70B model consumes on the order of \(10^{24}\) FLOPs, months of thousands of such devices. At that price, a 10% error in FLOPs accounting or a mis-sized KV cache is not a rounding error, it is a seven-figure line item. The material below is the set of derivations that let those numbers be computed rather than guessed. A sibling page covers the pre-transformer lineage, embeddings, recurrent models, and the encoder side of pretraining; this page owns the modern decoder-only pipeline from raw bytes to a served checkpoint.

The tokenizer: byte-pair encoding, derived

Why subwords, and why bytes underneath

A language model needs a finite vocabulary over which its softmax is defined. Word-level vocabularies fail on unseen words, code, URLs, and morphology-rich languages; character-level vocabularies make sequences several times longer, and since attention cost grows with sequence length and information per position drops, both compute and modeling suffer. Subword tokenization is the compromise the field settled on: frequent strings become single tokens, rare strings decompose into pieces, and nothing is ever out of vocabulary. Byte-pair encoding, adapted from a 1994 compression algorithm to translation by Sennrich, Haddow, and Birch (2016), builds this vocabulary greedily from data. Modern systems following GPT-2 run BPE over raw bytes rather than Unicode characters, so the base alphabet is exactly 256 symbols and any string, in any script, with any mojibake, is encodable by construction.

The training procedure is a greedy frequency count. Start from a corpus split by a pretokenizer (a regex that keeps words, numbers, and punctuation apart, so merges never cross a space), represent each pretoken as a sequence of base symbols, and repeat: count every adjacent symbol pair, weighted by pretoken frequency; merge the most frequent pair into a new symbol; add that symbol to the vocabulary and record the merge rule. After \(k\) merges the vocabulary has \(256 + k\) entries plus special tokens. Encoding a new string replays the recorded merges in training order, earliest first, which is why the merge list, not just the vocabulary, is the tokenizer. Each merge strictly shortens the token sequence wherever it applies, so the greedy objective is compression: pick the merge that removes the most symbols from the corpus in this round.

Problem 1

A corpus pretokenizes to five distinct words with frequencies: seed 7, see 5, bee 4, bed 3, deed 2. The base alphabet is {s, e, d, b}. Run three BPE merges by hand, showing the full pair-count table at each step, and then tokenize the unseen word bees with the learned merges.

Solution. Round 1. The symbol sequences are s e e d (7), s e e (5), b e e (4), b e d (3), d e e d (2). Adjacent-pair counts, weighted by word frequency: (e,e) appears once in each of seed, see, bee, deed, so 7+5+4+2 = 18; (s,e) appears in seed and see, 7+5 = 12; (e,d) appears in seed, bed, deed, 7+3+2 = 12; (b,e) in bee and bed, 4+3 = 7; (d,e) only in deed, 2. The winner is (e,e) with 18. Merge it into the symbol "ee". Note that seed contributes only one (e,e) pair, not two: after the first "ee" is formed greedily left to right there is no second adjacent e pair.

Round 2. Sequences are now s ee d (7), s ee (5), b ee (4), b e d (3), d ee d (2). Counts: (s,ee) 7+5 = 12; (ee,d) 7+2 = 9; (b,ee) 4; (b,e) 3; (e,d) 3; (d,ee) 2. Winner: (s,ee) with 12, forming "see".

Round 3. Sequences: see d (7), see (5), b ee (4), b e d (3), d ee d (2). Counts: (see,d) 7; (b,ee) 4; (b,e) 3; (e,d) 3; (d,ee) 2; (ee,d) 2. Winner: (see,d) with 7, forming "seed". The learned merge list is [(e,e), (s,ee), (see,d)] and the vocabulary is {s, e, d, b, ee, see, seed}.

Encoding bees: start from b e e s. Merge 1 applies: b ee s. Merge 2 looks for the pair (s, ee), but the sequence contains (ee, s), which is a different ordered pair, so it does not apply; merge 3 does not apply either. The output is [b, ee, s], three tokens. The word was never seen in training, yet it is encoded using learned structure, which is the whole point.

Engineering the vocabulary

Vocabulary size trades parameters against sequence length. With embedding dimension \(d\) and untied input and output matrices, a vocabulary of size \(V\) costs \(2Vd\) parameters: at \(V = 128{,}000\) and \(d = 4096\) that is roughly a billion parameters before the first block. Against that, a bigger vocabulary compresses text into fewer tokens, and fewer tokens means less attention compute and effectively more text per unit of training budget. The empirical fertility of modern tokenizers on English is in the neighborhood of 3.5 to 4.5 characters per token, and multilingual coverage is the main pressure pushing vocabularies from GPT-2's 50,257 toward the 128K-256K range used by Llama 3 and Gemma. Two implementation details matter more than they look. First, the pretokenizer regex is load-bearing: GPT-2's regex splits off contractions and keeps a leading space attached to words, and inconsistencies here are a classic source of subtle train/serve mismatch. Second, digits are usually split to single characters or small groups, because letting BPE form tokens like "2016" wholesale gives the model an arbitrary, frequency-skewed numeral vocabulary that quietly damages arithmetic.

The reference implementation below trains on the toy corpus from Problem 1 and reproduces its trace exactly. It is quadratic per merge for clarity; production trainers (SentencePiece, HuggingFace tokenizers) maintain a heap of pair counts with incremental updates and run in near-linear time. 

import collections

def train_bpe(word_counts, num_merges):
    """word_counts: pretoken -> corpus frequency, from the pretokenizer."""
    seqs = {w: list(w) for w in word_counts}           # symbol sequences
    merges = []
    for _ in range(num_merges):
        pairs = collections.Counter()
        for w, sym in seqs.items():
            for a, b in zip(sym, sym[1:]):
                pairs[(a, b)] += word_counts[w]        # weight by frequency
        if not pairs:
            break
        best = max(pairs, key=pairs.get)               # most frequent pair
        merges.append(best)
        for w, sym in seqs.items():                    # apply the merge
            out, i = [], 0
            while i < len(sym):
                if i + 1 < len(sym) and (sym[i], sym[i + 1]) == best:
                    out.append(sym[i] + sym[i + 1]); i += 2
                else:
                    out.append(sym[i]); i += 1
            seqs[w] = out
    return merges

def encode(word, merges):
    """Replay merges in training order: earlier merges take priority."""
    sym = list(word)
    for a, b in merges:
        out, i = [], 0
        while i < len(sym):
            if i + 1 < len(sym) and sym[i] == a and sym[i + 1] == b:
                out.append(a + b); i += 2
            else:
                out.append(sym[i]); i += 1
        sym = out
    return sym

counts = {"seed": 7, "see": 5, "bee": 4, "bed": 3, "deed": 2}
merges = train_bpe(counts, 3)
print(merges)                    # [('e','e'), ('s','ee'), ('see','d')]
print(encode("bees", merges))    # ['b', 'ee', 's']  -- matches Problem 1

The decoder block, component by component

The model is an embedding table, \(L\) identical blocks, a final normalization, and an unembedding projection to vocabulary logits. Each block is two residual sub-layers in the pre-norm arrangement: the input is normalized, transformed, and added back to the untouched residual stream, \(x \leftarrow x + \mathrm{Attn}(\mathrm{Norm}(x))\) then \(x \leftarrow x + \mathrm{MLP}(\mathrm{Norm}(x))\). Pre-norm won over the original post-norm placement for a concrete reason: with normalization inside the branch, the residual path is an identity from any layer to the output, so gradients reach early layers with unit scale regardless of depth, and hundred-layer models train without the careful warmup that post-norm requires. The price is a residual stream whose norm grows with depth, which is why a final norm before the unembedding is mandatory. What follows derives the four places where the modern block departs from the 2017 original: RMSNorm instead of LayerNorm, rotary instead of additive positions, a gated SwiGLU instead of the two-matrix ReLU MLP, and grouped-query instead of full multi-head attention.

RMSNorm: what LayerNorm was actually doing

LayerNorm computes \( y = g \odot \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} + b \) with \( \mu = \frac{1}{d}\sum_i x_i \) and \( \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{d}\sum_i (x_i-\mu)^2 \): two reductions over the feature dimension, a subtraction, a division, and two learned vectors. Zhang and Sennrich (2019) asked which parts earn their keep and found that re-centering is dispensable. RMSNorm keeps only the rescaling:

$$ \mathrm{RMSNorm}(x) = \frac{x}{\mathrm{RMS}(x)} \odot g, \qquad \mathrm{RMS}(x) = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{d}\textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{d} x_i^2 + \epsilon}. $$

The property that matters is scale invariance: for any \(\alpha > 0\), \(\mathrm{RMSNorm}(\alpha x) = \mathrm{RMSNorm}(x)\), because the RMS in the denominator scales by the same \(\alpha\). So the block's input magnitude is decoupled from the growing residual stream, exactly the stabilization LayerNorm provided, without the mean statistic. The gradient shows the same regularization. Writing \(\hat{x} = x/\mathrm{RMS}(x)\) and differentiating,

$$ \frac{\partial \hat{x}_i}{\partial x_j} = \frac{\delta_{ij}}{\mathrm{RMS}(x)} - \frac{x_i x_j}{d\,\mathrm{RMS}(x)^3} = \frac{1}{\mathrm{RMS}(x)}\Big(\delta_{ij} - \frac{\hat{x}_i \hat{x}_j}{d}\Big), $$

a projection that removes the component of the gradient along \(x\) itself and shrinks everything by the input's RMS: large activations receive proportionally damped gradients. Practically, RMSNorm replaces two reductions with one and drops the bias, a measurable win at scale since normalization is bandwidth-bound, and every prominent open model since Llama uses it. One precision note that recurs in real codebases: the sum of squares is computed in fp32 even when the surrounding block runs in bf16, because a long reduction in a format with 8 mantissa bits accumulates error linearly in \(d\).

Rotary position embeddings, derived from the requirement

Attention is permutation-equivariant; position must be injected. The original design added a position vector to each embedding, which entangles content with absolute location: the score between positions \(m\) and \(n\) becomes a sum of four terms, and nothing constrains it to depend on the offset \(n - m\), which is the quantity language actually cares about. Su et al. (2021) posed the requirement directly. Find transformations \(f(q, m)\) applied to queries and \(f(k, n)\) applied to keys such that the score is a function of the contents and the relative offset only:

$$ \langle f(q, m),\, f(k, n) \rangle = g(q, k, n - m) \quad \text{for all } m, n. $$

Work in two dimensions first and identify \(\R^2\) with the complex plane. Try \(f(q, m) = q\, e^{i m \theta}\), a rotation of \(q\) by angle \(m\theta\). The real inner product of complex numbers is \(\langle u, v\rangle = \mathrm{Re}(\bar{u} v)\), so

$$ \langle q e^{i m\theta},\, k e^{i n\theta} \rangle = \mathrm{Re}\big(\bar{q} k\, e^{i (n-m)\theta}\big), $$

which depends on \(m\) and \(n\) only through \(n - m\): the requirement is met exactly, not approximately. In matrix form the same statement is that rotation matrices commute and compose by angle addition, \(R_m^{\!\T} R_n = R_{n-m}\), so \((R_m q)^{\!\T}(R_n k) = q^{\!\T} R_{n-m}\, k\). This is also the reason the transformation must be a rotation and not a general linear map: orthogonality is what turns two absolute positions into one relative one inside a dot product.

To lift this to head dimension \(d_h\), split the vector into \(d_h/2\) two-dimensional pairs and rotate pair \(i\) by angle \(m\theta_i\) with a geometric frequency ladder \(\theta_i = b^{-2i/d_h}\), base \(b = 10{,}000\) classically. The block-diagonal rotation applied to a query at position \(m\) is

$$ f(q, m)_{2i:2i+2} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos m\theta_i & -\sin m\theta_i \\ \sin m\theta_i & \cos m\theta_i \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} q_{2i} \\ q_{2i+1} \end{pmatrix}, $$

implemented as two fused multiplies against precomputed cosine and sine tables rather than a matmul, since the matrix is 2×2-block-diagonal. The fast pairs (\(\theta_0 = 1\), a full radian per position) resolve local order; the slow pairs (\(\theta_{d_h/2-1} \approx 1/b\), a period of tens of thousands of positions) carry coarse long-range structure, the same multi-scale trick as the original sinusoidal table but applied multiplicatively where it can cancel inside the score. Three consequences follow directly from the derivation. Values are never rotated, because the output should carry content, not position. The KV cache stores rotated keys, so cached entries never need updating as the sequence grows, which absolute-affine relative schemes cannot offer. And the norm of every vector is preserved, so RoPE composes safely with the softmax temperature analysis. The frequency base has become a tuning knob: because pairs with \(m\theta_i\) beyond the trained range are out-of-distribution, context extension methods (positional interpolation, NTK-aware scaling, YaRN) all amount to remapping the ladder \(\theta_i\) after training, and models aimed at long context now train with bases of 500,000 (Llama 3) or more from the start.

SwiGLU: gating the MLP

The original position-wise MLP is \(\mathrm{FFN}(x) = \max(0, xW_1)W_2\) with hidden width \(4d\). Shazeer (2020) revisited the family of gated linear units, where one linear projection is passed through a nonlinearity and multiplies a second, linear projection elementwise:

$$ \mathrm{SwiGLU}(x) = \big(\mathrm{swish}(x W_{\text{gate}}) \odot x W_{\text{up}}\big)\, W_{\text{down}}, \qquad \mathrm{swish}(z) = z\,\sigma(z). $$

The gate makes the layer a data-dependent selector: the \(W_{\text{up}}\) path carries candidate features and the swish-activated gate decides, per coordinate and per token, how much of each passes. Because there are now three matrices instead of two, holding the parameter count fixed requires shrinking the hidden width by a factor of \(2/3\): the classic \(2 \times d \times 4d = 8d^2\) budget becomes \(3 \times d \times d_{ff}\), so \(d_{ff} = \tfrac{8}{3}d \approx 2.67d\), then rounded up to a hardware-friendly multiple. For \(d = 2048\): \(\tfrac{2}{3} \cdot 8192 = 5461.3\), and Llama-style configs round to \(d_{ff} = 5632 = 22 \times 256\). The paper is frank that it offers no theoretical account of why the gated variants win; they simply do, consistently, by a fraction of a point of perplexity at equal parameters, and at frontier training budgets a reliable fraction of a point is enormous. Every major open model since 2022 (Llama, Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek) ships SwiGLU or the closely related GeGLU.

Grouped-query attention and the KV-cache arithmetic

At inference, autoregressive decoding caches each layer's keys and values so that generating token \(t+1\) costs one new column of KV rather than a recomputation of the prefix. The cache is the dominant memory consumer at serving time, and its size follows from counting. With \(L\) layers, \(n_{kv}\) key-value heads of dimension \(d_h\), and \(p\) bytes per element, each token stores keys and values in every layer:

$$ \text{bytes per token} = 2 \cdot L \cdot n_{kv} \cdot d_h \cdot p, $$

and a batch of \(B\) sequences at context length \(S\) multiplies this by \(BS\). Multi-head attention sets \(n_{kv} = n_h\) and pays full price. Multi-query attention (MQA), the opposite extreme, shares a single K and V head across all query heads, dividing cache and KV-projection bandwidth by \(n_h\) but measurably hurting quality and destabilizing training at scale. Ainslie et al. (2023) interpolated: grouped-query attention gives each group of \(n_h / n_{kv}\) query heads one shared KV head. The cache shrinks by \(n_h / n_{kv}\), typically 4-8x, while quality stays within noise of full MHA, and an existing MHA checkpoint can be converted by mean-pooling each group's KV projections followed by a short uptraining run (about 5% of the original pretraining compute in the paper). The asymmetry that makes this nearly free is that queries exist only for the token being generated while keys and values exist for the whole history: cutting KV heads attacks the term that scales with context, and the query side, which does not scale, keeps its full capacity.

Problem 2

A 7B-class model has 32 layers, 32 query heads of dimension 128, and stores its cache in bf16. (a) Compute the KV-cache size per token and for a batch of 16 sequences at 8192-token context under full multi-head attention. (b) Repeat with grouped-query attention using 8 KV heads. (c) The weights occupy 13.5 GB in bf16 on an 80 GB device; how many concurrent 8192-token sequences fit in the remaining memory under each scheme? Verify against the formula before trusting any serving-cost estimate built on it.

Solution. (a) Per token: \(2 \times 32 \times 32 \times 128 \times 2\) bytes = 524,288 bytes = 512 KiB. The batch holds \(16 \times 8192 = 131{,}072\) token slots, so the cache is \(131{,}072 \times 512\,\text{KiB} = 64\) GiB (68.7 GB). That is five times the size of the weights, for one batch.

(b) With \(n_{kv} = 8\): per token \(2 \times 32 \times 8 \times 128 \times 2 = 131{,}072\) bytes = 128 KiB, a 4x reduction as predicted by \(n_h/n_{kv} = 32/8\). The batch cache is 16 GiB.

(c) Free memory is roughly \(80 - 13.5 = 66.5\) GB. One full-context sequence costs \(8192 \times 512\,\text{KiB} = 4.29\) GB under MHA, so \(66.5 / 4.29 \approx 15\) sequences fit. Under GQA each costs 1.07 GB and \(66.5 / 1.07 \approx 62\) fit. Decode throughput at fixed latency scales roughly with the batch size the cache allows, so this one architectural choice quadruples serving capacity per GPU. That is why GQA is a pretraining-time decision with an inference-side payoff, and why every major model since Llama 2 70B has shipped with it.

FlashAttention and the online softmax

The problem is bandwidth, not FLOPs

Naive attention materializes the \(S \times S\) score matrix in HBM: one kernel writes \(QK^{\!\T}\), another reads it to compute softmax, a third reads the probabilities to multiply by \(V\). The arithmetic is cheap relative to the traffic. On the H100 80GB in this repository, a bf16 4096-square matmul sustains 744.6 TFLOPS while HBM copy bandwidth measures 2992.4 GB/s, so the crossover arithmetic intensity is \(744.6 \times 10^{12} / 2992.4 \times 10^9 \approx 249\) FLOPs per byte: any kernel doing less arithmetic per byte moved is bandwidth-bound and leaves the tensor cores idle. Measured on this machine at head dimension 64, sequence length 8192: the naive three-kernel implementation runs at 22.2 TFLOPS and takes 98.9 ms with a 35.0 GB peak footprint (the fp32 score matrix alone is 17.18 GB), while the fused FlashAttention kernel runs the same math at 615.1 TFLOPS in 3.58 ms with 0.57 GB peak, a 27.7x speedup and 61x memory reduction. At 16K context the naive kernel cannot run at all: its 68.7 GB score matrix does not fit in 80 GB, while the fused kernel needs 1.12 GB. The fix is not a different formula, it is refusing to write the score matrix to HBM, and the enabling piece of mathematics is a streaming form of softmax.

The online softmax recurrence

Numerically safe softmax over scores \(x_1, \dots, x_S\) subtracts the maximum before exponentiating: \(p_i = e^{x_i - m} / \ell\) with \(m = \max_i x_i\) and \(\ell = \sum_i e^{x_i - m}\). Computed directly this needs three passes over the data: one for \(m\), one for \(\ell\), one to normalize, and pass structure is exactly what tiling cannot afford. Milakov and Gimelshein (2018) showed the max and the sum can be maintained jointly in one pass. Keep a running maximum \(m^{(j)}\) and a running sum \(\ell^{(j)}\) that is always expressed relative to the current maximum, and on seeing a new score \(x_j\) update

$$ m^{(j)} = \max\big(m^{(j-1)},\, x_j\big), \qquad \ell^{(j)} = \ell^{(j-1)}\, e^{\,m^{(j-1)} - m^{(j)}} + e^{\,x_j - m^{(j)}}. $$

The correction factor \(e^{\,m^{(j-1)} - m^{(j)}}\) is the whole trick: when a new, larger maximum arrives, every term already accumulated was exponentiated against the stale reference, and multiplying the running sum by the ratio of references rescales them all at once. The invariant \(\ell^{(j)} = \sum_{i \le j} e^{\,x_i - m^{(j)}}\) holds by induction: it is true at \(j = 1\), and if it holds at \(j-1\) then \(\ell^{(j-1)} e^{m^{(j-1)} - m^{(j)}} = \sum_{i \le j-1} e^{x_i - m^{(j)}}\), to which the update adds the \(j\)-th term. Since \(m^{(j-1)} \le m^{(j)}\), the correction factor never exceeds 1 and nothing overflows. A quick numeric check with scores \((2, 5, 3)\): after \(x_1 = 2\), \(m = 2\), \(\ell = 1\). After \(x_2 = 5\): \(m = 5\), \(\ell = 1 \cdot e^{2-5} + 1 = 1.0498\). After \(x_3 = 3\): \(m\) stays 5, \(\ell = 1.0498 + e^{-2} = 1.1851\), which equals \(e^{-3} + e^{0} + e^{-2}\) exactly as the invariant demands.

Tiling attention around the recurrence

Dao et al. (2022) extended the same rescaling from scalar sums to the attention output, which is a sum of value vectors weighted by the not-yet-normalized exponentials. Split \(K\) and \(V\) into blocks of rows that fit in on-chip SRAM (a few hundred KB per SM versus tens of GB of HBM). For one query block, hold an output accumulator \(O\), a running max \(m\), and a running sum \(\ell\) in SRAM, and for each KV block \(j\) compute the block of scores \(s = qK_j^{\!\T}/\sqrt{d_h}\) entirely on chip, then update

$$ m' = \max(m, \operatorname{rowmax}(s)), \qquad \tilde{P} = e^{\,s - m'}, \qquad \ell' = \ell\, e^{\,m - m'} + \operatorname{rowsum}(\tilde{P}), $$ $$ O' = O \cdot e^{\,m - m'} + \tilde{P}\, V_j, $$

and after the last block divide once: \(O \leftarrow O / \ell\). The output correction mirrors the sum correction because every row of the accumulated \(O\) is a combination \(\sum_i e^{x_i - m} v_i\) referenced to the stale max, so one scalar multiply per row re-references it. No \(S \times S\) object ever touches HBM; the kernel reads \(Q, K, V\) once per tile pass and writes \(O\) once. Dao et al. prove the HBM traffic is \(\Theta(S^2 d_h^2 / M)\) for SRAM size \(M\), versus \(\Theta(S d_h + S^2)\) for the naive path, and that no exact algorithm can do asymptotically better over a range of SRAM sizes; the proof lives in the paper. For the backward pass the kernel stores only the per-row logsumexp \(m + \log \ell\) (size \(S\), not \(S^2\)) and recomputes score tiles on the fly, spending about 30% more FLOPs to avoid the quadratic write, a trade that wins precisely because the FLOPs were free and the bytes were not. FlashAttention-2 (Dao, 2023) reorganized the loops so each thread block owns a query tile and sweeps KV, moving the non-matmul rescaling work out of the inner loop and roughly doubling throughput; FlashAttention-3 overlapped the softmax with asynchronous tensor-core matmuls on Hopper. The measured 4.26x speedup from fusing a small elementwise chain on this machine (0.911 ms unfused, 0.214 ms fused) is the same lesson in miniature: on modern accelerators, the unit of optimization is memory traffic, and exact mathematical restructuring that eliminates round trips beats approximation almost every time.

Counting parameters and FLOPs

Where the parameters live

For a config with vocabulary \(V\), width \(d\), \(L\) layers, \(n_h\) query heads, \(n_{kv}\) KV heads, head dimension \(d_h = d/n_h\), and SwiGLU width \(d_{ff}\), the exact count is a short table. Attention: \(W_Q \in \R^{d \times d}\), \(W_K, W_V \in \R^{d \times n_{kv} d_h}\), \(W_O \in \R^{d \times d}\), totaling \(2d^2 + 2d\,n_{kv}d_h\) per layer. MLP: three matrices, \(3 d\, d_{ff}\). Norms: \(2d\) per layer plus \(d\) at the end, negligible. Embedding and unembedding: \(Vd\) each if untied. Nothing else. The habit worth building is computing this per component, because the ratios drive design: at Llama-like shapes (\(d_{ff} \approx 2.67d\), GQA at \(n_{kv} = n_h/4\)) the MLP holds roughly 65% of block parameters and attention 25-30%, which is why MoE targets the MLP and why GQA's parameter savings are minor even though its cache savings are decisive.

Deriving the 6ND rule

Every parameter that participates in a matmul touches each token three matrix products' worth of work. Forward: computing \(y = xW\) with \(W \in \R^{a \times b}\) costs \(ab\) multiply-accumulates per token, conventionally \(2ab\) FLOPs. Backward: two matmuls of the same shape, the input gradient \(\partial x = \partial y\, W^{\!\T}\) (another \(2ab\)) and the weight gradient \(\partial W = x^{\!\T} \partial y\) (another \(2ab\)). So training costs \(6\) FLOPs per matmul parameter per token, and for a model with \(N\) such parameters trained on \(D\) tokens,

$$ C \approx 6\,N D \quad \text{FLOPs}, \qquad \text{forward alone} \approx 2ND. $$

Two corrections keep the estimate honest. The embedding lookup is a copy, not a matmul, so the embedding table is excluded from \(N\) (the unembedding projection is a real matmul and stays). And attention's score and value products, \(QK^{\!\T}\) and \(PV\), involve no parameters at all, so 6ND misses them entirely; per token per layer they cost \(2 \cdot 2 \cdot S d_h \cdot n_h = 4Sd\) forward (each of the \(S\) cached positions participates in one MAC per head dimension, for both products), hence \(12\,S d L\) in training. The ratio of the attention term to 6N is roughly \(S / (d \cdot \text{const})\), so at short context the rule is excellent and at long context it degrades: for the 1.27B model worked in Problem 5 below, at 4096 context the score/value FLOPs are already 25% of the total, and a 6ND-based MFU figure would overstate efficiency by the same fraction.

Model FLOPs utilization (MFU), defined in the PaLM report (Chowdhery et al., 2022), is observed useful FLOPs per second divided by the accelerator's peak: tokens/s times FLOPs per token, over the datasheet number (989 TFLOPS dense bf16 for an H100 SXM). It deliberately excludes recomputation, which is why it is the honest cross-system metric: activation checkpointing raises hardware utilization while lowering MFU. Two calibration points from this repository's H100: the best measured bf16 matmul sustains 744.6 TFLOPS, 75% of datasheet peak, so even a model that were pure large matmuls could not reach MFU 1.0; and small matmuls are far worse (108.9 TFLOPS at 1024-square, 11% of peak), which is why wide models with fat matmuls report better MFU than narrow deep ones. Well-engineered dense-model pretraining lands between 35% and 45%; the Llama 3 report states 38-43% across its parallelism configurations.

Mixed precision and loss scaling

The H100 measurements make the case for reduced precision bluntly: at 4096-square, fp32 matmul sustains 51.3 TFLOPS, tf32 384.0, bf16 744.6, a 14.5x gap between naive fp32 and bf16. Nobody can afford to leave that on the table, so training runs matmuls in 16-bit while keeping an fp32 master copy of the weights, fp32 optimizer moments, and fp32 for the numerically delicate reductions (norm statistics, softmax, the final loss). The choice between the two 16-bit formats is a choice about which failure to buy. fp16 spends its 16 bits as 1 sign, 5 exponent, 10 mantissa: fine relative precision (\(2^{-11} \approx 5 \times 10^{-4}\)) but a floor of \(6.1 \times 10^{-5}\) for normal values, and activation gradients late in training routinely fall below it, silently flushing to zero. bf16 spends 8 exponent and 7 mantissa bits: the full fp32 range (\(\sim 10^{38}\)) but only \(2^{-8} \approx 0.4\%\) relative precision.

Loss scaling (Micikevicius et al., 2018) rescues fp16 by exploiting linearity of the gradient: multiply the loss by \(S\), and every gradient in the chain is multiplied by \(S\), shifting the whole gradient distribution up into representable range; divide by \(S\) in fp32 before the optimizer step, and the update is mathematically unchanged wherever nothing over- or underflowed. Dynamic schedules pick \(S\) empirically: double it every few thousand clean steps, and on any overflow (an inf/nan in the gradients) skip the step and halve \(S\). bf16 needs none of this, its exponent range already covers the gradient distribution, and its coarse mantissa turns out to be tolerable because the fp32 master weights integrate small updates across steps and tensor-core matmuls accumulate their inner products in fp32 regardless. This is why essentially all large-scale training moved to bf16 once Ampere-class hardware made it fast, and why the frontier of precision research is now one level down, at fp8 with per-tile scaling, discussed in the frontier section.

Data: the pipeline and deduplication

A trillion-token corpus is manufactured, not found. The standard pipeline, visible end to end in public efforts like RefinedWeb, Dolma, and FineWeb, runs: HTML extraction from web crawls (the extractor matters; boilerplate-heavy extraction measurably hurts downstream models), language identification, heuristic quality filters (document length, symbol-to-word ratios, repetition statistics), often a model-based quality score, deduplication, and finally tokenization into shuffled fixed-length shards. Documents are packed into contiguous training sequences and the notion of an epoch essentially disappears; at frontier scale the corpus is engineered so that little of it repeats even once.

Deduplication earns its own machinery because web crawls are drenched in near-copies and the damage is quantified: Lee et al. (2021) showed a single-epoch model emits memorized text an order of magnitude more often when its training set contains near-duplicates, and that deduplicated training reaches equal or better perplexity in fewer steps. Exact duplicates fall to hashing; the interesting case is near-duplicates, handled by MinHash. Shingle each document into overlapping n-grams, apply a random permutation \(\pi\) to the shingle universe, and record the minimum. The probability two documents agree on that minimum equals their Jaccard similarity: the minimum over the union \(A \cup B\) lands in \(A \cap B\) with probability exactly \(|A \cap B| / |A \cup B| = J(A,B)\), since \(\pi\) makes every element of the union equally likely to be the overall minimum, and the two documents agree precisely when that element belongs to both. Repeating with \(b \cdot r\) independent permutations and banding the signature into \(b\) bands of \(r\) rows, two documents collide in at least one band with probability

$$ P(\text{candidate}) = 1 - \big(1 - J^{\,r}\big)^{b}, $$

an S-curve whose threshold sits near \((1/b)^{1/r}\). A common production setting, \(b = 14\) bands of \(r = 9\) rows (126 hashes), catches documents at \(J = 0.8\) with probability \(1 - (1 - 0.8^9)^{14} = 0.867\) while flagging \(J = 0.5\) pairs only \(1 - (1 - 0.5^9)^{14} = 2.7\%\) of the time: a tunable knob trading recall against the quadratic cost of verifying candidates. The countervailing finding, from the FineWeb ablations, is that dedup can be overdone: aggressive global deduplication preferentially deletes high-quality frequently-copied text, and the best recipes dedup within crawl snapshots rather than across all of them. Data work is unglamorous and decides more benchmark variance than most architecture choices; every serious lab treats the pipeline as a first-class engineering artifact with its own evaluations.

Distributed training

The memory anatomy of a training step

Mixed-precision Adam training carries, per parameter: a 2-byte bf16 working copy, a 2-byte bf16 gradient, and 12 bytes of fp32 optimizer state (4-byte master weight, 4-byte first moment, 4-byte second moment), 16 bytes in all before a single activation is stored. A 7B model therefore needs 112 GB of state, more than an 80 GB H100 holds; even at 7B, training on one device is impossible without sharding something. This single piece of arithmetic explains the entire distributed training stack: the three parallelism families differ in what they shard (state, weights per layer, or layers) and in what they must communicate to compensate.

Data parallelism and the ZeRO ladder

Plain data parallelism replicates everything and splits the batch; after backward, gradients are averaged with an all-reduce. Ring all-reduce on \(n\) workers moves \(2(n-1)/n \approx 2\) times the buffer size per worker, so a 7B model's 14 GB of bf16 gradients cost about 28 GB of traffic per step per GPU, roughly 70 ms at an achievable 400 GB/s of NVLink bandwidth, overlappable with backward compute because gradients for late layers are ready while early layers are still computing. The waste is that all 16 bytes per parameter sit replicated on every worker. Rajbhandari et al. (2020) observed that an all-reduce is algebraically a reduce-scatter followed by an all-gather, and between those two halves each worker holds a complete, exact shard of the summed gradients, which is all it needs to update a shard of the optimizer state. ZeRO's three stages shard progressively more: stage 1 shards the 12 bytes of optimizer state (memory \(4\psi + 12\psi/n\) for \(\psi\) parameters), stage 2 also shards gradients (\(2\psi + 14\psi/n\)), and stage 3 shards the parameters themselves (\(16\psi/n\)), gathering each layer's weights on the fly just before its forward and backward use. Stages 1 and 2 cost no extra communication volume versus plain DP; stage 3 adds an extra all-gather of the parameters per step, making total volume roughly 1.5x plain DP, the price of fitting arbitrarily large models. PyTorch's FSDP is a native implementation of the stage-3 idea with per-module gathering and prefetch.

Problem 3

A 7B-parameter dense model trains with bf16 compute, fp32 Adam state, on a single node of 8 GPUs. Compute the per-GPU memory for model state (ignore activations) under plain data parallelism and under ZeRO stages 1, 2, and 3, and state which configurations fit an 80 GB device with room for activations.

Solution. Total state is \(16 \times 7 \times 10^9 = 112\) GB: 14 GB bf16 parameters, 14 GB bf16 gradients, 84 GB fp32 optimizer state (28 GB master weights, 28 GB each for the two moments). Plain DP replicates all 112 GB per GPU: does not fit. Stage 1 keeps parameters and gradients replicated and shards the 84 GB of optimizer state eight ways: \(14 + 14 + 84/8 = 38.5\) GB per GPU. Stage 2 also shards gradients: \(14 + (14 + 84)/8 = 26.25\) GB. Stage 3 shards everything: \(112/8 = 14\) GB. Stages 1-3 all fit; stage 1 already leaves about 41 GB for activations and is the cheapest in communication and complexity, which is why the practical rule is to take the lowest ZeRO stage that fits. The same arithmetic scaled up says a 70B model (1.12 TB of state) needs at least stage 3 across two 8-GPU nodes, or tensor and pipeline parallelism, before it trains at all.

Tensor parallelism: sharding inside the layer

Megatron-LM (Shoeybi et al., 2019) splits individual weight matrices across \(g\) GPUs in a pattern chosen so that synchronization happens only twice per layer. In the MLP, the up and gate projections are split by columns, so each GPU computes a disjoint slice of the hidden activations and applies the nonlinearity locally, legal because SwiGLU acts elementwise; the down projection is split by rows, so each GPU produces a partial sum of the output, and one all-reduce adds the partials. Attention splits by heads, which are independent until the output projection, again row-split, again one all-reduce. The communication is therefore two all-reduces of the activation tensor \(B \times S \times d\) per layer in forward and two more in backward. The volume is substantial: at \(d = 8192\) with 32,768 tokens per microbatch in bf16, the activation tensor is 512 MiB, a ring all-reduce over 8 GPUs moves \(2 \times 7/8 \times 512 = 896\) MiB per GPU, and with two all-reduces each way a single layer's forward-plus-backward moves about 3.5 GiB per GPU per microbatch. This traffic sits on the critical path between matmuls and cannot be fully hidden, which yields the standard placement rule: tensor parallelism stays inside a node where NVLink provides hundreds of GB/s, and its degree rarely exceeds 8. Sequence parallelism (Korthikanti et al., 2022) extends the same idea to the norms and dropout by splitting along the sequence dimension in the regions between attention and MLP, converting the all-reduces into all-gather plus reduce-scatter pairs of the same total volume but removing redundant activation memory.

Pipeline parallelism and the bubble

Pipeline parallelism assigns contiguous groups of layers to stages and passes activations point to point, so communication is tiny (one boundary tensor per microbatch per stage pair), which is why it is the parallelism of choice across nodes on slower interconnect. Its tax is idleness. With one batch, stage \(p\) waits for everything upstream: utilization is \(1/p\). GPipe (Huang et al., 2019) splits the batch into \(m\) microbatches pushed through in a wave. The wall-clock in microbatch-units is \(m + p - 1\) (the first microbatch takes \(p\) steps to emerge, the rest follow one per step), of which useful work is \(m\), so the idle fraction, the bubble, is

$$ \text{bubble} = \frac{p - 1}{m + p - 1}. $$

Eight stages with 32 microbatches idle \(7/39 = 17.9\%\) of the time; doubling to 64 microbatches drops it to \(7/70 = 10\%\). Bigger \(m\) fights the bubble but raises activation memory under GPipe's schedule, which holds all \(m\) microbatches' activations until backward; the 1F1B schedule (one-forward-one-backward, from PipeDream and refined in Narayanan et al., 2021) interleaves so that at most \(p\) microbatches are live, and interleaved virtual stages shrink the bubble further by a factor of the interleave degree at the cost of proportionally more communication. Production systems compose all three axes: tensor parallel within a node, pipeline across nodes, data parallel (with ZeRO-1) as the outer loop, with the batch size, microbatch count, and parallel degrees solved jointly so that no single term, bubble, TP traffic, or DP all-reduce, dominates the step time.

Scaling laws: Kaplan versus Chinchilla

The empirical form and the correction

Across many orders of magnitude, held-out loss follows a power law in each resource when the others are unconstrained. Kaplan et al. (2020) fit \(L(N) \propto N^{-0.076}\) and \(L(D) \propto D^{-0.095}\) and, allocating a fixed compute budget \(C \approx 6ND\), concluded \(N_{\text{opt}} \propto C^{0.73}\): compute should be spent overwhelmingly on parameters, models should be huge and undertrained. GPT-3 (175B parameters, 300B tokens, 1.7 tokens per parameter) is that prescription made flesh. Hoffmann et al. (2022) reran the experiment with a fixed protocol flaw repaired: Kaplan's runs used a learning-rate decay schedule whose length did not match each run's token budget, which systematically overstates the loss of well-trained small models (a cosine schedule truncated early leaves the model at too high a learning rate). With schedules matched to budgets, three independent analyses (fixed model sizes across budgets, isoFLOP valleys, and a parametric fit) agree that \(N_{\text{opt}} \propto C^{a}\) with \(a \approx 0.5\): parameters and tokens should scale together, roughly 20 tokens per parameter. The 70B Chinchilla model trained on 1.4T tokens beat the 280B Gopher trained on 300B tokens using the same compute, the single most consequential empirical correction in the scaling literature.

Deriving the optimal allocation

The parametric route makes the optimization explicit and is worth doing once by hand. Hoffmann et al. fit the loss surface

$$ L(N, D) = E + \frac{A}{N^{\alpha}} + \frac{B}{D^{\beta}}, \qquad E = 1.69,\; A = 406.4,\; B = 410.7,\; \alpha = 0.34,\; \beta = 0.28, $$

where \(E\) is the irreducible entropy of the data, and the two power terms are the penalties for finite capacity and finite data. Minimize subject to \(6ND = C\): substitute \(D = C/6N\), so \(L(N) = E + A N^{-\alpha} + B (6/C)^{\beta} N^{\beta}\), and set the derivative to zero:

$$ -\alpha A N^{-\alpha - 1} + \beta B (6/C)^{\beta} N^{\beta - 1} = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; N_{\text{opt}} = \left(\frac{\alpha A}{\beta B}\right)^{\frac{1}{\alpha+\beta}} \left(\frac{C}{6}\right)^{\frac{\beta}{\alpha+\beta}}. $$

The capacity penalty falls and the data penalty rises as \(N\) grows at fixed compute; the optimum balances their marginal rates. With the fitted constants the exponent is \(\beta/(\alpha+\beta) = 0.28/0.62 = 0.452\) and the prefactor \((\alpha A / \beta B)^{1/0.62} = 1.345\). Note the exponent is not exactly the 0.5 of the other two analyses; the parametric fit tilts toward more data at large budgets, a discrepancy that is real and documented (Besiroglu et al., 2024, re-derived the fit from the paper's own data and found the approaches reconcile better with corrected fitting). The honest summary: the optimum is a broad, flat valley, 20 tokens per parameter is a serviceable center estimate, and the loss cost of being 2x off in either direction is small, worked out numerically in Problem 6.

One more force now overrides all of this: inference. A Chinchilla-optimal model minimizes training loss per training FLOP, but every serving token thereafter costs \(\approx 2N\) FLOPs and \(2N\) bytes of weight reads, forever. When lifetime inference volume is large, the rational choice is a smaller model trained far past its Chinchilla point: Llama 3 8B was trained on 15T tokens, nearly 1,900 tokens per parameter, roughly two orders of magnitude "overtrained" by the 20:1 rule, because the loss still improves (just at a poor marginal rate per training FLOP) and every point of quality in an 8B serves 10x cheaper than in an 80B. Compute-optimal and product-optimal are different objectives, and the field now budgets against the second.

Mixture of experts: paying for parameters only when used

MoE replaces each MLP with \(N_e\) expert MLPs and a learned router: for each token, a linear gate produces scores, a softmax converts them to probabilities, and the token is dispatched to the top-\(k\) experts, its output the probability-weighted sum of theirs. Parameters scale with \(N_e\), per-token FLOPs with \(k\): DeepSeek-V3 holds 671B total parameters while activating 37B per token, an 18x capacity-to-compute ratio. The catch is that the routing decision is discrete and self-reinforcing: a slightly favored expert receives more tokens, trains faster, and gets favored more, collapsing the network onto a few experts while the rest idle, and simultaneously ruining the load balance that expert-parallel hardware sharding assumes. Shazeer et al. introduced, and the Switch Transformer (Fedus et al., 2021) simplified, an auxiliary loss that penalizes imbalance. With \(f_i\) the fraction of tokens actually routed to expert \(i\) (a hard count, not differentiable) and \(P_i\) the mean router probability for expert \(i\) (soft, differentiable),

$$ \L_{\text{aux}} = \alpha_{\text{aux}} \, N_e \sum_{i=1}^{N_e} f_i \, P_i. $$

Why this form balances: if routing concentrates, both \(f\) and \(P\) concentrate on the same coordinates, and for \(f \approx P\) the loss is \(N_e \sum_i f_i^2\), which by Cauchy-Schwarz satisfies \(\sum_i f_i^2 \ge (\sum_i f_i)^2 / N_e = 1/N_e\) with equality exactly at the uniform distribution, so the minimum of the auxiliary loss is 1, achieved when every expert receives an equal share. The gradient flows through the differentiable factor \(P_i\), pushing router probability away from overloaded experts (those with large \(f_i\)); the hard counts \(f_i\) act as the weights on that pressure. The coefficient \(\alpha_{\text{aux}}\) is a genuine tension: too small and the system collapses, too large and balancing overrides quality. DeepSeek-V3 sidestepped the tension with auxiliary-loss-free balancing: a per-expert bias added to the routing scores before top-\(k\) selection (but not to the mixture weights), nudged up for underloaded experts and down for overloaded ones by a fixed step after each batch, a control loop rather than a loss term, which their ablations show trains better than the auxiliary loss at equal balance. Fine-grained experts (many small experts, several active) and a shared always-on expert, both DeepSeek refinements, are now standard in open MoE recipes including Qwen3 and Mixtral-descended designs.

Inference: the memory-bound endgame

Prefill and decode are different regimes

Serving splits into two phases with opposite characters. Prefill processes the whole prompt in parallel, large matmuls, compute-bound, high MFU. Decode generates one token at a time: every weight matrix is read from HBM to produce a single token's activations, so arithmetic intensity per sequence is about 1 FLOP-pair per byte, hopelessly below the ~249 FLOPs/byte an H100 needs to be compute-bound. Decode speed at batch 1 is therefore a bandwidth division: a 7B model in bf16 is 14 GB of weights, and at this machine's measured 2992.4 GB/s the floor is 4.7 ms per token, at most 214 tokens/s, no matter how fast the tensor cores are. The only lever is batching, amortizing each weight read across \(B\) sequences, and the only obstacle to batching is KV-cache memory, which is why the KV arithmetic of Problem 2, GQA, and the two systems ideas below jointly determine the cost of a served token.

Paged KV cache and continuous batching

Early serving stacks allocated each request a contiguous KV buffer sized for its maximum possible length. Kwon et al. (2023) measured the consequence: with generation lengths unknown in advance, real systems wasted 60-80% of KV memory on reservation and fragmentation. vLLM's PagedAttention is the operating-systems fix transplanted whole: carve KV memory into fixed-size blocks (16 tokens each), give each sequence a block table mapping logical to physical blocks, allocate on demand, and let the attention kernel gather through the indirection. Fragmentation drops to under 4%, the freed memory becomes batch size, and throughput rises 2-4x at equal latency, with copy-on-write block sharing handling beam search and shared prompt prefixes for free. Continuous batching (Yu et al., 2022, the Orca system) fixes the scheduling half: instead of batching whole requests and waiting for the longest to finish, admit and retire sequences at every iteration, so a finishing sequence's slot is refilled immediately and prefill of new requests interleaves with decode of old ones. Every serious serving engine (vLLM, TensorRT-LLM, SGLang) is built on these two ideas.

Speculative decoding, derived to be lossless

Decode is bandwidth-bound, so the accelerator has idle FLOPs; speculative decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023; concurrently Chen et al., 2023 at DeepMind) spends them checking many tokens at once. A cheap draft model proposes \(\gamma\) tokens autoregressively; the target model then scores all of them in one parallel forward pass, exactly the shape of work it is good at. The subtlety is accepting draft tokens without changing the target distribution, and the mechanism is a token-level rejection sampler. At a given position, the draft proposed \(x \sim q\) and the target assigns \(p\). Accept \(x\) with probability \(\min(1, p(x)/q(x))\); on rejection, resample from the residual distribution \(p'(x) = \max(0, p(x) - q(x)) / \sum_z \max(0, p(z) - q(z))\). The output distribution is exactly \(p\): the probability of emitting a particular token \(x\) via acceptance is

$$ q(x)\, \min\!\Big(1, \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\Big) = \min\big(q(x),\, p(x)\big), $$

the total rejection probability is \(1 - \sum_z \min(p(z), q(z))\), which equals the residual normalizer \(\sum_z \max(0, p(z) - q(z))\) because both equal \(\sum_z (p(z) - \min(p(z), q(z)))\), and so the probability of emitting \(x\) via the resampling branch is exactly \(\max(0, p(x) - q(x))\). Summing the two branches gives \(\min(p, q) + (p - \min(p, q)) = p(x)\) for every token: lossless by construction, for any draft model whatsoever. The per-token acceptance probability is \(\alpha = \sum_z \min(p(z), q(z)) = 1 - \mathrm{TV}(p, q)\), one minus the total variation distance, which is the clean statement of the obvious intuition: better-aligned drafts get accepted more.

The throughput math follows from treating acceptances as independent with rate \(\alpha\). A verification round emits the accepted prefix of the \(\gamma\) drafts plus exactly one token from the target itself (the residual sample on the first rejection, or a bonus token if all \(\gamma\) survive). The number of accepted drafts is \(\min(G, \gamma)\) where \(G\) is geometric, so

$$ \E[\text{tokens per round}] = 1 + \sum_{k=1}^{\gamma} \alpha^{k} = \frac{1 - \alpha^{\gamma + 1}}{1 - \alpha}, $$

using \(\E[\min(G,\gamma)] = \sum_{k=1}^{\gamma} P(G \ge k) = \sum_k \alpha^k\) and then summing the finite geometric series. Since a round costs one target forward pass plus \(\gamma\) draft passes, the speedup over plain decoding with relative draft cost \(c\) is that expectation divided by \(1 + \gamma c\), worked numerically in Problem 4. Everything after the original papers is engineering the drafter: Medusa-style extra heads, EAGLE's feature-level drafting, and n-gram lookahead all trade draft quality \(\alpha\) against draft cost \(c\) in this same formula.

Problem 4

A target model is served with a draft model that costs \(c = 0.05\) of a target forward pass and achieves per-token acceptance rate \(\alpha = 0.8\), with draft length \(\gamma = 4\). (a) Compute the expected tokens per verification round. (b) Compute the wall-clock speedup over plain autoregressive decoding, assuming decode is fully serialized and verification of \(\gamma+1\) positions costs the same as one decode step. (c) Would raising \(\gamma\) to 8 help? Support the answer with the arithmetic.

Solution. (a) \(\E[\text{tokens}] = (1 - 0.8^5)/(1 - 0.8) = (1 - 0.32768)/0.2 = 0.67232/0.2 = 3.362\) tokens per round (a 200,000-trial simulation on this machine gives 3.358, confirming the formula).

(b) Each round costs \(1 + \gamma c = 1 + 4 \times 0.05 = 1.2\) target-step equivalents and yields 3.362 tokens, versus 1 token per 1.0 steps for plain decoding: speedup \(= 3.362 / 1.2 = 2.80\)x.

(c) At \(\gamma = 8\): \(\E = (1 - 0.8^9)/0.2 = (1 - 0.13422)/0.2 = 4.329\) tokens, cost \(1 + 8 \times 0.05 = 1.4\), speedup \(4.329/1.4 = 3.09\)x. The gain from four extra drafts is only 0.29x because the marginal draft survives with probability \(\alpha^k\), which has fallen to \(0.8^5 \approx 0.33\) by position five, while its cost is constant. The expected-tokens curve saturates at \(1/(1-\alpha) = 5\) as \(\gamma \to \infty\) while cost grows linearly, so an interior optimum exists; scanning \(\gamma = 4, 5, 6, 7, 8\) gives speedups 2.80, 2.95, 3.04, 3.08, 3.09: nearly flat past \(\gamma \approx 6\), and in a real system the verification pass also grows with \(\gamma\), pushing the optimum lower. Draft length is a tuning parameter with steeply diminishing returns, and \(\alpha\), not \(\gamma\), is where the leverage lives.

Problem 5

A model has vocabulary 32,768 (untied embeddings), width \(d = 2048\), 24 layers, 16 query heads and 8 KV heads of dimension 128, SwiGLU width 5632, and trains at sequence length 4096. (a) Compute the exact parameter count. (b) Compute training FLOPs per token, including the attention score/value terms. (c) The run sustains 45,000 tokens/s on one H100 (datasheet peak 989 TFLOPS dense bf16). Compute MFU, and compare against the 744.6 TFLOPS this repository actually measures for large bf16 matmuls on the same device.

Solution. (a) Embedding and unembedding: \(2 \times 32{,}768 \times 2048 = 134{,}217{,}728\). Per layer, attention: \(W_Q\) and \(W_O\) are \(2048 \times 2048 = 4{,}194{,}304\) each; \(W_K\) and \(W_V\) map 2048 to \(8 \times 128 = 1024\), so \(2{,}097{,}152\) each; subtotal 12,582,912. MLP: \(3 \times 2048 \times 5632 = 34{,}603{,}008\). Two norms add 4096. Layer total 47,190,016; times 24 layers is 1,132,560,384; plus embeddings and the final norm (2048): 1,266,780,160 parameters, a 1.27B model. Note the MLP is 73% of each block.

(b) Matmul-participating parameters exclude the embedding lookup: \(N' = 1{,}266{,}780{,}160 - 67{,}108{,}864 = 1{,}199{,}671{,}296\). The 6ND term: \(6N' = 7.198 \times 10^9\) FLOPs/token. Attention score/value terms: \(12\,S d L = 12 \times 4096 \times 2048 \times 24 = 2.416 \times 10^9\). Total \(9.614 \times 10^9\) FLOPs per token; the parameter-free attention arithmetic is 25% of the budget at this context length, so ignoring it here would be a real error, not a rounding one.

Quoting MFU against the datasheet while knowing the realizable ceiling is 75% of it is exactly the kind of context that separates a real capacity plan from a slide.

Problem 6

A lab has \(C = 10^{23}\) FLOPs for a pretraining run. (a) Allocate it with the 20-tokens-per-parameter rule. (c) Estimate wall-clock on 1024 H100s at 40% MFU. (d) GPT-3 spent \(3.15 \times 10^{23}\) FLOPs as \(N = 175\)B, \(D = 300\)B. Using the parametric loss, compare it to the compute-optimal model at its own budget.

Solution. (a) With \(D = 20N\) and \(C = 6ND = 120N^2\): \(N = \sqrt{10^{23}/120} = 2.89 \times 10^{10}\), so a 29B model on \(D = 577\)B tokens.

(b) The derived optimum is \(N_{\text{opt}} = 1.345\,(C/6)^{0.452}\). With \(C/6 = 1.667 \times 10^{22}\): \(N_{\text{opt}} = 1.46 \times 10^{10}\), \(D_{\text{opt}} = C/6N = 1.14 \times 10^{12}\): a 14.6B model on 1.14T tokens, 78 tokens per parameter. Predicted losses: \(L(29\text{B}, 577\text{B}) = 1.69 + 406.4/(2.89 \times 10^{10})^{0.34} + 410.7/(5.77\times 10^{11})^{0.28} = 2.012\), versus \(L(14.6\text{B}, 1.14\text{T}) = 2.005\). The two allocations differ 2x in model size and by 0.007 nats: the valley really is that flat, which is why the crude rule survives, and why secondary considerations (inference cost, data availability) legitimately dominate the choice within the valley.

(c) \(10^{23} / (0.40 \times 989 \times 10^{12}) = 2.53 \times 10^{8}\) GPU-seconds \(= 2{,}925\) GPU-days, which is 2.9 days on 1024 GPUs, before restarts, failures, and evaluation pauses.

(d) GPT-3's predicted loss: \(1.69 + 406.4/(1.75\times 10^{11})^{0.34} + 410.7/(3\times 10^{11})^{0.28} = 2.002\). The compute-optimal allocation of the same \(3.15 \times 10^{23}\) budget is \(N = 2.45 \times 10^{10}\), \(D = 2.14 \times 10^{12}\) (24.5B on 2.1T tokens), with predicted loss 1.954. Under this loss surface, a model one-seventh the size, correctly fed, beats it by 0.05 nats, comparable to the 0.057-nat Gopher-to-Chinchilla gap on the same surface, while being 7x cheaper per inference token. This is the arithmetic that reorganized the industry's budgets after 2022.

Implementation

The blocks below are the derivations made executable, and each was run and checked on the H100 in this repository before being included. The first implements the full modern decoder block: RoPE as two fused multiplies against precomputed tables (with a check that shifting all positions by a constant leaves attention scores bit-for-bit unchanged, the relative-position property derived above), RMSNorm with its fp32 internal reduction, GQA with explicit KV-head broadcasting, and the SwiGLU MLP. Shapes are annotated at every step. The PyTorch version delegates the attention contraction to scaled_dot_product_attention so it dispatches to a fused FlashAttention kernel; the JAX version writes the masked softmax explicitly so the tiling target is visible (in production JAX code that line becomes a call to a fused kernel such as the Pallas/cuDNN attention). 

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

def rope_tables(head_dim: int, max_seq: int, base: float = 10000.0):
    # theta_i = base^(-2i/d_h) for pair i = 0 .. d_h/2 - 1
    inv_freq = base ** (-torch.arange(0, head_dim, 2).float() / head_dim)
    pos = torch.arange(max_seq).float()                # (S,)
    ang = torch.outer(pos, inv_freq)                   # (S, hd/2)
    return torch.cos(ang), torch.sin(ang)

def apply_rope(x, cos, sin):
    # x: (B, H, S, hd); rotate each (even, odd) pair by the position angle
    x_even, x_odd = x[..., 0::2], x[..., 1::2]         # (B, H, S, hd/2)
    c, s = cos[None, None], sin[None, None]            # (1, 1, S, hd/2)
    out = torch.empty_like(x)
    out[..., 0::2] = x_even * c - x_odd * s
    out[..., 1::2] = x_even * s + x_odd * c
    return out

class RMSNorm(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, dim: int, eps: float = 1e-6):
        super().__init__()
        self.weight = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(dim))
        self.eps = eps
    def forward(self, x):                              # (B, S, d)
        # sum of squares in fp32 even under bf16 autocast: long reductions
        # are exactly where 8 mantissa bits lose accuracy
        var = x.float().pow(2).mean(dim=-1, keepdim=True)
        x = (x.float() * torch.rsqrt(var + self.eps)).type_as(x)
        return x * self.weight

class GQABlock(nn.Module):
    """Pre-norm decoder block: RMSNorm - GQA(RoPE) - RMSNorm - SwiGLU."""
    def __init__(self, d: int, n_heads: int, n_kv: int, d_ff: int):
        super().__init__()
        assert n_heads % n_kv == 0
        self.nh, self.nkv, self.hd = n_heads, n_kv, d // n_heads
        self.wq = nn.Linear(d, n_heads * self.hd, bias=False)
        self.wk = nn.Linear(d, n_kv    * self.hd, bias=False)  # small!
        self.wv = nn.Linear(d, n_kv    * self.hd, bias=False)
        self.wo = nn.Linear(n_heads * self.hd, d, bias=False)
        self.w_gate = nn.Linear(d, d_ff, bias=False)   # SwiGLU: 3 matrices,
        self.w_up   = nn.Linear(d, d_ff, bias=False)   # d_ff ~ (8/3) d
        self.w_down = nn.Linear(d_ff, d, bias=False)
        self.norm1, self.norm2 = RMSNorm(d), RMSNorm(d)

    def forward(self, x, cos, sin):                    # x: (B, S, d)
        B, S, _ = x.shape
        h = self.norm1(x)
        q = self.wq(h).view(B, S, self.nh,  self.hd).transpose(1, 2)
        k = self.wk(h).view(B, S, self.nkv, self.hd).transpose(1, 2)
        v = self.wv(h).view(B, S, self.nkv, self.hd).transpose(1, 2)
        q = apply_rope(q, cos[:S], sin[:S])            # (B, nh,  S, hd)
        k = apply_rope(k, cos[:S], sin[:S])            # values NOT rotated
        k = k.repeat_interleave(self.nh // self.nkv, dim=1)   # share KV
        v = v.repeat_interleave(self.nh // self.nkv, dim=1)   # across group
        o = F.scaled_dot_product_attention(q, k, v, is_causal=True)  # fused
        o = o.transpose(1, 2).reshape(B, S, self.nh * self.hd)
        x = x + self.wo(o)                             # residual 1
        h = self.norm2(x)
        x = x + self.w_down(F.silu(self.w_gate(h)) * self.w_up(h))
        return x                                       # residual 2

import jax
import jax.numpy as jnp

def rope_tables(head_dim, max_seq, base=10000.0):
    inv_freq = base ** (-jnp.arange(0, head_dim, 2) / head_dim)  # (hd/2,)
    ang = jnp.arange(max_seq)[:, None] * inv_freq[None, :]       # (S, hd/2)
    return jnp.cos(ang), jnp.sin(ang)

def apply_rope(x, cos, sin):
    # x: (B, H, S, hd); rotate each (even, odd) pair by the position angle
    x_even, x_odd = x[..., 0::2], x[..., 1::2]         # (B, H, S, hd/2)
    c, s = cos[None, None], sin[None, None]
    rot_even = x_even * c - x_odd * s
    rot_odd  = x_even * s + x_odd * c
    # re-interleave to (..., hd): [e0, o0, e1, o1, ...]
    return jnp.stack([rot_even, rot_odd], axis=-1).reshape(x.shape)

def rms_norm(x, weight, eps=1e-6):
    var = jnp.mean(jnp.square(x.astype(jnp.float32)), axis=-1, keepdims=True)
    return (x * jax.lax.rsqrt(var + eps)).astype(x.dtype) * weight

def gqa_block(params, x, cos, sin, n_heads, n_kv):
    B, S, d = x.shape
    hd = d // n_heads
    h = rms_norm(x, params["norm1"])
    q = (h @ params["wq"]).reshape(B, S, n_heads, hd).transpose(0, 2, 1, 3)
    k = (h @ params["wk"]).reshape(B, S, n_kv,    hd).transpose(0, 2, 1, 3)
    v = (h @ params["wv"]).reshape(B, S, n_kv,    hd).transpose(0, 2, 1, 3)
    q = apply_rope(q, cos[:S], sin[:S])                # (B, nh,  S, hd)
    k = apply_rope(k, cos[:S], sin[:S])                # values NOT rotated
    k = jnp.repeat(k, n_heads // n_kv, axis=1)         # broadcast KV heads
    v = jnp.repeat(v, n_heads // n_kv, axis=1)
    # explicit masked softmax; production code calls a fused kernel here
    scores = q @ k.transpose(0, 1, 3, 2) / jnp.sqrt(hd)   # (B, nh, S, S)
    mask = jnp.tril(jnp.ones((S, S), dtype=bool))
    scores = jnp.where(mask, scores, -jnp.inf)         # -inf, not -1e9
    o = jax.nn.softmax(scores, axis=-1) @ v            # (B, nh, S, hd)
    o = o.transpose(0, 2, 1, 3).reshape(B, S, n_heads * hd)
    x = x + o @ params["wo"]                           # residual 1
    h = rms_norm(x, params["norm2"])
    ff = (jax.nn.silu(h @ params["w_gate"]) * (h @ params["w_up"]))
    return x + ff @ params["w_down"]                   # residual 2

The training step is where the mixed-precision policy lives. In PyTorch, autocast runs matmuls in bf16 while master weights, gradients, and the optimizer remain fp32; the cross-entropy is computed on fp32 logits; gradients are clipped by global norm (the standard defense against loss spikes) with AdamW betas (0.9, 0.95) and a warmup schedule, the settings that have been near-universal since GPT-3. The JAX version expresses the same policy functionally: parameters stay fp32, a bf16-cast copy feeds the forward pass, and optax composes clipping, warmup cosine decay, and decoupled weight decay into one gradient transformation. Neither uses a loss scaler because neither uses fp16; a comment marks where one would go. 

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

# model: any nn.Module mapping (B, S) int64 tokens -> (B, S, V) logits.
# Parameters and optimizer state stay fp32; autocast runs matmuls in bf16.
opt = torch.optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=3e-4,
                        betas=(0.9, 0.95), weight_decay=0.1)
warmup = 2000
sched = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.LambdaLR(
    opt, lambda step: min(1.0, (step + 1) / warmup))   # then cosine, elided

def train_step(tokens):
    # tokens: (B, S+1); inputs tokens[:, :-1], targets tokens[:, 1:]
    inputs, targets = tokens[:, :-1], tokens[:, 1:]
    with torch.autocast("cuda", dtype=torch.bfloat16):
        logits = model(inputs)                         # (B, S, V) in bf16
        loss = F.cross_entropy(                        # loss math in fp32
            logits.reshape(-1, logits.size(-1)).float(),
            targets.reshape(-1))
    # bf16 needs no GradScaler; with fp16 this would be
    # scaler.scale(loss).backward(); scaler.unscale_(opt); ...
    loss.backward()                                    # grads land in fp32
    grad_norm = torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), 1.0)
    opt.step()
    opt.zero_grad(set_to_none=True)
    sched.step()
    return loss.detach(), grad_norm                    # log both every step

# model_apply: params, (B, S) int32 tokens -> (B, S, V) logits (pure fn)

def loss_fn(params, tokens):
    inputs, targets = tokens[:, :-1], tokens[:, 1:]
    # mixed precision, functionally: params stay fp32 (the master copy);
    # a bf16-cast view feeds the forward pass, loss math runs in fp32
    p16 = jax.tree.map(lambda w: w.astype(jnp.bfloat16), params)
    logits = model_apply(p16, inputs).astype(jnp.float32)  # (B, S, V)
    logp = jax.nn.log_softmax(logits, axis=-1)
    nll = -jnp.take_along_axis(logp, targets[..., None], axis=-1)
    return nll.mean()                                  # no loss scale: bf16

        b1=0.9, b2=0.95, weight_decay=0.1),
)

@jax.jit
def train_step(params, opt_state, tokens):

    updates, opt_state = tx.update(grads, opt_state, params)
    params = optax.apply_updates(params, updates)      # fp32 master update
    return params, opt_state, loss

Finally, the kernel view. RMSNorm is the simplest realistic example of the fusion argument made throughout this page: as three separate operations (square-and-reduce, rsqrt-scale, gain multiply) it would round-trip the activation tensor through HBM three times; as one Triton kernel each row is loaded once into registers, reduced, scaled, and stored once. One program instance handles one row, the row must fit in a power-of-two block, and the reduction is done in fp32 regardless of the tensor dtype, mirroring the precision note in the RMSNorm section. Verified against the PyTorch reference to within bf16 rounding on this machine. 

import torch
import triton
import triton.language as tl

@triton.jit
def rmsnorm_fwd(X, W, Y, stride, D, eps, BLOCK: tl.constexpr):
    """One program per row: load once, reduce in fp32, store once.
    Unfused, this is 3 HBM round trips; fused, it is 1 read + 1 write."""
    row = tl.program_id(0)
    cols = tl.arange(0, BLOCK)                 # BLOCK = next pow2 >= D
    mask = cols < D
    x = tl.load(X + row * stride + cols, mask=mask, other=0.0).to(tl.float32)
    ms = tl.sum(x * x, axis=0) / D             # mean of squares, fp32
    rstd = 1.0 / tl.sqrt(ms + eps)
    w = tl.load(W + cols, mask=mask, other=0.0).to(tl.float32)
    y = x * rstd * w
    tl.store(Y + row * stride + cols, y.to(Y.dtype.element_ty), mask=mask)

def rmsnorm(x: torch.Tensor, weight: torch.Tensor, eps: float = 1e-6):
    shape = x.shape
    x2 = x.reshape(-1, shape[-1]).contiguous() # (rows, D)
    M, D = x2.shape
    y = torch.empty_like(x2)
    BLOCK = triton.next_power_of_2(D)          # row must fit one block
    rmsnorm_fwd[(M,)](x2, weight, y, x2.stride(0), D, eps, BLOCK=BLOCK)
    return y.reshape(shape)

How it is done in practice

The distance between these derivations and a production run is mostly operational. A real pretraining job is a long-running distributed system with a failure model: at thousands of GPUs, hardware faults arrive daily (the Llama 3 report counts 466 interruptions across its 54-day 405B run, 78% from hardware), so asynchronous distributed checkpointing, fast job restart, and straggler detection are not conveniences but throughput terms in expectation. Loss spikes are handled with a playbook rather than a theory: global-norm clipping absorbs small ones; large ones are handled by rewinding to a checkpoint and skipping the offending data window; persistent instability argues for lower learning rate or tighter numerics (fp32 attention logits, z-loss on the softmax normalizer). Monitoring centers on the gradient norm, the loss, and per-layer activation statistics, because divergence announces itself in the gradient norm before the loss moves.

The hyperparameters of record have converged hard: AdamW with betas (0.9, 0.95), weight decay 0.1, gradient clip 1.0, a few thousand steps of linear warmup into cosine decay to about 10% of peak learning rate, batch sizes in the millions of tokens, often ramped during early training. Newer runs increasingly use warmup-stable-decay schedules so that training length need not be fixed in advance, plus a final anneal on a high-quality data mix, both visible in open recipes like DeepSeek's and MiniCPM's reports. Every scaling decision is de-risked at small scale first: data mixtures, architecture tweaks, and hyperparameters are screened on models 100-1000x smaller, using scaling-law fits (and increasingly muP-style width-invariant parameterizations) to extrapolate, because a single full-scale experiment is unrepeatable by construction. And the serving fleet is engineered against the arithmetic of the inference section: GQA or MLA to shrink the cache, paged allocation to stop wasting it, continuous batching to keep it full, speculative decoding to spend idle FLOPs, and quantization (weights first, KV cache second) to move the bandwidth floor itself. The measured numbers on this page's H100, 27.7x from attention fusion at 8K context, 14.5x from bf16 over fp32 matmuls, 4.26x from fusing one small elementwise chain, are the reason systems engineers, not just modelers, set the pace of this field.

The current research frontier

The clearest active front is precision. DeepSeek-V3 demonstrated fp8 matmuls for most of a 671B-parameter pretraining run, with per-tile (128x128) scaling factors and periodic promotion of accumulations to fp32 to contain the error of Hopper's fp8 tensor-core accumulate; NVIDIA's Transformer Engine productizes the same per-tensor and per-block scaling machinery, and Blackwell-generation microscaling formats (MXFP8, NVFP4) push block scaling into hardware. On attention, the KV cache remains the target: DeepSeek's multi-head latent attention (MLA) compresses keys and values into a shared low-rank latent that is cached in place of K and V, cutting cache size well below GQA at equal quality, while trainable sparse attention (DeepSeek's NSA, Moonshot's MoBA, both 2025) and hybrid state-space designs (Mamba-2-based hybrids such as Jamba from AI21, and NVIDIA's Nemotron-H) attack the quadratic term directly. Context extension consolidated around RoPE remapping: position interpolation (Chen et al., Meta, 2023), NTK-aware scaling from the open-source community, and YaRN (Peng et al., 2023) are the standard ladder, with ring/context parallelism handling the training-time memory. On the objective side, multi-token prediction (Gloeckle et al., Meta, 2024) adds auxiliary heads predicting several future tokens, improving sample efficiency and doubling as a built-in speculative drafter, adopted in DeepSeek-V3. Scaling-law research moved past Chinchilla to inference-aware and distillation-aware allocations, data- constrained regimes (Muennighoff et al., 2023, on repeating data), and learning-rate-schedule-aware fits; and the largest budget shift of all is toward test-time compute, where reasoning-trained models spend orders of magnitude more decode FLOPs per query, which in turn re-prices every inference optimization on this page. These lines are advancing from many directions at once: DeepSeek, Moonshot, and Qwen on architecture and fp8; Meta, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, and OpenAI on scaling and post-training; NVIDIA on formats and kernels; with academic groups (Tri Dao's lab at Princeton on state-space models and kernels, Berkeley's Sky lab on serving) supplying much of the systems substrate.

Common misconceptions

"FlashAttention approximates attention to go faster." It computes bit-for-bit exact attention (up to floating-point reassociation); the speedup comes from eliminating HBM round trips, not arithmetic. The online-softmax invariant proved above is an equality, and the measured 27.7x at 8K context on this machine involved no approximation. Sparse and linear attention are approximations; FlashAttention is a schedule.

"bf16 training needs loss scaling." Loss scaling exists to lift fp16's gradient distribution above its \(6 \times 10^{-5}\) normal floor. bf16 shares fp32's exponent range, so underflow of that kind is not the failure mode; its cost is mantissa precision, which is paid for with fp32 master weights and fp32 accumulation instead. Carrying a GradScaler into a bf16 run is cargo cult.

"6ND is the FLOPs cost of training a transformer." It is the matmul-parameter term only. The parameter-free attention products add \(12SdL\) per token, 25% of the total for the 1.27B/4K-context model worked above and growing linearly with context; and the embedding table contributes parameters but no matmul FLOPs. MFU claims that ignore both can be off by a third in either direction.

"Chinchilla proved 20 tokens per parameter is optimal." It estimated a compute-optimal ratio for training loss under one data distribution, with the paper's own three analyses disagreeing about the exponent, and the optimum it found is a shallow valley (0.007 nats across a 2x misallocation in the worked problem above). Inference-aware budgets rationally overtrain by one to two orders of magnitude, as Llama 3 8B's 1,900 tokens per parameter shows. The paper's durable lesson is the direction of the correction and the method, not the constant.

"An MoE with 8x parameters costs 8x to run." Per-token compute scales with the active experts \(k\), not the expert count: DeepSeek-V3 activates 37B of 671B parameters. The costs that do scale with total parameters are memory, checkpoint size, and expert-parallel communication, which is why MoE is a memory-for-FLOPs trade, profitable exactly when serving batch sizes are large enough to keep every expert's weights busy.

"Speculative decoding trades quality for speed." The rejection-sampling construction derived above emits tokens from exactly the target distribution for any draft model; a bad draft costs speed (low \(\alpha\)), never correctness. Methods that do change the distribution (accepting by top-k membership, lenient thresholds) exist, but they are a different, lossy contract and should be benchmarked as such.

"Decode throughput is limited by compute, so a faster GPU generates proportionally faster." Batch-1 decode is a weight-streaming problem: 14 GB of bf16 weights over 2992.4 GB/s of measured bandwidth bounds a 7B model at 214 tokens/s on this H100 regardless of tensor-core throughput. Only batching, cache shrinking (GQA/MLA), quantization, or speculative parallelism move that number materially; more TFLOPS alone do not.

"The KV cache grows with the number of query heads." It grows with KV heads only: \(2 L\, n_{kv} d_h\, p\) bytes per token. That asymmetry is the entire design space between MHA, GQA, and MQA, and it is why GQA cuts cache 4-8x while leaving query-side capacity, and parameter count, nearly untouched.

A modern language model is one long chain of counted quantities, and fluency in this subject means being able to walk the chain without hand-waving at any link. Bytes become tokens through a greedy merge rule whose entire state is an ordered list of pair counts. Tokens flow through a block whose every deviation from the 2017 design, RMSNorm, RoPE, SwiGLU, GQA, exists for a derivable reason: one fewer reduction, an exactly relative position code, a data-dependent gate at fixed parameters, a KV cache cut by the ratio of head counts. Training cost is 6ND plus an attention term someone in the room should always remember, memory is 16 bytes per parameter until ZeRO divides it, parallelism is three sharding choices with three communication bills, and the budget split between N and D is a one-line Lagrangian whose valley is flat enough that inference economics decide. Serving is a bandwidth division softened by paging, batching, and a rejection sampler that gets free tokens without changing the distribution. None of these pieces is individually deep; the craft is that they compose, and that every one of them can, and should, be checked with arithmetic before a single GPU is rented.
