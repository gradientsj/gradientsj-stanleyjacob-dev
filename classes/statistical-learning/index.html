Statistical learning: from least squares to the exponential family

Supervised learning has a small number of load-bearing derivations, and almost everything a practitioner does is one of them wearing different clothes. This page derives them end to end: least squares three ways (calculus, projection geometry, Gaussian maximum likelihood), logistic regression with the sigmoid forced by the exponential family and Newton's method worked by hand, generalized linear models with the one gradient formula they all share, generative classifiers (Gaussian discriminant analysis and Naive Bayes) against their discriminative counterparts, kernels with the representer theorem proved and the SVM dual derived from KKT conditions, the exact bias-variance decomposition, ridge regression as a Gaussian prior, and EM for Gaussian mixtures built from Jensen's inequality with a full iteration computed numerically. Every number in every worked problem was verified by running the computation, and every algorithm is implemented in PyTorch and JAX side by side.

Why this subject matters now

It is tempting to treat classical statistical learning as a historical layer beneath deep learning, something to be skimmed on the way to transformers. The opposite is closer to the truth. The loss functions that train modern networks are exponential-family negative log-likelihoods: softmax cross-entropy is the categorical GLM objective, mean squared error is the Gaussian one, and the reason both have the gradient "prediction minus target" is a single theorem about canonical links derived on this page. The optimizer folklore of deep learning (why second-order methods converge in a handful of steps when they fit in memory, why L2 regularization is a prior, why the observed Fisher information is the Hessian) is GLM theory. Kernel methods, which looked superseded a decade ago, returned to the center of the field when Jacot, Gabriel, and Hongler at EPFL showed that infinitely wide networks train as kernel regression under the neural tangent kernel, and the sharpest available analyses of why overparameterized models generalize (benign overfitting at Berkeley, double descent from Belkin and collaborators) are statements about least squares and ridge regression in high dimension. Meanwhile the deployed world still runs on the classical stack directly: insurance pricing and epidemiology on Poisson and gamma GLMs, ad-click prediction on regularized logistic regression at billions of rows, forecasting pipelines on cross-validated ridge and boosted stumps. What has changed in the last five years is not that this material became obsolete, but that the bar moved: knowing the formulas is table stakes, and what distinguishes a strong practitioner is being able to derive them, because the derivations are what transfer when the model in front of them is not in a textbook.

Least squares

The problem and the calculus derivation

The training set is \(n\) pairs \((x_i, y_i)\) with \(x_i \in \R^d\) (a leading 1 is folded into \(x_i\) so the intercept is a coordinate of \(\theta\)) and \(y_i \in \R\). The hypothesis is linear, \(h_\theta(x) = \theta\T x\), and the fitting criterion is the sum of squared residuals,

$$ J(\theta) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \big(\theta\T x_i - y_i\big)^2 \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\lVert X\theta - y \rVert^2, $$

where \(X \in \R^{n \times d}\) stacks the inputs as rows and \(y \in \R^n\) stacks the targets. To differentiate, expand the quadratic:

$$ J(\theta) = \tfrac{1}{2}\big(\theta\T X\T X \theta - 2\, y\T X \theta + y\T y\big). $$

Two matrix-calculus facts do the work, and both reduce to coordinates. For the linear term, \(\partial (b\T \theta)/\partial \theta_k = b_k\), so \(\nabla_\theta\, b\T\theta = b\); here \(b = X\T y\). For the quadratic term with symmetric \(A = X\T X\), write \(\theta\T A \theta = \sum_{j,k} A_{jk}\theta_j \theta_k\); differentiating with respect to \(\theta_m\) picks up \(A_{mk}\theta_k\) from the \(j{=}m\) terms and \(A_{jm}\theta_j\) from the \(k{=}m\) terms, giving \(2\sum_k A_{mk}\theta_k\) by symmetry, i.e. \(\nabla_\theta\, \theta\T A\theta = 2A\theta\). Therefore

$$ \nabla_\theta J \;=\; X\T X\,\theta - X\T y, $$

and setting the gradient to zero gives the normal equations

$$ X\T X\, \theta \;=\; X\T y, \qquad \theta^\ast = (X\T X)^{-1} X\T y \;\text{ when } X\T X \text{ is invertible.} $$

The Hessian is \(X\T X\), which is positive semidefinite (\(v\T X\T X v = \lVert Xv\rVert^2 \ge 0\)), so \(J\) is convex and the stationary point is a global minimum. When \(X\) has linearly dependent columns, \(X\T X\) is singular, the minimizer is a whole affine set, and the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse \(\theta = X^{+} y\) selects the minimum-norm member; that choice is what lstsq routines return and it matters again in the discussion of interpolation at the end of this page.

The projection view

The same equations fall out of geometry with no calculus at all. The set of achievable prediction vectors \(\{X\theta : \theta \in \R^d\}\) is the column space of \(X\), a \(d\)-dimensional subspace of \(\R^n\) (when columns are independent). Minimizing \(\lVert X\theta - y\rVert\) means finding the point of that subspace closest to \(y\), which is the orthogonal projection of \(y\) onto it. The defining property of the projection is that the residual \(r = y - X\theta\) is orthogonal to the subspace, hence orthogonal to every column of \(X\):

$$ X\T (y - X\theta) = 0 \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; X\T X \theta = X\T y. $$

Same normal equations, now with content: least squares does not make residuals small in every direction, it makes them exactly orthogonal to everything the model can express. The fitted values are \(\hat y = X(X\T X)^{-1}X\T y = Hy\), where \(H\) is the hat matrix. \(H\) is symmetric, and idempotent, since \(H^2 = X(X\T X)^{-1}X\T X(X\T X)^{-1}X\T = X(X\T X)^{-1}X\T = H\); symmetric idempotent matrices are exactly orthogonal projections. Its diagonal entries \(h_{ii} = x_i\T(X\T X)^{-1}x_i\), the leverages, measure how much observation \(i\) pulls its own fit, satisfy \(0 \le h_{ii} \le 1\), and sum to \(\tr(H) = d\). The leverages return with a starring role in the cross-validation section.

Problem 1

Fit a line \(y = \theta_0 + \theta_1 x\) by least squares to the four points \((0,1), (1,2), (2,2), (3,4)\). Form the normal equations explicitly, solve them by hand, and verify that the residual vector is orthogonal to both columns of the design matrix.

Solution. The design matrix has rows \((1, x_i)\), so

$$ X\T X = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 6 \\ 6 & 14 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad X\T y = \begin{pmatrix} 1+2+2+4 \\ 0+2+4+12 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 9 \\ 18 \end{pmatrix}. $$

The determinant is \(4 \cdot 14 - 6 \cdot 6 = 20\), so

$$ (X\T X)^{-1} = \frac{1}{20}\begin{pmatrix} 14 & -6 \\ -6 & 4 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \theta = \frac{1}{20}\begin{pmatrix} 14 \cdot 9 - 6 \cdot 18 \\ -6 \cdot 9 + 4 \cdot 18 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{20}\begin{pmatrix} 18 \\ 18 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.9 \\ 0.9 \end{pmatrix}. $$

The fitted line is \(\hat y = 0.9 + 0.9x\), with fitted values \((0.9, 1.8, 2.7, 3.6)\) and residuals \(r = (0.1, 0.2, -0.7, 0.4)\). Orthogonality: against the intercept column, \(0.1 + 0.2 - 0.7 + 0.4 = 0\); against the \(x\) column, \(0(0.1) + 1(0.2) + 2(-0.7) + 3(0.4) = 0.2 - 1.4 + 1.2 = 0\). Both inner products vanish, so \(r\) is orthogonal to the column space, confirming the projection characterization. The residual sum of squares is \(0.01 + 0.04 + 0.49 + 0.16 = 0.70\). This little dataset is reused for the leverage and cross-validation computation in Problem 8.

The probabilistic view: Gaussian noise makes least squares maximum likelihood

So far squared error was a choice. A generative assumption turns it into a consequence. Suppose the targets are generated as

$$ y_i = \theta\T x_i + \varepsilon_i, \qquad \varepsilon_i \sim \N(0, \sigma^2) \text{ independent}, $$

so \(p(y_i \mid x_i; \theta) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma} \exp\!\big({-\frac{(y_i - \theta\T x_i)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\big)\). Independence makes the likelihood of the dataset a product, and the log turns it into a sum:

$$ \ell(\theta) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log p(y_i \mid x_i; \theta) \;=\; -\,n \log\!\big(\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma\big) \;-\; \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \big(y_i - \theta\T x_i\big)^2. $$

The first term does not involve \(\theta\), and the second is \(-J(\theta)/\sigma^2\). Maximizing \(\ell\) over \(\theta\) is therefore exactly minimizing the sum of squared residuals, for any \(\sigma\): under independent Gaussian noise of constant variance, the maximum-likelihood estimator is the least-squares estimator. The assumptions are doing real work in that sentence. Heavier-tailed noise (Laplace) would make the MLE minimize absolute error; input-dependent variance would make it weighted least squares. Maximizing over \(\sigma^2\) as well, set \(\partial \ell / \partial \sigma^2 = -n/(2\sigma^2) + \mathrm{RSS}/(2\sigma^4) = 0\) to get \(\hat\sigma^2 = \mathrm{RSS}/n\), the average squared residual (biased low by the factor \((n-d)/n\) relative to the unbiased estimator, because the same data chose \(\theta\)).

Locally weighted regression

A straight line through all the data is a strong global commitment. Locally weighted linear regression keeps the linear machinery but makes the commitment local: to predict at a query point \(x\), solve a weighted least-squares problem that cares most about training points near \(x\),

$$ \min_\theta \; \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i(x)\,\big(\theta\T x_i - y_i\big)^2, \qquad w_i(x) = \exp\!\Big({-\frac{\lVert x_i - x\rVert^2}{2\tau^2}}\Big), $$

then predict \(\theta\T x\) with the locally fitted \(\theta\). The derivation is the same calculus with a diagonal weight matrix \(W = \diag(w_1, \dots, w_n)\): the objective is \(\tfrac12 (X\theta - y)\T W (X\theta - y)\), the gradient is \(X\T W X \theta - X\T W y\), and the solution is \(\theta = (X\T W X)^{-1} X\T W y\). The bandwidth \(\tau\) controls the size of the neighborhood: small \(\tau\) tracks local wiggles (low bias, high variance), large \(\tau\) recovers plain least squares (high bias, low variance), a first sighting of the tradeoff quantified exactly later. The price of locality is that nothing is learned once and reused: the model is non-parametric, the whole training set must be kept, and every prediction costs a fresh \(O(nd^2 + d^3)\) solve. The same weighted solve, with weights supplied by the model itself rather than by distance to a query, is the inner loop of Newton's method for every GLM below, which is why it earns its derivation here.

Logistic regression

Where the sigmoid comes from

For binary classification, \(y \in \{0, 1\}\), the natural model of the conditional is a Bernoulli with some mean \(\phi = p(y{=}1)\). Write the Bernoulli mass function in exponential form:

$$ p(y; \phi) = \phi^y (1-\phi)^{1-y} = \exp\!\Big( y \log\frac{\phi}{1-\phi} + \log(1-\phi) \Big). $$

The coefficient multiplying the sufficient statistic \(y\) is the natural parameter \(\eta = \log\frac{\phi}{1-\phi}\), the log-odds. Inverting it: \(e^\eta = \phi/(1-\phi)\), so \(\phi\,(1 + e^\eta) = e^\eta\) and

$$ \phi = \frac{e^\eta}{1 + e^\eta} = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\eta}} \;=\; \sigma(\eta). $$

The sigmoid is not an aesthetic choice among S-shaped curves; it is the unique function that maps the natural parameter of a Bernoulli back to its mean. Logistic regression is the decision to make the natural parameter linear in the features, \(\eta = \theta\T x\), giving \(h_\theta(x) = \sigma(\theta\T x) = p(y{=}1 \mid x; \theta)\). The general version of this construction, for any exponential-family output, is the GLM section below.

Gradient and Hessian of the log-likelihood

Both labels can be written in one expression, \(p(y \mid x; \theta) = h^y (1-h)^{1-y}\) with \(h = \sigma(\theta\T x)\), so the negative log-likelihood over the dataset is the cross-entropy

$$ \L(\theta) = -\sum_{i=1}^{n} \Big[ y_i \log h_i + (1 - y_i) \log (1 - h_i) \Big], \qquad h_i = \sigma(\theta\T x_i). $$

The one identity needed is the sigmoid's derivative. From \(\sigma(z) = (1+e^{-z})^{-1}\),

$$ \sigma'(z) = \frac{e^{-z}}{(1+e^{-z})^2} = \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}} \cdot \frac{e^{-z}}{1+e^{-z}} = \sigma(z)\,\big(1 - \sigma(z)\big). $$

Differentiate one term of \(\L\) with respect to \(\theta\) using the chain rule, with \(z_i = \theta\T x_i\):

$$ -\nabla_\theta \Big[ y_i \log h_i + (1-y_i)\log(1-h_i) \Big] = -\Big( \frac{y_i}{h_i} - \frac{1-y_i}{1-h_i} \Big) h_i (1-h_i)\, x_i = -\big( y_i(1-h_i) - (1-y_i)h_i \big) x_i = (h_i - y_i)\, x_i. $$

Summing over the data and stacking into matrices,

$$ \nabla_\theta \L = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (h_i - y_i)\, x_i = X\T (h - y), $$

the "prediction minus label, times input" form that will turn out to be universal across GLMs. Differentiating once more, only \(h_i\) depends on \(\theta\), and \(\nabla_\theta h_i = h_i(1-h_i)x_i\), so

$$ \nabla^2_\theta \L = \sum_{i=1}^{n} h_i (1 - h_i)\, x_i x_i\T = X\T W X, \qquad W = \diag\big(h_i(1-h_i)\big). $$

For any \(v\), \(v\T X\T W X v = \lVert W^{1/2} X v \rVert^2 \ge 0\) since every \(h_i(1-h_i) > 0\): the Hessian is positive semidefinite everywhere, the negative log-likelihood is convex, and any stationary point is a global minimum. One honest caveat: if the classes are linearly separable, no stationary point exists, since scaling any separating \(\theta\) up forever increases the likelihood, and the MLE runs off to infinity. Regularization (the MAP section below) restores a finite optimum, which is why production solvers regularize by default.

Newton's method and IRLS

Newton's method minimizes a function by repeatedly jumping to the minimum of its local quadratic model. Taylor-expanding \(\L\) around the current iterate \(\theta_t\),

$$ \L(\theta) \approx \L(\theta_t) + g\T (\theta - \theta_t) + \tfrac{1}{2} (\theta - \theta_t)\T H\, (\theta - \theta_t), $$

with \(g = \nabla \L(\theta_t)\) and \(H = \nabla^2 \L(\theta_t)\). Setting the gradient of the right-hand side to zero gives \(g + H(\theta - \theta_t) = 0\), i.e. the update

$$ \theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - H^{-1} g \;=\; \theta_t - \big(X\T W X\big)^{-1} X\T (h - y). $$

Near the optimum the quadratic model is nearly exact and the error squares at every step (quadratic convergence); the worked problem below shows the digit-doubling live. The update has a second reading that explains the name iteratively reweighted least squares. Substitute \(g = X\T(h-y)\) and factor:

$$ \theta_{t+1} = (X\T W X)^{-1}\big( X\T W X \theta_t - X\T (h - y) \big) = (X\T W X)^{-1} X\T W z, \qquad z = X\theta_t + W^{-1}(y - h). $$

Each Newton step is exactly a weighted least-squares solve (the locally weighted machinery from the previous section) against a working response \(z\): the current linear predictions, corrected by the residual scaled up by the inverse variance. Points with \(h_i\) near 0 or 1 have tiny weight \(h_i(1-h_i)\) and correspondingly amplified working residuals; points near the decision boundary carry the most weight. Every classical GLM fitter, from the original Nelder-Wedderburn formulation to statsmodels today, is this loop.

Problem 2

Take the four training points \(x = 0, 1, 2, 3\) with labels \(y = 0, 1, 0, 1\) and the model \(p(y{=}1 \mid x) = \sigma(\theta_0 + \theta_1 x)\). Starting from \(\theta = (0, 0)\), carry out one full Newton step by hand: gradient, Hessian, matrix inverse, and update. Then state what the second iteration and the converged solution are, and verify the quadratic convergence pattern in the gradient norms.

Solution. The design matrix rows are \((1, x_i)\). At \(\theta = (0,0)\) every logit is 0, so every \(h_i = \sigma(0) = 0.5\) and every weight is \(h_i(1-h_i) = 0.25\). Gradient, using \(h - y = (0.5, -0.5, 0.5, -0.5)\):

$$ g = X\T(h - y) = \begin{pmatrix} 0.5 - 0.5 + 0.5 - 0.5 \\ 0(0.5) + 1(-0.5) + 2(0.5) + 3(-0.5) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}. $$

Hessian: with all weights equal to 0.25, \(H = 0.25\, X\T X = 0.25 \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 6 \\ 6 & 14 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1.5 \\ 1.5 & 3.5 \end{pmatrix}\). Its determinant is \(3.5 - 2.25 = 1.25\), so

$$ H^{-1} = \frac{1}{1.25}\begin{pmatrix} 3.5 & -1.5 \\ -1.5 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2.8 & -1.2 \\ -1.2 & 0.8 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad H^{-1} g = \begin{pmatrix} 2.8(0) - 1.2(-1) \\ -1.2(0) + 0.8(-1) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1.2 \\ -0.8 \end{pmatrix}. $$

The Newton update is \(\theta_1 = \theta_0 - H^{-1}g = (-1.2,\; 0.8)\). Running the same arithmetic again from \(\theta_1\) (logits \(-1.2, -0.4, 0.4, 1.2\), probabilities \(0.23148, 0.40131, 0.59869, 0.76852\)) gives gradient \((0, -0.09574)\), Hessian \(\begin{pmatrix} 0.83631 & 1.25447 \\ 1.25447 & 2.80235\end{pmatrix}\), and \(\theta_2 = (-1.35598,\; 0.90399)\). Iterating to convergence yields \(\theta^\ast = (-1.36228,\; 0.90818)\) with final probabilities \((0.204, 0.388, 0.612, 0.796)\) and negative log-likelihood 2.34749. The gradient's second component falls as \(1 \to 9.57 \times 10^{-2} \to 3.57 \times 10^{-3} \to 5.81 \times 10^{-6} \to\) machine zero: the number of correct digits roughly doubles per step, the signature of quadratic convergence. All values match the PyTorch and JAX runs in the implementation section to the printed precision.

Generalized linear models

The exponential family

A family of distributions is an exponential family if its density or mass function can be written as

$$ p(y; \eta) = b(y)\, \exp\!\big( \eta\T T(y) - a(\eta) \big), $$

with natural parameter \(\eta\), sufficient statistic \(T(y)\) (usually just \(y\)), log partition function \(a(\eta)\), and base measure \(b(y)\). The role of \(a\) is to normalize: \(a(\eta) = \log \int b(y) e^{\eta\T T(y)}\, dy\). The Bernoulli was put in this form above, with \(T(y) = y\), \(\eta = \log\frac{\phi}{1-\phi}\), and \(a(\eta) = -\log(1-\phi) = \log(1 + e^\eta)\). The Gaussian with unit variance also fits: expand

$$ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-(y-\mu)^2/2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-y^2/2} \cdot \exp\!\big( \mu y - \tfrac{1}{2}\mu^2 \big), $$

so \(\eta = \mu\), \(T(y) = y\), \(a(\eta) = \eta^2/2\), and \(b(y)\) absorbs the \(\theta\)-free factor. The Poisson: \(p(y; \lambda) = \frac{\lambda^y e^{-\lambda}}{y!} = \frac{1}{y!} \exp(y \log\lambda - \lambda)\), so \(\eta = \log \lambda\), \(a(\eta) = \lambda = e^\eta\). The multinomial joins the family in Problem 9. The log partition function is not bookkeeping; it is a moment generating machine. Differentiate the identity \(\int b(y)\, e^{\eta T(y) - a(\eta)}\, dy = 1\) with respect to \(\eta\) (scalar case, differentiation under the integral):

$$ \int b(y)\, e^{\eta T(y) - a(\eta)} \big( T(y) - a'(\eta) \big)\, dy = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \E[T(y)] = a'(\eta). $$

Differentiating once more gives \(\int p\,(T - a')^2\,dy - a'' = 0\), i.e. \(\Var[T(y)] = a''(\eta)\). The mean is the first derivative of \(a\), the variance the second. Check against the members: Bernoulli, \(a' = \frac{e^\eta}{1+e^\eta} = \sigma(\eta) = \phi\) and \(a'' = \sigma(1-\sigma) = \phi(1-\phi)\); Gaussian, \(a' = \eta = \mu\), \(a'' = 1\); Poisson, \(a' = a'' = e^\eta = \lambda\), the equal-mean-and-variance property. Since \(a'' \) is a variance it is nonnegative, so \(a\) is convex, a fact about to guarantee that every GLM has a convex fitting problem.

The GLM construction and the shared gradient

A generalized linear model makes three assumptions: (1) given \(x\), the target follows an exponential-family distribution; (2) the natural parameter is linear in the features, \(\eta = \theta\T x\) (the canonical link; non-canonical links insert a function between them); (3) the prediction is the conditional mean \(h_\theta(x) = \E[y \mid x] = a'(\theta\T x)\). The negative log-likelihood of one observation, dropping the \(\theta\)-free \(\log b(y)\), is

$$ \L_i(\theta) = a(\theta\T x_i) - y_i \,\theta\T x_i, $$

and the chain rule gives its gradient in two lines:

$$ \nabla_\theta \L_i = a'(\theta\T x_i)\, x_i - y_i x_i = \big( \underbrace{a'(\theta\T x_i)}_{\hat y_i}\, -\, y_i \big)\, x_i. $$

For every canonical-link GLM, the gradient of the negative log-likelihood is "prediction minus target, times input". Least squares (\(a' = \eta\)), logistic regression (\(a' = \sigma\)), Poisson regression (\(a' = \exp\)), and softmax regression all share it; the only thing that changes is the mean function. Differentiating once more, \(\nabla^2_\theta \L = \sum_i a''(\theta\T x_i)\, x_i x_i\T = X\T W X\) with weights equal to the conditional variances, positive semidefinite because \(a''\) is a variance: every canonical GLM is a convex problem, and Newton's method on it is IRLS with variance-function weights. This is also why softmax cross-entropy plus a final linear layer produces the tidy "probabilities minus one-hot" gradient in every deep-learning framework: the last layer of a classifier is a canonical GLM, whatever sits below it.

Poisson regression, worked

Counts (arrivals, claims, page views) get \(y \mid x \sim \text{Poisson}(e^{\theta\T x})\). The exponential mean function guarantees positivity and makes effects multiplicative: a unit increase in feature \(j\) multiplies the predicted rate by \(e^{\theta_j}\), which is why insurance and epidemiology report rate ratios.

Problem 3

Data: \(x = 1, 2, 3\) (no intercept), \(y = 1, 3, 5\), model \(y \sim \text{Poisson}(e^{\theta x})\). Starting from \(\theta = 0\), compute the gradient and Hessian of the negative log-likelihood by hand and take one Newton step. Compare with the converged solution and explain the overshoot.

Solution. At \(\theta = 0\) every predicted rate is \(\mu_i = e^0 = 1\). The shared GLM gradient form gives \( g = \sum_i (\mu_i - y_i)\, x_i = (1-1)(1) + (1-3)(2) + (1-5)(3) = 0 - 4 - 12 = -16, \) and the Hessian is \( H = \sum_i \mu_i x_i^2 = 1(1) + 1(4) + 1(9) = 14. \) The Newton step is \(\theta_1 = 0 - (-16)/14 = 8/7 \approx 1.1429\). The converged MLE, from the implementation section, is \(\theta^\ast = 0.52680\) with fitted rates \((1.694, 2.868, 4.857)\); the first step overshot by more than a factor of two. The reason is that the quadratic model is built from the variance at the current point (\(\mu_i = 1\)), but the true curvature grows exponentially as \(\theta\) increases, so the local quadratic badly underestimates the function ahead of it. Subsequent steps land in a region where the quadratic model is accurate and convergence becomes quadratic. Production fitters guard this first-step behavior with step halving or a line search; the mathematics of the iteration is unchanged.

Generative versus discriminative

Logistic regression models \(p(y \mid x)\) directly and never commits to how \(x\) is distributed. A generative classifier models the class-conditional densities \(p(x \mid y)\) and the prior \(p(y)\), then classifies through Bayes' rule, \(p(y \mid x) \propto p(x \mid y)\, p(y)\). The two approaches answer the same question from opposite directions, and their comparison is one of the cleanest theory results in the subject.

Gaussian discriminant analysis

Model: \(y \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\phi)\), and \(x \mid y{=}k \sim \N(\mu_k, \Sigma)\) with a shared covariance. The joint log-likelihood over the data is

$$ \ell = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \Big[ \log p(y_i; \phi) + \log \N(x_i; \mu_{y_i}, \Sigma) \Big]. $$

Maximizing over \(\phi\): the first sum is \(\sum_i [y_i \log\phi + (1-y_i)\log(1-\phi)]\), whose derivative \(\frac{n_1}{\phi} - \frac{n - n_1}{1-\phi}\) vanishes at \(\hat\phi = n_1 / n\), the class-1 fraction (\(n_1 = \sum_i y_i\)). Maximizing over \(\mu_1\): only class-1 terms matter, each contributing \(-\tfrac12 (x_i - \mu_1)\T \Sigma^{-1} (x_i - \mu_1)\); the gradient with respect to \(\mu_1\) is \(\Sigma^{-1} \sum_{i: y_i = 1} (x_i - \mu_1)\), which vanishes at the class mean \(\hat\mu_1 = \frac{1}{n_1}\sum_{i:y_i=1} x_i\) (and symmetrically for \(\hat\mu_0\)). For \(\Sigma\), parameterize by the precision \(\Lambda = \Sigma^{-1}\); the Gaussian terms contribute \(\tfrac{n}{2}\log\lvert\Lambda\rvert - \tfrac12 \sum_i (x_i - \mu_{y_i})\T \Lambda\, (x_i - \mu_{y_i})\). Two matrix derivatives, both standard and both derivable entrywise: \(\partial \log\lvert\Lambda\rvert / \partial \Lambda = \Lambda^{-1}\) (from the cofactor expansion of the determinant) and \(\partial\, (v\T \Lambda v) / \partial \Lambda = v v\T\). Setting the gradient to zero:

$$ \frac{n}{2}\, \Lambda^{-1} - \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \mu_{y_i})(x_i - \mu_{y_i})\T = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \hat\Sigma = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \mu_{y_i})(x_i - \mu_{y_i})\T, $$

the pooled within-class covariance. Every estimate is a counting or averaging operation; no iteration, no solver. That is the practical appeal of generative training.

The posterior is a sigmoid

Now compute what GDA believes about \(y\) given \(x\):

$$ p(y{=}1 \mid x) = \frac{ \phi\, \N(x; \mu_1, \Sigma) }{ \phi\, \N(x; \mu_1, \Sigma) + (1-\phi)\, \N(x; \mu_0, \Sigma) } = \frac{1}{1 + \exp(-A(x))}, $$

where \(A(x)\) is the log of the ratio of the two numerator terms. Expanding the Gaussian exponents, the quadratic pieces \(-\tfrac12 x\T \Sigma^{-1} x\) are identical in both classes because the covariance is shared, and they cancel:

$$ A(x) = \log\frac{\phi}{1-\phi} + \tfrac{1}{2}\big( \mu_0\T \Sigma^{-1} \mu_0 - \mu_1\T \Sigma^{-1} \mu_1 \big) + (\mu_1 - \mu_0)\T \Sigma^{-1} x. $$

This is linear in \(x\): the GDA posterior is exactly \(\sigma(\theta\T x + \theta_0)\) with \(\theta = \Sigma^{-1}(\mu_1 - \mu_0)\). A one-dimensional numerical check: with \(\mu_0 = -1\), \(\mu_1 = 2\), \(\sigma^2 = 1.5\), \(\phi = 0.4\), the formulas give \(\theta_1 = (2-(-1))/1.5 = 2\) and \(\theta_0 = \frac{1 - 4}{2(1.5)} + \log\frac{0.4}{0.6} = -1 - 0.40546 = -1.40546\); evaluating both the direct Bayes ratio and \(\sigma(2x - 1.40546)\) at \(x = 0.7\) yields 0.498634 from each, agreeing to all printed digits. The converse fails: a logistic-form posterior does not imply Gaussian classes (Poisson class-conditionals also produce one). GDA therefore makes strictly stronger assumptions than logistic regression. The classical results quantify the tradeoff: Efron (1975) showed that when the Gaussian assumption holds, discriminant analysis is asymptotically more efficient (logistic regression pays roughly a one-third efficiency penalty at moderate separations), while Ng and Jordan (2001) showed the flip side, that the generative model converges to its (higher) asymptotic error with \(O(\log d)\) samples against \(O(d)\) for the discriminative one, so the generative model can win small-sample races even when its assumptions are wrong enough to lose in the limit.

Naive Bayes and Laplace smoothing

For high-dimensional discrete \(x\) (documents as word vectors), modeling \(p(x \mid y)\) in full is hopeless: a vocabulary of 50,000 binary features would need \(2^{50000}\)-sized tables. Naive Bayes assumes the features are conditionally independent given the class, \(p(x \mid y) = \prod_j p(x_j \mid y)\), collapsing the table to per-word probabilities. The MLE for each is a count ratio, and therein lies a trap: a word never seen in a class gets probability zero, and one such word in a test document vetoes the entire class regardless of all other evidence. Laplace smoothing adds one phantom occurrence of every word to every class. This is not a hack: placing a uniform Dirichlet prior on the word distribution and taking the posterior mean gives \(\hat p_{jk} = \frac{N_{jk} + 1}{N_k + V}\) exactly, where \(N_{jk}\) counts word \(j\) in class \(k\), \(N_k\) is the class's total word count, and \(V\) the vocabulary size.

Problem 4

A multinomial Naive Bayes spam filter has vocabulary {cheap, win, meeting, project}. The spam training corpus contains these words 4, 3, 0, and 1 times respectively (8 tokens); the ham corpus contains them 0, 1, 4, and 3 times (8 tokens). Priors are \(\tfrac12\) each. Using Laplace smoothing, classify the message "cheap cheap meeting" and give the exact posterior probability of spam. Then show what happens without smoothing.

Solution. With \(V = 4\), the smoothed estimates divide counts plus one by \(8 + 4 = 12\):

$$ p(\text{word} \mid \text{spam}) = \Big( \tfrac{5}{12}, \tfrac{4}{12}, \tfrac{1}{12}, \tfrac{2}{12} \Big), \qquad p(\text{word} \mid \text{ham}) = \Big( \tfrac{1}{12}, \tfrac{2}{12}, \tfrac{5}{12}, \tfrac{4}{12} \Big). $$

The message has counts (2, 0, 1, 0), so the unnormalized scores are

$$ s_{\text{spam}} = \tfrac{1}{2} \Big(\tfrac{5}{12}\Big)^2 \tfrac{1}{12} = \tfrac{25}{3456}, \qquad s_{\text{ham}} = \tfrac{1}{2} \Big(\tfrac{1}{12}\Big)^2 \tfrac{5}{12} = \tfrac{5}{3456}. $$

The likelihood ratio is exactly 5, so \( p(\text{spam} \mid \text{message}) = \frac{25}{25 + 5} = \frac{5}{6} \approx 0.8333. \) The two occurrences of "cheap" (25 times likelier in spam) outvote the single "meeting" (5 times likelier in ham). Without smoothing, \(p(\text{meeting} \mid \text{spam}) = 0/8 = 0\), so the spam score is exactly zero and the message is called ham with probability 1, on the strength of one word the spam corpus happened never to contain. A single pseudocount is the difference between a confident right answer and a certain wrong one.

The bias-variance decomposition, exactly

Fix a query point \(x\). Data are generated as \(y = f(x) + \varepsilon\) with \(\E[\varepsilon] = 0\), \(\Var[\varepsilon] = \sigma^2\), and a learning procedure trained on a random dataset \(\D\) produces a predictor \(\hat h_\D\). The expected squared error of the prediction at \(x\), over both the noise in the test label and the randomness of the training set, decomposes with no approximation. Write \(\bar h(x) = \E_\D[\hat h_\D(x)]\) for the average prediction over training sets. First split off the test noise:

$$ \E\big[ (y - \hat h_\D(x))^2 \big] = \E\big[ (\varepsilon + f(x) - \hat h_\D(x))^2 \big] = \sigma^2 + \E_\D\big[ (f(x) - \hat h_\D(x))^2 \big], $$

because \(\varepsilon\) is independent of \(\D\) and has mean zero, killing the cross term \(2\,\E[\varepsilon]\,\E_\D[f - \hat h_\D] = 0\). Now insert and subtract \(\bar h(x)\) inside the remaining square:

$$ \E_\D\big[ (f - \bar h + \bar h - \hat h_\D)^2 \big] = (f - \bar h)^2 + \E_\D\big[ (\bar h - \hat h_\D)^2 \big] + 2 (f - \bar h)\, \E_\D[\bar h - \hat h_\D]. $$

The last expectation is \(\bar h - \bar h = 0\) by the definition of \(\bar h\), so the cross term vanishes identically and

$$ \E\big[ (y - \hat h_\D(x))^2 \big] \;=\; \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{irreducible}} \;+\; \underbrace{\big( f(x) - \bar h(x) \big)^2}_{\text{bias}^2} \;+\; \underbrace{\E_\D\big[ (\hat h_\D(x) - \bar h(x))^2 \big]}_{\text{variance}}. $$

Nothing here is an inequality or an asymptotic statement; for squared loss the decomposition is an identity, made famous for neural networks by Geman, Bienenstock, and Doursat (1992). Bias is the systematic error of the procedure's average answer; variance is how much the answer sloshes across training sets. Constraining a model (fewer features, more regularization, larger kernel bandwidth) typically raises bias and lowers variance; the sum is what matters, and the next problem shows the sum being genuinely minimized at nonzero regularization.

Regularization as a prior

Ridge regression adds a quadratic penalty to least squares:

$$ J_\lambda(\theta) = \tfrac{1}{2} \lVert X\theta - y \rVert^2 + \tfrac{\lambda}{2} \lVert \theta \rVert^2, \qquad \nabla J_\lambda = X\T X\theta - X\T y + \lambda\theta = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \theta_\lambda = (X\T X + \lambda I)^{-1} X\T y. $$

The regularized system is always solvable: \(X\T X\) is positive semidefinite with eigenvalues \(d_i^2 \ge 0\), so \(X\T X + \lambda I\) has eigenvalues \(d_i^2 + \lambda \ge \lambda > 0\) and is invertible regardless of collinearity or \(d > n\). In the SVD basis \(X = U D V\T\), the ridge solution is \(\theta_\lambda = V \, \diag\!\big(\frac{d_i}{d_i^2 + \lambda}\big)\, U\T y\): each singular direction is shrunk by the factor \(d_i^2/(d_i^2 + \lambda)\), barely touching strong directions and crushing weak ones, which is exactly where noise dominates.

The Bayesian reading makes \(\lambda\) interpretable. Put a prior \(\theta \sim \N(0, \tau^2 I)\) and keep the Gaussian noise model \(y_i \mid x_i, \theta \sim \N(\theta\T x_i, \sigma^2)\). The maximum a posteriori estimate maximizes the log posterior, which by Bayes' rule is the log likelihood plus the log prior plus a constant:

$$ \log p(\theta \mid \D) = -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \theta\T x_i)^2 \;-\; \frac{1}{2\tau^2} \lVert \theta \rVert^2 + \text{const}. $$

Multiplying through by \(-\sigma^2\) (which preserves the argmax up to flipping to argmin), the MAP problem is exactly ridge with

$$ \lambda = \frac{\sigma^2}{\tau^2}: $$

noisy data or a tight prior push \(\lambda\) up, clean data or a diffuse prior push it toward zero, and \(\tau \to \infty\) recovers plain maximum likelihood. The same computation with a Laplace prior \(p(\theta_j) \propto e^{-\lvert\theta_j\rvert / b}\) yields an \(\ell_1\) penalty, the lasso, whose non-differentiable corner at zero produces exact sparsity. And the same computation applied to the logistic likelihood gives \(\ell_2\)-regularized logistic regression, which restores a finite unique optimum on separable data: the likelihood's urge to scale \(\theta\) to infinity now loses to the quadratic prior for all sufficiently large \(\lVert\theta\rVert\). One caution carried from the decomposition above: MAP with the prior treated as a tuning knob is not "being Bayesian", it is choosing a regularizer; the honest Bayesian object is the posterior, and the honest frequentist tool for choosing \(\lambda\) is cross-validation.

Kernels

Feature maps and the kernel trick

Linear models become nonlinear by mapping inputs through a feature map \(\phi: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{H}\) and fitting a linear model in \(\mathcal{H}\). The catch is cost: degree-3 polynomial features of a 1,000-dimensional input already number in the hundreds of millions. The observation that rescues this is that many algorithms touch the features only through inner products \(\langle \phi(x), \phi(x')\rangle\). If that inner product has a closed form \(k(x, x')\) computable in the original space, the algorithm can run in \(\mathcal{H}\) without ever materializing it. For example, for \(x, z \in \R^d\),

$$ (x\T z + c)^2 = \sum_{j,k} (x_j x_k)(z_j z_k) + \sum_j (\sqrt{2c}\, x_j)(\sqrt{2c}\, z_j) + c^2, $$

which is an explicit inner product of \(O(d^2)\)-dimensional feature vectors, computed in \(O(d)\) time. The Gaussian kernel \(k(x, z) = \exp(-\lVert x - z\rVert^2 / 2\tau^2)\) corresponds, via the exponential's power series, to an infinite-dimensional feature map; no finite \(\phi\) reproduces it.

The representer theorem

Why is it legitimate to search only over functions of the form \(f(\cdot) = \sum_i \alpha_i k(\cdot, x_i)\), a finite-dimensional family, when the hypothesis space is an infinite-dimensional reproducing kernel Hilbert space? Because the optimum always lies there. Let \(\mathcal{H}\) be the RKHS of kernel \(k\), whose defining (reproducing) property is \(f(x) = \langle f, k(\cdot, x)\rangle_{\mathcal H}\) for every \(f \in \mathcal{H}\). Consider any problem of the form

$$ \min_{f \in \mathcal{H}} \;\; L\big( f(x_1), \dots, f(x_n) \big) + \Omega\big( \lVert f \rVert_{\mathcal H} \big), $$

where \(L\) is an arbitrary loss depending on \(f\) only through its values at the training points and \(\Omega\) is strictly increasing. Decompose any candidate \(f\) orthogonally against the span of the training-point representers: \(f = f_\parallel + f_\perp\) with \(f_\parallel \in \mathrm{span}\{k(\cdot, x_1), \dots, k(\cdot, x_n)\}\) and \(\langle f_\perp, k(\cdot, x_i)\rangle = 0\) for all \(i\). Two consequences follow. First, by the reproducing property, \(f(x_i) = \langle f_\parallel + f_\perp,\, k(\cdot, x_i)\rangle = f_\parallel(x_i)\): the orthogonal component is invisible to the loss. Second, by Pythagoras, \(\lVert f \rVert^2 = \lVert f_\parallel \rVert^2 + \lVert f_\perp \rVert^2 \ge \lVert f_\parallel \rVert^2\), with equality only if \(f_\perp = 0\). So replacing \(f\) by \(f_\parallel\) leaves the loss unchanged and strictly decreases \(\Omega\) unless \(f_\perp = 0\): every minimizer has the form

$$ f^\ast(\cdot) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \alpha_i\, k(\cdot, x_i). $$

This is the representer theorem, proved for splines by Kimeldorf and Wahba (1971) and in the general form above by Schölkopf, Herbrich, and Smola (2001). It converts an infinite-dimensional variational problem into an \(n\)-dimensional one, and it is the license behind kernel ridge regression, SVMs, and Gaussian-process posterior means alike. The price is visible in the same formula: the solution size grows with \(n\), which is the scaling wall the practice section returns to.

Which functions are kernels

A function \(k\) is a valid kernel exactly when it is symmetric and every Gram matrix \(K\) with \(K_{ij} = k(x_i, x_j)\) is positive semidefinite. Necessity is a two-line computation: if \(k(x, z) = \langle \phi(x), \phi(z)\rangle\) then for any coefficients \(c\),

$$ c\T K c = \sum_{i,j} c_i c_j \langle \phi(x_i), \phi(x_j)\rangle = \Big\lVert \sum_i c_i\, \phi(x_i) \Big\rVert^2 \;\ge\; 0. $$

Sufficiency, that PSD Gram matrices guarantee some feature space exists, is Mercer's theorem (1909) in the continuous setting and the Moore-Aronszajn construction of the RKHS in general; the constructions are out of scope here and live in Schölkopf and Smola's Learning with Kernels, chapter 2. What matters operationally is the closure algebra. If \(k_1, k_2\) are kernels then \(k_1 + k_2\) is (Gram matrices add, and PSD matrices are closed under addition since \(c\T(K_1 + K_2)c = c\T K_1 c + c\T K_2 c \ge 0\)); \(a k_1\) is for \(a \ge 0\); and \(k_1 k_2\) is, because the elementwise product of PSD matrices is PSD (Schur's product theorem; concretely, the product kernel's feature map is the tensor product \(\phi_1 \otimes \phi_2\)). Polynomials with nonnegative coefficients and limits of kernels are therefore kernels, which is the modular arithmetic by which the Gaussian kernel is assembled from \(e^{x\T z/\tau^2}\) and normalization.

The SVM: primal, dual, KKT

For separable data with labels \(y_i \in \{-1, +1\}\), a hyperplane \(w\T x + b = 0\) classifies by sign, and the geometric distance from \(x_i\) to it is \(y_i(w\T x_i + b) / \lVert w \rVert\) when correctly classified. Maximizing the minimum distance is awkward as stated because \((w, b)\) can be rescaled freely; fix the scale by requiring the closest points to satisfy \(y_i(w\T x_i + b) = 1\). Then the margin is \(1/\lVert w\rVert\) on each side and maximizing it is

$$ \min_{w, b} \;\; \tfrac{1}{2} \lVert w \rVert^2 \qquad \text{s.t.} \quad y_i (w\T x_i + b) \ge 1, \;\; i = 1, \dots, n. $$

Introduce multipliers \(\alpha_i \ge 0\) for the constraints and form the Lagrangian

$$ \mathcal{L}(w, b, \alpha) = \tfrac{1}{2}\lVert w \rVert^2 - \sum_{i=1}^{n} \alpha_i \big[ y_i (w\T x_i + b) - 1 \big]. $$

The problem is convex with affine constraints, so strong duality holds (Slater's condition) and the optimum satisfies the KKT conditions. Stationarity in \(w\) and \(b\):

$$ \nabla_w \mathcal{L} = w - \sum_i \alpha_i y_i x_i = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; w = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i x_i, \qquad \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial b} = -\sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0. $$

Substituting both back into \(\mathcal{L}\): the quadratic term becomes \(\tfrac12 \sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j x_i\T x_j\), the constraint term expands to \(-\sum_{i,j} \alpha_i\alpha_j y_i y_j x_i\T x_j - b \sum_i \alpha_i y_i + \sum_i \alpha_i\), and the \(b\) term dies by the second stationarity condition, leaving the dual

$$ \max_{\alpha \ge 0,\; \sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0} \;\; W(\alpha) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \alpha_i - \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j\, \langle x_i, x_j \rangle. $$

Two structural facts drop out. Complementary slackness, \(\alpha_i [y_i(w\T x_i + b) - 1] = 0\), forces \(\alpha_i = 0\) for every point strictly outside the margin: the solution is supported on the margin points alone, the support vectors, and deleting any other training point changes nothing. And the data enter the dual only through inner products, so replacing \(\langle x_i, x_j\rangle\) with \(k(x_i, x_j)\) trains the maximum- margin classifier in the kernel's feature space at no extra cost; prediction likewise needs only \(f(x) = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i k(x_i, x) + b\), a representer-theorem form. For non-separable data, slack variables \(\xi_i \ge 0\) with penalty \(C\sum_i \xi_i\) in the primal produce the identical dual with the single change \(0 \le \alpha_i \le C\) (the derivation adds multipliers for \(\xi_i \ge 0\) and the box constraint appears when they are eliminated); this soft-margin form is the one Cortes and Vapnik published in 1995 and the one every solver implements.

Problem 6

Three points in the plane: \(x_1 = (0,0)\) with \(y_1 = -1\), \(x_2 = (2,2)\) with \(y_2 = +1\), \(x_3 = (4,0)\) with \(y_3 = +1\). Solve the hard-margin SVM dual by hand: find the optimal \(\alpha\), recover \(w\) and \(b\), verify every KKT condition, compute the margin, and confirm strong duality by comparing primal and dual objective values.

Solution. The Gram matrix of inner products is

$$ K = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 8 & 8 \\ 0 & 8 & 16 \end{pmatrix}, $$

and with \(y = (-1, +1, +1)\) the equality constraint \(\sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0\) gives \(\alpha_1 = \alpha_2 + \alpha_3\). Since every \(K_{1j} = 0\), all dual quadratic terms involving \(\alpha_1\) vanish and the objective reduces to a function of \((\alpha_2, \alpha_3)\) alone:

$$ W = 2\alpha_2 + 2\alpha_3 - \tfrac{1}{2}\big( 8\alpha_2^2 + 16\alpha_2\alpha_3 + 16\alpha_3^2 \big) = 2\alpha_2 + 2\alpha_3 - 4\alpha_2^2 - 8\alpha_2\alpha_3 - 8\alpha_3^2. $$

Setting partial derivatives to zero: \(\partial W/\partial\alpha_2 = 2 - 8\alpha_2 - 8\alpha_3 = 0\) and \(\partial W/\partial\alpha_3 = 2 - 8\alpha_2 - 16\alpha_3 = 0\). Subtracting gives \(8\alpha_3 = 0\), so \(\alpha_3 = 0\) and \(\alpha_2 = \tfrac14\), hence \(\alpha = (\tfrac14, \tfrac14, 0)\), which satisfies \(\alpha \ge 0\). Recover the hyperplane:

$$ w = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i x_i = \tfrac{1}{4}(-1)(0,0) + \tfrac{1}{4}(+1)(2,2) = \big(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}\big), $$

and from the active constraint at the support vector \(x_1\): \(y_1(w\T x_1 + b) = 1\) gives \(-b = 1\), so \(b = -1\). KKT check on all three points: \(y_1 f(x_1) = -(0 - 1) = 1\) (active, \(\alpha_1 > 0\), consistent); \(y_2 f(x_2) = (1 + 1 - 1) = 1\) (active, \(\alpha_2 > 0\)); \(y_3 f(x_3) = (2 + 0 - 1) = 1 \ge 1\) with \(\alpha_3 = 0\) (complementary slackness holds; the point happens to sit on the margin but carries no weight, showing that "on the margin" and "support vector" are not synonyms). The margin is \(1/\lVert w\rVert = 1/\sqrt{1/2} = \sqrt 2\) per side, a corridor of total width \(2\sqrt2 \approx 2.828\). Strong duality: the primal objective is \(\tfrac12 \lVert w \rVert^2 = \tfrac12 \cdot \tfrac12 = \tfrac14\), and the dual is \(W = \sum \alpha_i - \tfrac12 \cdot \tfrac12 = \tfrac12 - \tfrac14 = \tfrac14\). Equal, as strong duality requires; the SMO and projected-gradient solvers in the implementation section both return \(\alpha = (0.25, 0.25, 0)\), \(w = (0.5, 0.5)\), \(b = -1\) exactly.

Expectation-maximization

Jensen's inequality

Everything rests on one inequality. Let \(f\) be concave and \(X\) a random variable. A concave function lies below its tangent line at any point; take the tangent at \(\mu = \E[X]\): there exists a slope \(g\) with \(f(x) \le f(\mu) + g\,(x - \mu)\) for all \(x\) (for differentiable \(f\), \(g = f'(\mu)\); otherwise any supergradient works). Take expectations of both sides; \(\E[x - \mu] = 0\), so

$$ \E[f(X)] \;\le\; f\big(\E[X]\big), $$

with equality when \(X\) is degenerate or \(f\) is affine on the support of \(X\). For \(f = \log\), strictly concave, equality requires \(X\) constant with probability 1. This is the entire mathematical content of EM; the rest is arrangement.

The evidence lower bound

A latent-variable model defines \(p(x, z; \theta)\), but the data show only \(x\), so the quantity to maximize is \(\log p(x; \theta) = \log \sum_z p(x, z; \theta)\), a log of a sum that couples all configurations of \(z\) and destroys the clean separation MLE enjoys with complete data. Multiply and divide by any distribution \(q(z)\) with the right support, and read the sum as an expectation over \(q\):

$$ \log p(x; \theta) = \log \sum_z q(z)\, \frac{p(x, z; \theta)}{q(z)} = \log\, \E_{q}\!\left[ \frac{p(x, z; \theta)}{q(z)} \right] \;\ge\; \E_{q}\!\left[ \log \frac{p(x, z; \theta)}{q(z)} \right] \;=:\; \mathrm{ELBO}(q, \theta), $$

by Jensen applied to the concave \(\log\). How loose is the bound? Expand the ELBO using \(p(x, z) = p(z \mid x)\, p(x)\):

$$ \mathrm{ELBO}(q, \theta) = \E_q\big[ \log p(z \mid x; \theta) + \log p(x; \theta) - \log q(z) \big] = \log p(x; \theta) - \KL\big( q(z) \,\Vert\, p(z \mid x; \theta) \big). $$

The gap between the log evidence and the bound is exactly a KL divergence, which is nonnegative and zero iff \(q\) equals the true posterior. So the bound is tight, and Jensen's equality condition (the ratio \(p(x,z)/q(z)\) constant in \(z\)) says the same thing, when

$$ q(z) = p(z \mid x; \theta). $$

EM is coordinate ascent on the ELBO. The E step fixes \(\theta\) and sets \(q\) to the posterior under the current parameters, raising the bound until it touches \(\log p(x;\theta)\). The M step fixes \(q\) and maximizes the ELBO over \(\theta\), which since \(\E_q[\log q]\) is then constant means maximizing the expected complete-data log-likelihood \(\E_q[\log p(x, z; \theta)]\), usually a closed-form problem. Monotonicity follows in one line: after an E step the bound equals the objective; the M step can only raise the bound; and the new objective is at least the new bound. Hence \(\log p(x; \theta_{t+1}) \ge \log p(x; \theta_t)\), the guarantee proved by Dempster, Laird, and Rubin (1977), with the convergence fine print (to a stationary point, not a global optimum, and under regularity conditions) supplied by Wu (1983).

EM for a Gaussian mixture, derived

The model: \(z_i \in \{1, \dots, K\}\) with \(\P(z_i = k) = \pi_k\), and \(x_i \mid z_i{=}k \sim \N(\mu_k, \sigma_k^2)\) (scalar case; the vector case replaces \(\sigma_k^2\) with \(\Sigma_k\) and nothing else changes structurally). E step: the posterior over each \(z_i\) is Bayes' rule over \(K\) alternatives, the responsibilities

$$ r_{ik} = \frac{ \pi_k\, \N(x_i; \mu_k, \sigma_k^2) }{ \sum_{j=1}^{K} \pi_j\, \N(x_i; \mu_j, \sigma_j^2) }. $$

M step: the expected complete-data log-likelihood is

$$ Q(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{k=1}^{K} r_{ik} \Big[ \log \pi_k - \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma_k^2) - \frac{(x_i - \mu_k)^2}{2\sigma_k^2} \Big]. $$

Maximize over \(\mu_k\): \(\partial Q/\partial \mu_k = \sum_i r_{ik} (x_i - \mu_k)/\sigma_k^2 = 0\) gives a responsibility-weighted mean. Over \(\sigma_k^2\): setting \(\partial Q / \partial \sigma_k^2 = \sum_i r_{ik} \big[ -\frac{1}{2\sigma_k^2} + \frac{(x_i - \mu_k)^2}{2\sigma_k^4} \big] = 0\) gives a weighted variance. Over \(\pi\), which must sum to one, add a Lagrange multiplier \(\beta(\sum_k \pi_k - 1)\): stationarity gives \(\sum_i r_{ik}/\pi_k + \beta = 0\), so \(\pi_k \propto \sum_i r_{ik}\), and summing over \(k\) fixes the constant since \(\sum_k \sum_i r_{ik} = n\). Writing \(N_k = \sum_i r_{ik}\) for the effective count assigned to component \(k\):

$$ \pi_k = \frac{N_k}{n}, \qquad \mu_k = \frac{1}{N_k} \sum_{i=1}^{n} r_{ik}\, x_i, \qquad \sigma_k^2 = \frac{1}{N_k} \sum_{i=1}^{n} r_{ik}\, (x_i - \mu_k)^2. $$

These are exactly the complete-data MLE formulas with hard labels replaced by soft ones, which is the right way to remember EM: pretend the posterior is a fractional labeling and re-estimate as if supervised. One warning belongs in the same breath: the GMM likelihood is unbounded (center one component on a data point and shrink its variance to zero and the likelihood diverges), so EM converges to a good local maximum only from reasonable initializations, and production implementations add a variance floor or a prior.

Problem 7

Data: \(x = \{0, 1, 5, 6\}\). Two-component 1-D GMM initialized at \(\pi_1 = 0.5\), \(\mu_1 = 1\), \(\mu_2 = 4\), \(\sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2 = 1\). Carry out one full EM iteration by hand: all four responsibilities, then the updated \(\pi_1, \mu_1, \mu_2, \sigma_1^2, \sigma_2^2\). Report the log-likelihood before and after, and the fixed point the iteration converges to.

Solution. E step. With equal priors and equal variances the responsibility of component 1 simplifies to a sigmoid of the log-likelihood ratio: \( r_{i1} = \sigma(\ell_i) \) with \( \ell_i = \frac{(x_i - \mu_2)^2 - (x_i - \mu_1)^2}{2}. \) Computing the four log-odds: for \(x{=}0\), \((16 - 1)/2 = 7.5\); for \(x{=}1\), \((9 - 0)/2 = 4.5\); for \(x{=}5\), \((1 - 16)/2 = -7.5\); for \(x{=}6\), \((4 - 25)/2 = -10.5\). Applying the sigmoid:

$$ r_1 = (0.999447,\; 0.989013,\; 0.000553,\; 0.000028), $$

so \(N_1 = 1.989041\) and \(N_2 = 4 - N_1 = 2.010959\).

M step. Mixing weight: \(\pi_1 = N_1/4 = 0.497260\). Means:

$$ \mu_1 = \frac{0(0.999447) + 1(0.989013) + 5(0.000553) + 6(0.000028)}{1.989041} = \frac{0.991942}{1.989041} = 0.498704, $$ $$ \mu_2 = \frac{0(0.000553) + 1(0.010987) + 5(0.999447) + 6(0.999972)}{2.010959} = \frac{11.008058}{2.010959} = 5.474033. $$

Variances, weighting squared deviations from the new means: for component 1 the four squared deviations are \(0.248706, 0.251298, 20.261665, 30.264257\), and the weighted sum is \(0.248568 + 0.248537 + 0.011200 + 0.000833 = 0.509138\), giving \(\sigma_1^2 = 0.509138 / 1.989041 = 0.255972\). For component 2 the weighted sum is \(0.016571 + 0.219927 + 0.224583 + 0.276633 = 0.737714\), giving \(\sigma_2^2 = 0.737714 / 2.010959 = 0.366847\). The log-likelihood rises from \(-9.43616\) at the initialization to \(-5.74290\) after this single iteration, a jump of 3.69 nats, most of the total possible gain. Iterating to convergence gives the symmetric fixed point \(\pi_1 = 0.5\), \(\mu = (0.5, 5.5)\), \(\sigma^2 = (0.25, 0.25)\), log-likelihood \(-5.67575\): each component claims one pair of points, centered between them, with variance equal to the pair's spread. Model selection and cross-validation

Training error is a biased estimate of generalization error, and the bias is not small: the parameters were chosen to make it small, so it systematically flatters the model, and the flattery grows with model capacity. Everything above with a free knob (ridge \(\lambda\), bandwidth \(\tau\), SVM \(C\), kernel width, mixture size \(K\)) needs an estimate of out-of-sample error to set that knob honestly. The clean protocol splits data three ways: training data fits parameters, validation data selects hyperparameters, and a test set is touched once at the end; a test set consulted repeatedly becomes a validation set, and its error estimate inherits selection bias.

When data are too scarce to burn a third of them on validation, \(k\)-fold cross-validation reuses everything: partition the data into \(k\) folds, train on \(k-1\) and evaluate on the held-out fold, rotate, and average the \(k\) errors. The estimate is of the procedure's error when trained on \(\frac{k-1}{k} n\) points, not of the particular fitted model, a distinction that matters when quoting numbers. Small \(k\) (5 or 10, the standard choices) trains on less data and slightly pessimizes the estimate; \(k = n\), leave-one-out, is nearly unbiased for the procedure but the \(n\) refits look expensive. For linear smoothers they are not, which is Problem 8. Two practices worth adopting from the applied literature: the one-standard-error rule (choose the simplest model within one standard error of the best cross-validated score, hedging against noise in the CV estimate itself), and nested cross-validation when both selecting and evaluating, so the selection never sees the evaluation folds. Information criteria (AIC, BIC) trade the recomputation for an analytic penalty on parameter count; Stone (1977) showed AIC and leave-one-out are asymptotically equivalent for likelihood models, so the two families are estimating the same thing by different routes.

Worked problems

Problem 8

Prove that for least squares the leave-one-out residual has the closed form \(y_i - \hat y_i^{(-i)} = \dfrac{y_i - \hat y_i}{1 - h_{ii}}\), where \(h_{ii}\) is the \(i\)-th leverage, so LOOCV costs one fit rather than \(n\). Then apply it to the dataset of Problem 1 and verify against direct refitting.

Solution. Write \(A = X\T X\) and \(h = h_{ii} = x_i\T A^{-1} x_i\). Deleting observation \(i\) changes the normal equations to \((A - x_i x_i\T)\, \theta^{(-i)} = X\T y - x_i y_i\). Invert the rank-one downdate with the Sherman-Morrison identity,

$$ (A - x_i x_i\T)^{-1} = A^{-1} + \frac{A^{-1} x_i x_i\T A^{-1}}{1 - h}, $$

(verified by multiplying both sides by \(A - x_i x_i\T\) and simplifying). The deleted prediction at \(x_i\) is then

$$ \hat y_i^{(-i)} = x_i\T \theta^{(-i)} = \Big( x_i\T A^{-1} + \frac{h\, x_i\T A^{-1}}{1 - h} \Big) (X\T y - x_i y_i) = \frac{1}{1-h}\, x_i\T A^{-1} (X\T y - x_i y_i) = \frac{\hat y_i - h\, y_i}{1 - h}, $$

using \(x_i\T A^{-1} X\T y = \hat y_i\) and \(x_i\T A^{-1} x_i = h\). Subtract from \(y_i\):

$$ y_i - \hat y_i^{(-i)} = \frac{(1-h)\, y_i - \hat y_i + h\, y_i}{1 - h} = \frac{y_i - \hat y_i}{1 - h}. $$

Each ordinary residual is inflated by \(1/(1 - h_{ii})\); high-leverage points, whose removal moves the fit most, get the largest corrections. For Problem 1's data, \(A^{-1} = \frac{1}{20}\begin{pmatrix} 14 & -6 \\ -6 & 4\end{pmatrix}\) gives leverages \(h = (0.7, 0.3, 0.3, 0.7)\) (for example \(h_{11} = \frac{14}{20} = 0.7\) at \(x = 0\); note they sum to \(2 = d\)). The ordinary residuals were \((0.1, 0.2, -0.7, 0.4)\), so the LOO residuals are

$$ \Big( \frac{0.1}{0.3},\; \frac{0.2}{0.7},\; \frac{-0.7}{0.7},\; \frac{0.4}{0.3} \Big) = \Big( \tfrac{1}{3},\; \tfrac{2}{7},\; -1,\; \tfrac{4}{3} \Big), $$

and the LOOCV mean squared error is \(\frac{1}{4}\big( \frac19 + \frac{4}{49} + 1 + \frac{16}{9} \big) = 0.742630\). Refitting the model four times with each point actually deleted reproduces all four LOO residuals and the same 0.742630 to machine precision. Compare the training MSE, \(0.70/4 = 0.175\): the honest estimate is 4.2 times larger, a compact demonstration of how much training error flatters even a two-parameter model on four points.

Problem 9

Derive softmax regression as the GLM of the categorical distribution: put the \(K\)-class categorical into exponential-family form, invert the natural parameters to obtain the softmax function, and show (a) that the resulting parameterization has a one-direction redundancy, and (b) that for \(K = 2\) softmax regression is exactly logistic regression.

Solution. A categorical over \(K\) outcomes with probabilities \(\phi_1, \dots, \phi_K\) (summing to 1) has mass function \(\prod_k \phi_k^{[y = k]}\). Taking logs and using \([y{=}K] = 1 - \sum_{k < K} [y{=}k]\) to eliminate the redundant coordinate,

$$ \log p(y) = \sum_{k=1}^{K-1} [y{=}k] \,\log\frac{\phi_k}{\phi_K} + \log \phi_K, $$

an exponential family with sufficient statistic \(T(y) = ([y{=}1], \dots, [y{=}K{-}1])\), natural parameters \(\eta_k = \log(\phi_k / \phi_K)\), and \(a(\eta) = -\log\phi_K\). To invert: \(\phi_k = \phi_K e^{\eta_k}\), and summing over all classes (with \(\eta_K = 0\) by construction) forces \(\phi_K \big(1 + \sum_{k < K} e^{\eta_k}\big) = 1\), so

$$ \phi_k = \frac{e^{\eta_k}}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} e^{\eta_j}} = \softmax(\eta)_k, $$

with \(a(\eta) = \log \sum_j e^{\eta_j}\), the log-sum-exp, whose gradient is the softmax itself, consistent with \(a' = \) mean. The GLM sets \(\eta_k = \theta_k\T x\) with one weight vector per class. (a) Adding any single vector \(c\) to every \(\theta_k\) shifts all logits by \(c\T x\), and softmax is invariant to a common shift because numerator and denominator both pick up \(e^{c\T x}\): the \(K\)-vector parameterization has exactly this redundancy, which is why one class's weights can be pinned to zero (as the derivation did with \(\eta_K = 0\)) or a regularizer used to select a representative. (b) For \(K = 2\),

$$ \phi_1 = \frac{e^{\theta_1\T x}}{e^{\theta_1\T x} + e^{\theta_2\T x}} = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(\theta_1 - \theta_2)\T x}} = \sigma\big( (\theta_1 - \theta_2)\T x \big): $$

two-class softmax is logistic regression with parameter \(\theta = \theta_1 - \theta_2\), and only the difference is identifiable, which is redundancy (a) again in miniature. The practical corollary reaches into deep learning: the final linear-plus-softmax layer of any classifier is this GLM, its gradient is \((\mathrm{softmax}(\eta) - \mathrm{onehot}(y))\, x\T\) by the shared GLM gradient theorem, and the log-sum-exp \(a(\eta)\) is why numerically stable implementations subtract the max logit before exponentiating.

Logistic regression by Newton's method

The PyTorch version writes the gradient and Hessian explicitly from the derived formulas \(X\T(h - y)\) and \(X\T W X\). The JAX version defines only the scalar loss and lets jax.grad and jax.hessian manufacture both, which is a useful correctness check: if the derivation in the text were wrong, the two would disagree. 

import torch
torch.set_default_dtype(torch.float64)

X = torch.tensor([[1., 0.], [1., 1.], [1., 2.], [1., 3.]])  # (n, d), col 0 = intercept
y = torch.tensor([0., 1., 0., 1.])                          # (n,)

def newton_logistic(X, y, iters=8):
    theta = torch.zeros(X.shape[1])                         # (d,)
    for t in range(iters):
        p = torch.sigmoid(X @ theta)                        # (n,)
        g = X.T @ (p - y)                                   # (d,)   gradient of NLL
        W = p * (1 - p)                                     # (n,)   Newton weights
        H = X.T @ (X * W[:, None])                          # (d, d) Hessian
        theta = theta - torch.linalg.solve(H, g)
        print(t + 1, theta.tolist())
    return theta

theta = newton_logistic(X, y)
# 1 [-1.2, 0.8]                                 <- the hand iteration in Problem 2
# 2 [-1.3559834341742991, 0.9039889561161993]
# 4 [-1.362276393812779,  0.9081842625418527]   converged to ~9 digits by step 4

import jax.numpy as jnp
jax.config.update("jax_enable_x64", True)

y = jnp.array([0., 1., 0., 1.])                             # (n,)

def nll(theta):
    eta = X @ theta                                         # (n,)
    return jnp.sum(jnp.logaddexp(0., eta) - y * eta)        # stable log(1 + e^eta)

@jax.jit
def newton_step(theta):
    g = jax.grad(nll)(theta)                                # (d,)   autodiff gradient
    H = jax.hessian(nll)(theta)                             # (d, d) autodiff Hessian
    return theta - jnp.linalg.solve(H, g)

theta = jnp.zeros(2)
for t in range(8):
    theta = newton_step(theta)
    print(t + 1, theta)

# 4 [-1.36227639  0.90818426]   identical to the explicit PyTorch iterates

One fitting routine for every canonical GLM

The point of this block is the shared-gradient theorem made executable. In PyTorch the fitter takes the mean function \(a'\) and variance function \(a''\) as arguments; in JAX it takes only the cumulant function \(a\) itself and differentiates it, so a one-line lambda defines a whole regression family. Logistic, Poisson, and Gaussian members run through the same loop; the Gaussian one converges in a single step because its quadratic model is exact. 

def fit_glm(X, y, mean_fn, var_fn, iters=25):
    """Newton/IRLS for any canonical-link GLM.
    mean_fn = a', var_fn = a'', both evaluated at eta = X @ theta."""
    theta = torch.zeros(X.shape[1])
    for _ in range(iters):
        eta = X @ theta                                     # (n,)
        mu, w = mean_fn(eta), var_fn(eta)                   # (n,), (n,)
        g = X.T @ (mu - y)                                  # same form for every GLM
        H = X.T @ (X * w[:, None])
        theta = theta - torch.linalg.solve(H, g)
    return theta

# three members of the family, one routine
th_logit = fit_glm(X, y, torch.sigmoid,
                   lambda e: torch.sigmoid(e) * (1 - torch.sigmoid(e)))
Xp = torch.tensor([[1.], [2.], [3.]]); yp = torch.tensor([1., 3., 5.])
th_pois = fit_glm(Xp, yp, torch.exp, torch.exp, iters=40)
yl = torch.tensor([1., 2., 2., 4.])
th_ols = fit_glm(X, yl, lambda e: e, torch.ones_like, iters=1)  # 1 step = exact
print(th_logit.tolist(), th_pois.tolist(), th_ols.tolist())
# [-1.3622763938, 0.9081842626]  [0.5267980069]  [0.9, 0.9]
# Poisson matches Problem 3's converged value; OLS matches Problem 1.

# The NLL of a canonical GLM is sum_i A(theta.x_i) - y_i theta.x_i, so a
# single cumulant function A defines the model; autodiff supplies the mean
# (A') inside the gradient and the variance weights (A'') inside the Hessian.
def glm_nll(A):
    def loss(theta, X, y):
        eta = X @ theta
        return jnp.sum(jax.vmap(A)(eta) - y * eta)
    return loss

logistic_loss = glm_nll(lambda e: jnp.logaddexp(0., e))     # A(eta) = log(1+e^eta)
poisson_loss  = glm_nll(jnp.exp)                            # A(eta) = e^eta
gauss_loss    = glm_nll(lambda e: 0.5 * e**2)               # A(eta) = eta^2 / 2

def fit_glm(loss, X, y, iters=25):
    theta = jnp.zeros(X.shape[1])
    for _ in range(iters):
        g = jax.grad(loss)(theta, X, y)
        H = jax.hessian(loss)(theta, X, y)
        theta = theta - jnp.linalg.solve(H, g)
    return theta

Xp = jnp.array([[1.], [2.], [3.]]); yp = jnp.array([1., 3., 5.])
print(fit_glm(poisson_loss, Xp, yp))                        # [0.52679801]
print(fit_glm(logistic_loss, X, y, iters=10))               # [-1.36227639  0.90818426]
yl = jnp.array([1., 2., 2., 4.])
print(fit_glm(gauss_loss, X, yl, iters=1))                  # [0.9 0.9] = OLS

EM for a two-component Gaussian mixture

Both versions compute responsibilities in log space through a softmax over the component axis, which is the numerically safe form of the E-step ratio. The PyTorch version is a plain loop; the JAX version phrases the iteration as lax.scan, the idiomatic JAX shape for any fixed-point iteration, and returns the whole trajectory of responsibilities for free. 

x = torch.tensor([0., 1., 5., 6.])                          # (n,)

def em_step(x, pi1, mu, var):
    # E step: log posterior weights, softmax over the component axis
    logw = torch.stack([
        torch.log(pi1) - 0.5 * torch.log(var[0]) - (x - mu[0])**2 / (2 * var[0]),
        torch.log(1 - pi1) - 0.5 * torch.log(var[1]) - (x - mu[1])**2 / (2 * var[1])])
    r = torch.softmax(logw, dim=0)                          # (2, n) responsibilities
    # M step: weighted MLE formulas from the derivation
    n_k = r.sum(dim=1)                                      # (2,) effective counts
    mu_new = (r * x).sum(dim=1) / n_k                       # (2,)
    var_new = (r * (x[None, :] - mu_new[:, None])**2).sum(dim=1) / n_k
    return n_k[0] / len(x), mu_new, var_new, r[0]

pi1, mu, var = torch.tensor(0.5), torch.tensor([1., 4.]), torch.tensor([1., 1.])
for t in range(100):
    pi1, mu, var, r = em_step(x, pi1, mu, var)
    if t == 0:
        print("r:", r.tolist())   # [0.99945, 0.98901, 0.00055, 0.00003]
        print(pi1.item(), mu.tolist(), var.tolist())
        # 0.49726 [0.49870, 5.47403] [0.25597, 0.36684]   <- Problem 7's M step
print(pi1.item(), mu.tolist(), var.tolist())
# 0.5 [0.5, 5.5] [0.25, 0.25]   the converged fixed point

x = jnp.array([0., 1., 5., 6.])                             # (n,)

def em_step(params, _):
    pi1, mu, var = params
    logw = jnp.stack([
        jnp.log(pi1) - 0.5 * jnp.log(var[0]) - (x - mu[0])**2 / (2 * var[0]),
        jnp.log(1 - pi1) - 0.5 * jnp.log(var[1]) - (x - mu[1])**2 / (2 * var[1])])
    r = jax.nn.softmax(logw, axis=0)                        # (2, n) responsibilities
    n_k = r.sum(axis=1)                                     # (2,)
    mu_new = (r * x).sum(axis=1) / n_k
    var_new = (r * (x[None, :] - mu_new[:, None])**2).sum(axis=1) / n_k
    return (n_k[0] / x.shape[0], mu_new, var_new), r[0]

init = (jnp.array(0.5), jnp.array([1., 4.]), jnp.array([1., 1.]))
(pi1, mu, var), resp = jax.lax.scan(em_step, init, None, length=100)
print(resp[0])          # [0.99945 0.98901 0.00055 0.00003]  first E step

The SVM dual on the toy margin problem

The PyTorch version is a miniature SMO in the style of Platt (1998): pick a pair of multipliers, optimize the dual analytically along the line that keeps \(\sum_i \alpha_i y_i\) fixed, clip to the box, repeat. The JAX version does projected gradient ascent, projecting the gradient onto the equality constraint and clipping at \(\alpha \ge 0\); adequate for a three-point toy, and a QP or SMO solver is the right tool beyond that. Both recover the hand solution of Problem 6. 

Xs = torch.tensor([[0., 0.], [2., 2.], [4., 0.]])
ys = torch.tensor([-1., 1., 1.])
K = Xs @ Xs.T                                               # linear-kernel Gram (n, n)

def smo(K, y, C=1e6, sweeps=50):
    n = len(y); alpha = torch.zeros(n); b = torch.tensor(0.)
    for _ in range(sweeps):
        for i in range(n):
            for j in range(n):
                if i == j: continue
                f = (alpha * y) @ K + b                     # (n,) decision values
                Ei, Ej = f[i] - y[i], f[j] - y[j]
                eta = K[i, i] + K[j, j] - 2 * K[i, j]       # curvature of the pair
                if eta <= 1e-12: continue
                s = y[i] * y[j]                             # box ends for the pair
                L = max(0., (alpha[j] - alpha[i]).item()) if s < 0 else \
                    max(0., (alpha[i] + alpha[j] - C).item())
                Hb = min(C, C + (alpha[j] - alpha[i]).item()) if s < 0 else \
                     min(C, (alpha[i] + alpha[j]).item())
                if L >= Hb: continue
                aj = torch.clamp(alpha[j] + y[j] * (Ei - Ej) / eta, L, Hb)
                alpha[i] = alpha[i] + s * (alpha[j] - aj)   # keeps sum a_i y_i = 0
                alpha[j] = aj
                sv = alpha > 1e-9                       # recover b from any SV
                b = (y[sv] - ((alpha * y) @ K)[sv]).mean()
    return alpha, b

alpha, b = smo(K, ys)
w = ((alpha * ys)[:, None] * Xs).sum(dim=0)
print(alpha.tolist(), w.tolist(), b.item())
# [0.25, 0.25, 0.0] [0.5, 0.5] -1.0    <- exactly Problem 6's hand solution

Xs = jnp.array([[0., 0.], [2., 2.], [4., 0.]])
ys = jnp.array([-1., 1., 1.])
K = Xs @ Xs.T                                               # (n, n)
Q = (ys[:, None] * ys[None, :]) * K                         # y_i y_j K_ij

def dual(alpha):                                            # maximize this
    return alpha.sum() - 0.5 * alpha @ Q @ alpha

@jax.jit
def step(alpha, lr=0.01):
    d = jax.grad(dual)(alpha)
    d = d - (d @ ys) / (ys @ ys) * ys       # project onto sum alpha_i y_i = 0
    return jnp.clip(alpha + lr * d, 0.0)    # then clip at the box alpha >= 0

alpha = jnp.zeros(3)
for t in range(4000):
    alpha = step(alpha)
w = ((alpha * ys)[:, None] * Xs).sum(axis=0)
print(alpha, alpha @ ys, dual(alpha), w)
# [0.25 0.25 0.]  constraint ~ -6e-16,  dual = 0.25 = ||w||^2 / 2,  w = [0.5 0.5]
# dual optimum equals the primal objective: strong duality, verified numerically

How it is done in practice

The gap between these derivations and a production fit is mostly numerical linear algebra and scale. No serious library solves the normal equations by forming \((X\T X)^{-1}\): squaring \(X\) squares its condition number, so numpy.linalg.lstsq and scikit-learn's LinearRegression go through SVD or QR on \(X\) itself, and ridge solvers use Cholesky on the regularized system, whose \(\lambda I\) shift restores conditioning. For logistic regression and GLMs, scikit-learn's LogisticRegression defaults to L-BFGS rather than full Newton, because storing and factoring a \(d \times d\) Hessian is wasteful when \(d\) is tens of thousands; the newton-cholesky solver added in scikit-learn 1.2 is the classical IRLS from this page and is the fast choice when \(n \gg d\). Its counterpart in the statistics world, statsmodels' GLM.fit(), has run Nelder-Wedderburn IRLS essentially unchanged since the formulation appeared in 1972, and Quantco's glum exists because insurers fit Poisson and Tweedie GLMs with millions of rows and thousands of one-hot columns, where exploiting sparsity in \(X\T W X\) assembly dominates the runtime.

Linear SVMs and logistic regression at web scale abandoned Newton entirely. LIBLINEAR (Fan et al., 2008) runs coordinate descent on the dual and is why "liblinear" appears as a scikit-learn solver name; Google's ad-click system described by McMahan et al. (2013) trained per-coordinate FTRL-regularized logistic regression on billions of examples, online, one example at a time, precisely because the GLM gradient "prediction minus label times features" is computable per impression. Kernel SVMs pay \(O(n^2)\) memory for the Gram matrix and roughly cubic time, which caps exact solvers like LIBSVM's SMO in the hundreds of thousands of points; past that, practice switches to explicit approximate feature maps, most commonly random Fourier features (Rahimi and Recht, 2007), which turn a kernel problem back into a linear one on \(m \ll n\) sampled features. The arithmetic itself is not the bottleneck it once was: on an NVIDIA H100 80GB in this repository's benchmark run, a 4096-dimensional fp32 matrix multiply sustains 51.3 TFLOP/s (384.0 with TF32 enabled), so forming \(X\T X\) for \(d\) in the thousands is milliseconds; the cost centers of production fitting are data movement, one-hot sparsity handling, and cross-validation loops over hyperparameters, not the solve.

The current research frontier

The most consequential development is that the classical bias-variance picture, derived exactly above, turned out to be incomplete in the overparameterized regime. Belkin, Hsu, Ma, and Mandal (2019) documented double descent: as model capacity passes the interpolation threshold where training error hits zero, test error spikes and then descends again, so the classical U-shaped curve is only the left half of the picture. Nakkiran and collaborators at Harvard and OpenAI showed the same phenomenon in deep networks along model size, data size, and training time. The theory caught up on the linear side: Bartlett, Long, Lugosi, and Tsigler (2020) gave conditions for benign overfitting, where minimum-norm least squares interpolates noisy data yet generalizes, with the covariance spectrum deciding benignity; Hastie, Montanari, Rosset, and Tibshirani (2022) computed exact asymptotics for ridgeless interpolation under proportional scaling. None of this repeals the bias-variance identity, which is an algebraic fact; it shows that capacity is not a one-dimensional knob, and that the implicit regularization of the minimum-norm solution is a variance-control mechanism the classical accounting did not price in.

Kernels returned to the frontier from a different direction. Jacot, Gabriel, and Hongler at EPFL proved that an infinitely wide network trained by gradient descent evolves as kernel regression under the neural tangent kernel, and the parallel line at Google Brain (Lee et al.) established the correspondence between wide networks and Gaussian processes; the two results together made kernel theory the sharpest available lens on early training dynamics, while the finite-width corrections that separate real networks from their kernel limits remain actively contested. On the applied-statistics side, conformal prediction, developed by Vovk and coworkers and brought to the machine-learning mainstream by groups at Berkeley and elsewhere (Angelopoulos and Bates's 2021 tutorial is the standard entry point), wraps any of the predictors on this page with finite-sample, distribution-free coverage guarantees, and has become the default answer to "what does this model not know" in deployed pipelines. And the GLM lineage itself keeps absorbing hardware-era scale: the softmax layer of every large language model is the categorical GLM of Problem 9, trained by the same prediction-minus-target gradient, which makes the exponential-family calculus on this page a live dependency of systems six orders of magnitude larger than anything McCullagh and Nelder contemplated.

Common misconceptions

"Least squares is solved by computing \((X\T X)^{-1} X\T y\)." That formula is the derivation, not the algorithm. Forming \(X\T X\) squares the condition number, so a marginally ill-conditioned design becomes numerically singular; production code factors \(X\) directly by QR or SVD, and regularized problems go through Cholesky on the shifted system. The normal equations are how to understand the solution, not how to compute it.

"The logistic-regression MLE always exists; the optimizer just has to find it." On linearly separable data the likelihood increases forever along the ray of any separating hyperplane's parameter vector, so there is no maximizer; the optimizer diverges with ever-larger weights and saturating probabilities. This is common in practice with many features and few examples. Regularization restores a unique finite optimum, which is one reason scikit-learn regularizes by default.

"Naive Bayes is broken because its independence assumption is false." The assumption is false and the predicted probabilities are indeed poorly calibrated (typically overconfident, since correlated evidence is double-counted). But classification only needs the argmax, and the argmax survives large distortions of the class scores. Ng and Jordan (2001) sharpened this: the generative model reaches its asymptotic error with far fewer samples, so Naive Bayes can beat logistic regression on small data even while modeling the data wrong.

"The bias-variance tradeoff implies bigger models must generalize worse." The decomposition is an identity about one predictor at one point; it says nothing about how bias and variance move as capacity grows. Past the interpolation threshold, minimum-norm fitting can drive variance down again, producing the second descent documented by Belkin et al. and, in linear regression, the benign-overfitting conditions of Bartlett et al. The identity survives; the folklore monotone U-curve does not.

"Any reasonable similarity function can be used as a kernel." Kernel algorithms silently assume the Gram matrix is positive semidefinite; the dual is otherwise unbounded or non-convex. Plausible-looking similarities fail this: the hyperbolic-tangent "sigmoid kernel" is not PSD for general parameter settings, and a metric's negation is not automatically a kernel. Validity means symmetric plus PSD Gram matrices, checked through the closure rules or Mercer's condition, not through intuition about similarity.

"Support vectors are the points on the margin." The implication runs the other way: every support vector (\(\alpha_i > 0\)) lies on the margin (hard-margin case), but a point can sit exactly on the margin with \(\alpha_i = 0\), contributing nothing to \(w\), as \(x_3\) does in Problem 6. Complementary slackness permits either \(\alpha_i = 0\) or an active constraint or both; geometry alone does not identify the support set.

"EM maximizes the likelihood." EM monotonically increases the likelihood and converges, under Wu's (1983) conditions, to a stationary point, which may be a poor local maximum or a saddle; for Gaussian mixtures the global supremum is actually infinite, at degenerate solutions with a zero-variance component glued to one data point. Every serious implementation runs multiple initializations and floors or regularizes the variances, and reports the best local optimum found, not "the" MLE.

Key takeaway: supervised learning is one construction examined from different angles. Choose an exponential-family model of the output, make its natural parameter linear in the features, and maximum likelihood hands back a convex problem whose gradient is always prediction minus target times input; least squares, logistic, Poisson, and softmax regression are that sentence instantiated four times, and Newton's method on any of them is reweighted least squares. Generative models buy data efficiency with stronger assumptions and pay for it asymptotically; regularizers are priors with the logarithm taken; the bias-variance identity prices every one of these choices, and cross-validation estimates the price when the theory's unknowns are unavailable. Kernels extend the whole apparatus to infinite feature spaces through the representer theorem, with the SVM dual showing how convex duality turns geometry into sparse inner products, and EM extends it to latent variables by riding Jensen's inequality up a lower bound that touches the likelihood at every E step. Every derivation here was checked by hand and then by machine, in PyTorch and JAX, to the digit; that habit, deriving and then verifying, is the actual content of the subject.
