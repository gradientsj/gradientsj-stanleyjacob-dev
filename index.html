Reliability engineering

Built to run, not just to ship.

I've run Kubernetes (GKE) platforms serving real-time models in production, provisioning GPU node pools and autoscaling deployments with Terraform and Helm so dozens of engineers could ship rollouts self-service instead of waiting days. The same instinct shows up everywhere I work: make the safe path the easy path, then automate the toil away.

That means CI/CD pipelines that gate every deploy on accuracy, latency, and throughput checks with automated rollback, so a bad release reverts cleanly instead of paging someone. It means an observability stack with tracing and dashboards for p50/p99 latency, queue depth, and utilization, backed by SLOs and multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerting that cuts time to detection rather than drowning the team in noise.

And it means owning systems when they break: on-call rotations, runbooks for the real failure modes, blameless post-mortems that stop repeat incidents, scheduled synthetic health checks that catch outages before users do, and destructive testing to prove the platform holds up before it has to.

Machine learning

Models, end to end.

My instinct is to break a problem down to its math and rebuild it in software, and a lot of the time that turns into machine learning. I've built models across the spectrum, from feedforward and convolutional networks through recurrent models up to large transformers, trained on audio and video as much as on images and text, with multimodal pipelines, mixed precision, and distributed training so experiments finish in hours, not days.

I care just as much about the half people skip: getting a trained model to actually serve. I deploy behind Triton and optimize with TensorRT using operator fusion, quantization, and dynamic shapes to hit latency targets without giving up accuracy, writing the performance-critical C++ and compiler tooling that keeps results correct as the graph changes underneath.

The foundation goes back to electrical engineering at UT Austin, signal processing and embedded systems, and computer science at Stanford, where I focused on AI, generative models, reinforcement learning, and compilers. I like understanding a system from the silicon up to the model on top.
