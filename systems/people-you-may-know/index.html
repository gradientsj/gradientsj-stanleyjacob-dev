Design People You May Know

Systems design · Machine learning systems · Dec 2025

People You May Know is the module on LinkedIn or Facebook that suggests connections: former colleagues, classmates, the person you met at a conference last week. It looks like a sidebar widget, and it is one of the highest-leverage systems either company runs, because network growth compounds. Each accepted connection makes the product stickier for both people, enriches the graph that powers every other recommendation, and raises the chance that the next session has something worth seeing. For a new user especially, the first few suggestions decide whether the product ever becomes useful at all, which is why the metric conversation later in this design ends at retention rather than clicks.

Interviewers like the question because it is link prediction on a giant graph: given the network as it exists today, predict which currently missing edges will form, where an edge is a connection between two people. That framing brings a family of graph-specific techniques that most recommender questions never touch, and it also brings sharp scaling questions, because the natural candidate generator produces a million raw candidates for a well-connected user, and sharp privacy questions, because suggesting the wrong person is one of the few recommendation failures that can do real harm.

Scope and requirements

The system produces a ranked list of suggested connections per user, surfaced in a feed module and a dedicated page, refreshed at least daily, with a target of a few hundred milliseconds to render from a precomputed store. Assume a graph of several hundred million users, an average of a few hundred connections each but a heavy tail reaching tens of thousands, and tens of millions of daily active users pulling suggestions. In scope: candidate generation, scoring, the batch-versus-online architecture, and the privacy and fairness machinery, which in this design are requirements rather than enhancements. Out of scope: the invitation flow itself and the messaging that follows.

Framing the problem as a machine learning task

The task: for a user u and a candidate v with no current edge, predict the probability that, if v is suggested to u, an invitation is sent and accepted. Requiring acceptance in the target matters, because predicting sends alone would teach the system to exploit whatever makes people click, while an accept needs both sides to recognize the relationship, making it the natural guard against spammy over-suggestion. Like most large recommenders this runs as a funnel, with cheap graph-structural candidate generation followed by a learned ranker, since scoring all possible pairs is out of the question: several hundred million users squared is on the order of 1017 pairs, and the graph structure itself is what makes the problem tractable.

Candidate generation

Triadic closure dominates: the empirical tendency that two people who share friends are likely to connect, which makes friends-of-friends the candidate source that carries the product. The size math is worth doing aloud. A user with 1,000 connections whose connections each have 1,000 of their own generates up to a million friend-of-friend paths; after collapsing duplicates (the same person reached through many mutuals, which is itself the signal), removing existing connections, and applying hard filters, perhaps 30,000 unique candidates survive for a well-connected user, and far fewer for most. The expansion is two hops of an adjacency-list lookup against the graph store, cheap enough to run in bulk daily.

Cohort sources fill in what the graph cannot see yet: same school with overlapping years, same employer with overlapping tenure, and same city cohorts generate candidates for exactly the people friends-of-friends fails, the new user with three connections. Imported address-book contacts are the strongest source of all when present, since a phone number in someone's contacts is near-proof of acquaintance, and they are also the source demanding the most care: imports must be consented, stored hashed, deletable on request, and never used to reveal that one person has another's contact information. Finally, graph embeddings provide a complementary source that finds non-obvious links. In the node2vec style, the system takes many short random walks through the graph, so that each walk visits a sequence of people the way a sentence visits words, and trains word-embedding machinery over those sequences, leaving each person with a vector placed near the people their walks kept encountering. Nearest neighbors in that space surface candidates in the same dense community even without direct mutuals today, the colleague-of-colleagues a pure friends-of-friends expansion misses.

Features and scoring

The graph features that score candidates are classical and strong. Common-neighbor count is the baseline: more mutuals, more likely. Adamic-Adar sharpens it by weighting each mutual inversely by the logarithm of their own connection count, on the reasoning that a shared friend who connects 50 people vouches for a real social circle while a shared "friend" who connects 50,000 vouches for almost nothing. Worked through: a candidate sharing two mutuals, one with 50 connections and one with 50,000, scores 1/ln(50) + 1/ln(50,000), about 0.256 + 0.092 = 0.348, while a different candidate with two mutuals of 5,000 connections each scores 2/ln(5,000), about 0.235, so the first candidate wins despite the tie in raw mutual count, and that asymmetry tracks how people actually meet. Preferential attachment, the product of the two users' degrees, captures the tendency of highly connected people to keep connecting, useful as a weak prior and dangerous as a ranking signal on its own. On top of the structural features sit profile overlap (shared employer, school, industry, location, with overlap in years), interaction recency (profile views, search appearances, being in the same event or group recently), and the embedding similarity from the random-walk vectors.

The ranker is a GBDT or compact neural model trained on historical invite outcomes: a suggestion that led to a sent-and-accepted invitation is a positive, sent-and-ignored is a negative, and shown-but-never-sent is a weaker negative. The censoring problem deserves naming: pairs the old system never showed produce no labels at all, so the model learns the value of candidates the previous policy liked, and candidates outside that distribution stay dark. A small exploration slice, mixing some lower-scored and source-diverse candidates into live suggestions, is the working answer, with the labeled exploration traffic feeding evaluation as well.

Offline evaluation

The split is temporal: train on edges formed before a cutoff, evaluate on edges formed in the following weeks, never randomly, since a random split leaks the future shape of the graph into training. Retrieval is measured by recall@K against newly formed edges: if a user formed 4 new connections in the evaluation window and the candidate stage had ranked 3 of those people in its top 100 for that user, recall@100 is 0.75 for that user, averaged across users to judge whether the funnel's first stage even contains the right people. The ranker is measured by AUC on the invite-accept labels, with one caution specific to this domain: easy negatives from random pairs inflate AUC into the high nineties and mean nothing, so evaluation negatives are drawn from real shown-but-not-accepted suggestions, which keeps the metric anchored to the decision the system actually makes.

The serving architecture: batch with patches

The architecture question is whether to assemble suggestions at request time or precompute them, and the load math settles it. Request-time assembly means a two-hop expansion plus feature fetch plus scoring of tens of thousands of candidates inside an interactive budget, for tens of millions of daily users, repeatedly; precompute means a daily batch job walks the graph, scores candidates, and writes each user's top 100 suggestions into a key-value store that serves reads in single-digit milliseconds. Batch wins on cost and predictability and loses on freshness, and freshness is exactly where it hurts most, since the moments when suggestions matter, right after a signup, a new connection, or a job change, are the moments a daily batch misses. The production answer is precompute with event-driven patches: connection events, profile changes, and contact imports flow through a stream, and a patch worker recomputes just the affected user's list (and, more cheaply, injects the most obvious new candidates) within minutes. The new-user moment is handled specially, with a synchronous lightweight path at signup that builds a first list from cohorts and any imported contacts alone, because day-one suggestions decide whether there is a day seven, and an empty module at signup is the most expensive blank space in the product.

The daily pipeline. Friends-of-friends expansion over the graph store dominates candidate volume, cohort and consent-gated contact sources fill its gaps, policy filters apply blocks and opt-outs before anything is scored, and the ranked top 100 per user lands in a store the client reads in milliseconds.

Triadic closure and Adamic-Adar, pictured

One small picture carries the two core graph intuitions. You share two mutuals with a candidate, but the mutuals are not equal: one keeps a tight circle of 50, the other connects to 50,000 people. Triadic closure says the shared neighbors make the new edge likely at all; Adamic-Adar says the quiet mutual is the one whose vouch counts.

Triadic closure makes Maya a candidate because she shares two mutuals with you. Adamic-Adar weights each mutual by 1 over the log of their own connection count, so Asha's vouch (0.26) carries nearly three times Sam's (0.09), and the pair sums to 0.35.

Privacy, fairness, and abuse

Privacy constraints here are hard requirements that shape the architecture, not policies sprinkled on top. Blocks suppress suggestions in both directions, unconditionally and forever. A "do not suggest me" setting removes a person from everyone else's candidates, and repeated dismissals of a particular suggestion suppress it durably, since showing someone the same unwanted face weekly is its own harm. The system must also avoid surfacing sensitive inferred relationships: two people whose only commonality is having visited the same clinic, attended the same support group, or appeared in the same patient's address book must not be introduced to each other, and the widely reported incidents in the press around exactly these cases are the reason candidate sources are audited individually rather than trusted in aggregate. Contact-import data is the recurring root cause in those stories, which is why its consent, storage, and deletion story was stated up front and why its use is confined to direct suggestion, never to inferring third-party relationships.

Fairness is a feedback problem. Triadic closure as a generator means the well-connected get the best suggestions and grow faster, while a low-degree user generates few candidates and receives few, so the rich get richer by construction. Countermeasures are deliberate: cohort and embedding sources weighted up for low-degree users, monitoring of suggestion volume and accept rates across degree deciles, and treating the new-user path as a first-class surface rather than an edge case. Abuse runs the other direction, with invite-spam farms mass-connecting to harvest contact graphs, and the defenses are rate limits on invitations, accept-rate floors below which a sender's reach is throttled, and the ranker itself learning to score outreach from flagged patterns down.

Online evaluation and operations

Online, the launch metrics form a deliberate chain: invite send rate measures whether suggestions are compelling, accept rate measures whether they were right, and week-4 retention of new users measures whether the module did its real job. Send rate alone is gameable by suggesting famous or familiar faces people click but who never accept, so accept rate is the guardrail that keeps the system from drifting spammy, and the new-user retention readout, slow as it is, is the one the product team should care about most. A/B tests on this system need network-effect care, since suggesting v to u in treatment changes v's experience in control; user-level randomization with cluster-level checks is the usual compromise. Operationally, the daily batch plus patch stream is monitored for staleness (age of each user's list), the candidate sources are tracked for share-of-accepts to catch a source rotting quietly, the model retrains weekly on fresh invite outcomes with temporal validation, and privacy filters are tested continuously with synthetic blocked pairs asserting that no such pair ever reaches a suggestion list, because that is the one failure mode where a single served result is one too many.

Follow-up questions

  • Why is accept rate the guardrail rather than send rate? Sends measure curiosity and can be inflated by suggesting recognizable people who never accept; an accept requires both sides to validate the relationship, so it anchors the system against spammy over-suggestion.
  • Why precompute suggestions instead of ranking at request time? Two-hop expansion plus scoring tens of thousands of candidates per request is wasteful for lists that change slowly. Daily precompute with event-driven patches gives predictable load, and the patches cover the moments freshness actually matters.
  • What does Adamic-Adar add over counting mutuals? It weights each mutual by 1 over the log of their own degree, so two candidates with two mutuals each can score 0.348 versus 0.235 depending on whether the mutuals are close friends or mega-connectors, separating ties that raw counts cannot.
  • How do you handle the not-sent censoring problem? Acknowledge that labels only exist for shown pairs, keep a small exploration slice of lower-scored and source-diverse candidates in live traffic, and use that slice for the least biased evaluation of model changes.
  • What is special about the new-user path? Friends-of-friends has nothing to expand, so cohorts and consented contacts carry day one, assembled synchronously at signup. Day-one suggestion quality is among the strongest levers on week-4 retention, which justifies the special path.
  • How do graph embeddings complement the heuristics? Random-walk embeddings place people near their community even without current mutuals, surfacing candidates two-plus hops out that Adamic-Adar never sees, and the embedding similarity doubles as a ranking feature on every candidate.
