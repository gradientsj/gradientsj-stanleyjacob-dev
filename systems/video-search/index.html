Design a video search system

Systems design · Machine learning systems · Nov 2025

Video search is the box at the top of YouTube: a user types "how to replace a bike chain" and expects, within a heartbeat, a ranked page of videos that actually show the repair. The query is text and the document is a moving picture with a soundtrack, and bridging that gap is the whole problem. Interviewers like the question because it forces a candidate to combine two systems that are usually taught separately, classical text retrieval and learned content understanding, and then to rank their merged output under a latency budget while the corpus grows by hours of video every second.

The framing worth stating up front is that there are two complementary signal sources and the design needs both. Text metadata, meaning the title, description, tags, and uploader-provided captions, is cheap to index and often accurate, but metadata lies: uploaders keyword-stuff, mislabel, and clickbait. Content understanding, meaning signals computed from the frames and the audio themselves, cannot lie about what is in the video, but it is expensive to compute and fuzzier to match against a text query. The hard parts of the design are making these two sources cooperate, correcting the biases in click-based training data, and serving fresh results for queries about events that happened twenty minutes ago.

Scope and requirements

The product takes a text query and returns a ranked list of videos from a corpus of, say, one billion videos with 500 hours uploaded per minute. Traffic is on the order of tens of thousands of queries per second at peak, and the backend budget for a search result page is around 200 milliseconds at the 95th percentile, of which the retrieval and ranking core gets perhaps half. Freshness is a product requirement, not a nicety, because a meaningful slice of queries concern breaking events where the best video is minutes old. In scope are query understanding, retrieval, and ranking; out of scope here are personalization beyond coarse signals such as language and region, video recommendation (a related but distinct system that starts from a user rather than a query), and ads.

Framing the problem as a machine learning task

The task decomposes into retrieval plus ranking. Retrieval takes the query and narrows a billion videos to a few hundred candidates using cheap, scalable structures, and ranking applies a learned model with richer features to order those candidates carefully. Trying to score every video with the ranker is computationally absurd, and trying to make retrieval perfect is unnecessary, since retrieval only needs to get good candidates into the pool, which is the funnel logic that organizes most large-scale search and recommendation systems.

Within retrieval, the input is the (possibly rewritten) query text and the output is a candidate set; within ranking, the input is a query-video pair with features and the output is a relevance score. An alternative framing, treating search as pure text matching over metadata, is the right baseline and a wrong destination, because it inherits every metadata lie; another alternative, scoring video content against the query with a heavy multimodal model at query time, is years of compute away from a 100 ms budget. The production answer precomputes content understanding at upload time and keeps query-time work light.

Data and labels

Training labels come almost entirely from behavior logs. A click on a result is weak evidence of relevance, a click followed by substantial watch time is stronger, and a click followed by an immediate bounce back to results is evidence against, so the practical label is a graded one built from click plus dwell. Human-rated query-video pairs, judged on written guidelines, are far cleaner and far scarcer, and they anchor evaluation and calibrate the click-derived labels.

The dominant noise in click data is position bias: top-ranked results get clicked more simply for being on top, so naively trained models learn to imitate the old ranker's ordering rather than relevance. Two mitigations are standard and worth explaining plainly. The first is randomization, occasionally swapping adjacent results for a tiny traffic slice, which produces data where position and relevance are untangled and the true effect of position can be measured. The second is bias-corrected training, where each click is down-weighted by the estimated probability that its position alone would have earned the click, so a click at rank one counts for less than a click at rank eight. Saying "we train on clicks" without one of these is a design with a known systematic error in its labels.

Features and representation

The text side is represented with an inverted index, the classic structure that maps each term to the list of videos whose metadata contains it, scored with BM25, a battle-tested formula that rewards documents containing rare query terms often, while damping repetition and normalizing for document length. ASR, automatic speech recognition, extends the text side powerfully: transcribing the audio track turns the spoken content into text that is indexed like any other field, so a video that never says "bike chain" in its title but demonstrates the repair aloud becomes findable. Transcripts carry recognition errors, especially on accents, music, and jargon, so transcript matches are a weaker-weighted field rather than being trusted like titles, and the per-word confidence the recognizer emits can gate which words are indexed at all.

The visual side is represented with embeddings, learned vectors where distance tracks semantic similarity. Frames are sampled from each video, one per few seconds plus shot boundaries, and pushed through an image encoder into vectors that are pooled into a single video-level vector. Crucially, the video vectors and query-text vectors must live in the same space, which is what CLIP-style training provides: a text encoder and an image encoder are trained jointly on millions of (image, text) pairs so that matching pairs land close together, and afterward a text query can be encoded and compared directly against video embeddings. Searching those vectors at scale uses an ANN index, approximate nearest neighbor search, which finds close vectors while examining only a tiny fraction of the corpus, trading a few points of recall for orders of magnitude in speed. The ranking stage adds the remaining feature families: text relevance scores from each index, embedding similarity, freshness, engagement statistics (historical click and watch rates for the video), channel quality, and query-independent quality signals such as resolution and stability, with the engagement features served from a feature store so training and serving read identical values.

Model architecture

Candidate generation is hybrid: the text index and the vector index are queried in parallel, each contributing its top few hundred, and the union is deduplicated into roughly 500 candidates. The two retrievers fail differently, which is the point: text retrieval nails exact names and rare terms but misses paraphrase, while embedding retrieval handles "fix a slipped gear thingy" but blurs precise entities, and the union covers both failure modes cheaply.

The ranker is a learned model over query-video feature vectors. A gradient-boosted decision tree ensemble, GBDT, which builds many small decision trees where each tree corrects the errors of the ones before it, is the strong baseline: it handles heterogeneous tabular features gracefully and trains fast enough for daily refreshes. The production-grade step up is a neural ranker that consumes the same features plus raw text and embedding inputs, often with multiple output heads predicting click, watch time, and satisfaction together, with the heads blended into one ordering score. I would tell the interviewer the GBDT ships first, because its feature attribution makes failure diagnosis tractable while the data pipelines mature, and the neural ranker follows once randomization and bias correction make the labels trustworthy enough to reward the extra capacity.

Offline evaluation

The two stages get different metrics. Candidate generation is judged by recall@K: across a set of queries with known relevant videos, the fraction of those relevant videos that survive into the top K candidates, since anything retrieval drops is gone forever. Ranking is judged by nDCG, normalized discounted cumulative gain, which rewards placing the most relevant items highest. A worked example keeps it concrete. Suppose a query has five judged results with graded relevance 3, 2, 3, 0, 1 in the order the ranker returned them, and each position's gain is discounted by 1 over log2 of (position + 1), giving discounts of 1.0, 0.631, 0.5, 0.431, and 0.387. The DCG is 2(1.0) + 3(0.631) + 0(0.5) + 3(0.431) + 1(0.387) ≈ 5.57 for a returned order of 2, 3, 0, 3, 1, while the ideal order 3, 3, 2, 1, 0 scores ≈ 6.32, so nDCG is 5.57 / 6.32 ≈ 0.88, and the gap to 1.0 is exactly the cost of ranking one excellent result third instead of first.

Split discipline matters more in search than almost anywhere because engagement features leak the future: if Tuesday's click counts inform a model evaluated on Monday's queries, offline numbers inflate. Splits are therefore time-based, training on weeks one through six and evaluating on week seven, with features computed only from data available before each evaluation moment.

The serving funnel

Query understanding runs first and stays thin: spelling correction, language identification, and entity recognition (detecting that "ronaldo skills 2025" contains a person entity that should weight name matches), all in about 5 milliseconds. The rewritten query then fans out to both retrieval paths in parallel, BM25 over the inverted index and ANN search over the vector index, each returning its top 300 in roughly 40 milliseconds, overlapped rather than summed because they run concurrently. The union and dedupe stage assembles about 500 candidates, the ranker scores them in around 30 milliseconds with batched feature lookups from the feature store, and a light final pass applies diversity and policy rules before the page renders, landing the core path near 80 milliseconds against the 100 ms allocation.

The serving funnel: a thin query understanding stage feeds the text and vector retrievers in parallel, their union of about 500 candidates flows into the learned ranker, and 20 videos come out. Candidate counts shrink left to right so each stage can afford a heavier model.

The indexing side

Everything the funnel touches was precomputed at upload time. When a video arrives, three processors run on it. The metadata indexer tokenizes title, description, tags, and uploader captions into the inverted index. The frame pipeline samples frames, embeds them with the image tower of the CLIP-style model, pools them into the video vector, and inserts it into the ANN index. The ASR system transcribes the audio, typically within minutes, and its transcript terms join the text index as a separate weaker-weighted field. The heavy content work, embedding and ASR, costs real compute, which is the reason it happens once per video at upload rather than per query.

Freshness gets its own lane because the batch indexes rebuild on cycles measured in hours, which is an eternity for a news query. A near-real-time index, a small append-only structure holding the newest videos indexed on metadata alone within seconds to minutes of upload, sits alongside the batch indexes and is queried with them, with its entries aging out as the batch indexes catch up. Content signals for fresh videos arrive late by construction, so the ranker is trained with explicit missing-feature handling and leans on metadata, channel history, and early engagement velocity until embeddings and transcripts land, which is also the design answer to why freshness is a ranking problem and not just an indexing one.

Upload-time processing: frames are embedded into the vector index, metadata and ASR transcripts feed the text index, and the highlighted near-real-time lane indexes new videos on metadata alone within minutes so news queries can find them before the heavy content signals arrive.

Online evaluation and operations

Offline nDCG gains earn an A/B test, and the online metrics are behavioral: the share of search sessions that end in a meaningful watch (session success), time to first satisfying click, abandonment and query-reformulation rates, and watch time from search. Watch time deserves its caveat said plainly, because optimizing it alone rewards clickbait titles and stretched videos, so satisfaction signals temper it: bounce rates, "not interested" feedback, surveys on a small slice, and long-term return visits. A change that lifts watch time while raising immediate bounces is a regression wearing a costume, and the metric design has to be able to say so.

Operationally, the ranker retrains daily to weekly since engagement features drift quickly, while the embedding and ASR models refresh on a slower cadence, and any embedding model change forces re-embedding the corpus and rebuilding the vector index, which is scheduled like the infrastructure event it is. Training-serving skew, where features are computed one way in the training pipeline and another way online, is guarded by serving both from one feature store and by logging the exact feature values used at serving time for training replay. Monitoring covers prediction score distributions per query segment, retrieval overlap between the two paths, fresh-index age, ASR backlog, and the per-stage latency tails, with alerts on distribution shifts that precede visible quality drops. Query distributions drift with the news cycle and with language mix, so per-language dashboards are the difference between catching a regression in a week and hearing about it from the press.

Follow-up questions

  • Why run two retrieval paths instead of one good one? They fail differently: the text index wins on exact names and rare terms, the vector index wins on paraphrase and visual queries, and the union covers both at the cost of a dedupe step. Collapsing to either alone reintroduces that path's blind spots.
  • How do you keep metadata lies from winning? Content signals, embedding similarity and transcript matches, enter the ranker alongside metadata relevance, and engagement features eventually punish videos that fail their promise. Bounce-aware labels mean a clickbaited click teaches the model the opposite of what the uploader hoped.
  • Why is position bias such a big deal in training data? Because clicks confound relevance with placement: training naively on them teaches the new ranker to copy the old one's ordering. Randomized swaps on a small traffic slice plus propensity-weighted training break the circularity.
  • How does a video uploaded five minutes ago become searchable? Through the near-real-time index, which carries metadata-only entries within seconds to minutes, while embeddings and transcripts backfill later. The ranker is trained to handle the missing content features rather than discarding fresh videos for lacking them.
  • What would you measure to decide if ASR is worth its cost? Retrieval recall and session success on the query slice that matches transcripts but not metadata, against the GPU cost of transcribing every upload. Long-tail how-to and lecture queries usually justify it; transcribing music uploads usually does not, so gating ASR by predicted speech content is the refinement.
  • GBDT or neural ranker? GBDT first: it is strong on tabular features, cheap to retrain daily, and diagnosable through feature attribution. The neural ranker earns its complexity once bias-corrected labels and text-embedding inputs give its extra capacity something real to learn.
