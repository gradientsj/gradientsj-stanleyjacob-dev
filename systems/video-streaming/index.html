Design YouTube

Systems design · Media and storage · Aug 2025

Designing YouTube means building two pipelines that never touch the same bottleneck: an upload path that swallows hundreds of hours of video every minute and turns each file into a dozen playable variants, and a playback path that serves a billion hours of watching a day from caches close to the viewers. Interviewers ask it because it is the canonical heavy-data design: the interesting decisions are not about request routing but about bytes, where they are transformed, where they sit, and who pays to move them. The hard parts live in the processing pipeline, which has to transcode planet-scale ingest fast enough that creators are not waiting hours, and in the delivery economics, where the content delivery network bill dwarfs every other line item and quietly dictates the architecture. Search, recommendations, and comments are real systems too, and I would explicitly scope them out in the first minute to spend the interview where the data is.

Scope and requirements

In scope: creators upload videos up to hours long, the service processes each upload into multiple resolutions and formats, viewers watch on phones, browsers, and TVs with playback starting quickly and adapting to their bandwidth, and the system counts views. Out of scope by agreement: search, recommendations, comments, and monetization, each its own interview. Non-functionally, uploads must survive flaky consumer connections and resume rather than restart, processing should make a typical video watchable in minutes, playback should start in under a second or two of perceived delay and rarely stall, and availability matters asymmetrically: a viewer who cannot watch leaves immediately, while a creator whose upload processes slowly merely grumbles, so the read path gets the stricter budget.

Sizing the problem

The canonical ingest figure is 500 hours of video uploaded per minute, which is 720,000 hours per day. An hour of 1080p source footage at consumer bitrates is around 5 GB, so daily ingest is roughly 720,000 × 5 GB, about 3.6 PB of raw video per day before a single transcode runs. The processed renditions add less than intuition suggests, because every output is smaller than a 1080p source and lower rungs shrink fast; the full ladder roughly doubles stored bytes, call it 7 PB a day of storage growth, which over a year passes 2.5 EB and explains why storage tiering and per-video popularity decisions are core rather than optimizations.

Playback is bigger. One billion watch hours per day at an average delivered bitrate of 3 Mbps is 1,000,000,000 hours × 3,600 seconds × 3,000,000 bits, about 1.35 exabytes of egress per day, and dividing by 86,400 seconds gives an average egress rate near 125 terabits per second, with regional evening peaks well above it. No origin datacenter serves that; the only architecture that works is pushing the bytes to a CDN, the geographically distributed cache fleet that answers from a point of presence near each viewer, and the design question becomes how to make the cache hit ratio extremely high, because every miss is origin bandwidth the company pays for twice.

The upload API

Consumer uploads fail constantly, on hotel Wi-Fi mid-gigabyte, so the upload is resumable: the file is sent in chunks, the server records which byte ranges have arrived, and after a disconnect the client asks where to resume instead of starting over. A 2 GB upload that dies at 90 percent costs one more chunk, not another 2 GB.

POST /api/v1/videos
{ "title": "Trail ride", "size_bytes": 2147483648 }
→ 201 { "video_id": "v71x2", "upload_url": "https://upload.example.com/u/8842" }

PUT /u/8842
Content-Range: bytes 0-8388607/2147483648
→ 308 Range: bytes=0-8388607          // server confirms what it holds

PUT /u/8842
Content-Range: bytes 8388608-16777215/2147483648
→ 308 ...                              // repeat until the final chunk
→ 201 { "video_id": "v71x2", "state": "processing" }

On the final chunk the assembled file lands in object storage, a content hash is computed for deduplication so a re-uploaded identical file short-circuits processing, and a record enters the metadata database with state processing while the pipeline takes over.

The processing pipeline as a DAG

Processing is modeled as a DAG, a directed acyclic graph of tasks where each task starts when its inputs are ready and independent tasks run in parallel. For one video the graph includes splitting the source, transcoding each piece at each quality level, generating thumbnails and preview strips, fingerprinting the content against a copyright reference database, running policy scans, packaging segments and manifests, and flipping the video's state to ready. Transcoding, the conversion of video from one encoding to another, is the expensive node: the source is decoded and re-encoded at each target resolution with a codec, the compression format the player must understand. H.264 plays on effectively every device ever made, while newer codecs such as VP9 and AV1 cut bitrate 30 to 50 percent at the same visual quality but cost far more compute to encode, so the pragmatic ladder encodes everything in H.264 and spends the expensive codecs on videos with enough predicted viewership to repay the encode in bandwidth savings.

The reason the pipeline splits videos before transcoding is wall-clock time. Encoding runs near real time per core-stream for good quality settings, so a 2-hour video transcoded as one file at one quality is a 2-hour wait, multiplied across the rungs of the ladder. Split the same video into 10-second segments and there are 720 independent pieces, and with a worker per piece each takes tens of seconds, so the whole video at every quality finishes in about a minute plus scheduling overhead, a speedup of two orders of magnitude bought entirely with parallelism. Segment boundaries are placed on keyframes, the self-contained frames a decoder can start from, so each piece encodes independently and the later concatenation is seamless.

The upload service assembles resumable chunks into the raw store and registers the video, the DAG fans the work across parallel workers (split, transcode per rung, thumbnails, fingerprinting, packaging), and the encoded store acts as origin for the CDN, which fills on demand along the dashed path.

Adaptive bitrate playback

Viewers live on wildly different and constantly changing connections, and the answer shipped by the whole industry is adaptive bitrate streaming through HLS or DASH. Both work the same way: the video exists as a ladder of renditions, say 240p at 0.4 Mbps up through 4K at 16 Mbps, each chopped into the same few-second segments, and a manifest, a small text file, lists the renditions and the URL of every segment. The player downloads the manifest, starts conservatively on a low rung so the first frame appears fast, measures the actual throughput of each segment download, and switches rungs at segment boundaries, up when the measured bandwidth comfortably exceeds the next rung's bitrate, down hard when the playback buffer shrinks. All the intelligence sits in the player; the servers just serve small static files over plain HTTP, which is exactly what CDNs are best at, and that is not a coincidence but the reason the protocols were designed this way.

Step 1: the player asks the playback service for the video. Step 2: the service returns the manifest with the rendition ladder. Step 3: the player starts fetching low-rung segments from the CDN edge. Step 4: a cache miss fills from the origin store along the dashed path. Step 5: having measured segment throughput, the player switches up to a higher rendition at a segment boundary.

The economics of this path reduce to one number, the cache hit ratio. Video popularity is extravagantly skewed, with a small fraction of videos producing nearly all watch time, so edges holding just the hot set answer most requests; at a 95 percent hit ratio, origin egress is 5 percent of 125 Tbps, around 6 Tbps, which is large but feasible, and every additional point of hit ratio is measured in real money. The long tail of rarely watched videos is deliberately not cached, served from origin through regional shield caches that absorb repeat misses. Popularity is also managed actively rather than waited on: a new upload from a creator with ten million subscribers will be hot within minutes, so the system prewarms it, pushing segments to edges in the regions where that creator's audience lives before the notification goes out, and regional skew is respected because a video viral in Brazil need not occupy cache space in Japan.

Counting a billion views

A view counter sounds like UPDATE videos SET views = views + 1, and at this scale that statement is the design failure: a viral video taking 50,000 views a minute would serialize 800 writes a second onto one hot row, and the count is read on every watch page render besides. Views are an aggregation problem, not an increment problem. Players emit watch events into an event stream, a streaming job aggregates counts per video over short windows, and the aggregate is flushed to the store every few seconds, so the displayed number is eventually consistent, meaning briefly stale but converging, which is harmless for a vanity metric and is why nobody notices the count jumping in steps on a live viral video. The same event stream feeds fraud filtering, which separates plausible human views from replay loops and click farms before they ever reach the public number, and that filtering is the actual reason view pipelines are deliberately asynchronous: classification needs context that a synchronous increment can never have.

Live streaming deserves its one contrasting paragraph, because it looks adjacent and inverts the constraints. The pipeline must run in seconds end to end, so transcoding happens on dedicated always-on encoders rather than a batch DAG, segments are two seconds rather than ten, manifests update continuously as new segments appear, and caches can only help with the last few segments because nothing older matters. The latency budget changes everything: a video-on-demand system optimizes cost per byte over an infinite shelf life, while a live system optimizes seconds of delay over content with a shelf life of nothing.

Scaling, failures, and operations

Each tier scales on its own axis. Upload ingestion scales with creator count and is the least demanding tier; the transcode fleet scales with ingest hours and is an ideal fit for elastic and preemptible compute, since a killed worker costs one 10-second segment re-encoded, and the DAG scheduler retries failed tasks idempotently because every task writes to a deterministic output location. Storage scales by adding object storage capacity, with lifecycle policies moving cold renditions to denser tiers and the coldest tail to archival storage where a rare view pays a seconds-long retrieval. The metadata database, holding video records and rendition lists, is read-heavy and shards by video ID with caches in front, and it is the one tier where a failure is visible everywhere, since a metadata outage means no watch page resolves to a manifest even though every segment is sitting healthy on the CDN; that asymmetry argues for aggressive caching of manifests themselves at the edge with short revalidation.

The failure stories: a dead transcode worker is retried invisibly, a CDN point of presence failing means its viewers re-resolve to a neighboring edge with a latency bump and a temporary hit-ratio dip as the neighbor warms, and an origin region failure is survived because encoded segments replicate across regions, with the CDN configured to fail over its fill path. Copyright fingerprinting and policy scanning sit in the DAG before publication, so the safety property is structural: a video that has not passed scanning has no ready state and no manifest, and there is no race where content is watchable before it is checked. The operational dashboard leans on rebuffer ratio (the fraction of watch time spent stalled), join time (click to first frame), cache hit ratio by region, transcode queue depth, and time-to-ready per upload percentile, because those five numbers are the product as the viewer and creator experience it.

Follow-up questions

  • Why split videos into segments before transcoding? Parallelism: a 2-hour video becomes 720 independent 10-second pieces that transcode simultaneously in about a minute total, instead of hours serially, and the same segmentation is what adaptive streaming needs anyway.
  • Why not transcode everything to AV1 if it saves 30 to 50 percent bandwidth? Encode cost and device support. The saving repays the encode only when a video is watched enough, so popular videos get the expensive codecs and the long tail stays H.264, which every device can decode.
  • Where does the first second of playback latency go? Manifest fetch plus the first low-rung segment. Starting on a low rendition makes the first frame cheap, and the player upgrades within a few segments once it has measured real throughput.
  • What happens on a CDN cache miss for an unpopular video? The edge fetches from a regional shield cache, which fetches from origin object storage, so the viewer pays a slower start and the system pays origin egress, acceptable precisely because the tail is rarely watched.
  • Why is the view count eventually consistent? Synchronous increments would hot-spot one row per viral video and bypass fraud filtering. Streaming aggregation batches millions of events into periodic flushes and lets classification run before numbers go public.
  • How would live streaming change this design? The DAG becomes an always-on low-latency encoder chain, segments shrink to seconds, manifests update continuously, and caching only helps at the live edge, so the cost structure shifts from storage to real-time compute and fanout.
