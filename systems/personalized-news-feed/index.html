Design a personalized news feed

Systems design · Machine learning systems · Dec 2025

When a person opens a social app, the system has a few thousand posts it could legitimately show them: posts from friends, pages they follow, and groups they belong to. The infrastructure that gathers those candidates, the fan-out of posts to follower inventories, is its own design question; this article assumes it exists and takes on the layer above it, the machine learning system that decides what to show first. That ordering decision is the product. The same inventory ordered well feels like a feed that knows you, and ordered badly feels like noise, and the difference between the two is a ranking model and the apparatus around it.

The goal deserves a careful statement, because the sloppy version leads the design astray. The aim is to order eligible posts so the person finds the most value in the session, and since value cannot be measured directly, it is operationalized through predicted interactions: the probability they will like, comment, share, hide, or spend time on each post. That proxy is genuinely imperfect, a fact worth saying plainly in an interview. People interact with outrage they do not value and silently appreciate things they never click, which is why the design below keeps humans in the loop on how predictions combine into a score and why online evaluation reaches beyond interaction counts. The interesting parts of the problem are the multi-stage funnel that fits heavy models into a tight budget, the multi-task model at the heart of it, and the gap between offline and online truth.

Scope and requirements

Assume hundreds of millions of daily users, an average inventory of about 2,000 eligible posts per feed load, and a server-side budget of roughly 200 milliseconds to produce a ranked feed, with the ML portion holding to half of that. The system must incorporate signals from the current session, because the feed a person sees on their third refresh should account for what they already saw and skipped on the first two. In scope: the ranking funnel, the main ranking model, the value computation that turns predictions into an ordering, integrity integration, and the training pipeline. Out of scope: candidate gathering, the content classifiers' internals, and ad insertion, which runs as a separate auction interleaved later.

Framing the problem as a machine learning task

The naive framing is a single binary model, engagement or not, and its weakness defines the real design: interactions differ wildly in what they say about value. A comment is rarer and far more meaningful than a passive like, a share extends reach, a hide is a strong negative that a single engagement model would drown out, since hides are perhaps a tenth as common as likes. The production framing is multi-task prediction: one model with a shared network and separate output heads, each head predicting one interaction, P(like), P(comment), P(share), P(hide), and expected dwell time, the time the person will spend on the post. The input is a (viewer, post, context) triple and the output is a vector of predictions, which a separate value model collapses into a single ranking score. Sharing the network matters statistically, not just operationally: comments are sparse, and a comment head sharing a trunk with the like head learns from the dense signal what the sparse signal alone could not teach it.

Data and labels

Training data is logged impressions joined with what happened next: each (viewer, post) impression becomes one example with a label per head, like or not, comment or not, dwell seconds, all within an attribution window. The labels are implicit, arising from behavior rather than human annotation, which makes them plentiful and biased at once. The dominant bias is position bias: posts shown at the top of the feed receive more of every interaction simply because they were seen, so a model trained naively learns "whatever the old system ranked first is good," partly recycling the incumbent's opinions. The standard mitigations are to log the display position and include it as a feature during training while fixing it to a constant at serving time, so the model can attribute some engagement to placement rather than content, and to keep a small randomized slice of traffic, around 1 percent with lightly shuffled order, which provides nearly position-unbiased data for evaluation. Hides and "see less like this" responses are logged with extra care, because they are the rare explicit negatives in a sea of implicit signals.

Features and representation

Four families carry most of the weight. Actor-viewer affinity captures the relationship: how often this viewer interacts with this author, how recently, and in which ways, computed over rolling windows. Content-type fit captures format taste: a person who watches videos but skims text posts should see that reflected, via the viewer's historical engagement rate per content type. Recency enters with decay, since a feed is partly news and a six-hour-old post from a close friend can still beat a two-minute-old post from an acquaintance, which is why recency is a feature the model weighs rather than a sort order. Topic match uses embeddings: the post's text mapped to a vector, the viewer's interest profile as an aggregate of vectors from posts they engaged with, and the similarity between them as a feature. Alongside these, session context flows through a short-term store: what the person already saw this session, what they skipped, and what they lingered on minutes ago, which is the difference between a feed that adapts mid-session and one that repeats itself.

All of this is served from a feature store, the system that computes and serves feature values consistently for training and serving, and consistency is the operative word. Training-serving skew is the failure mode where the offline pipeline and the online path compute the "same" feature differently: if offline affinity is computed over 90 days while the online store keeps 30, the model trains against one distribution and serves against another, and quality sags with no error anywhere to see. The strongest guard is logging the exact feature values used at serving time and training on those logs, so skew cannot arise from recomputation.

Model architecture

The funnel has two ranking stages because the heavy model cannot afford 2,000 candidates. The first-pass ranker is a cheap model, often a distilled version of the main ranker (a small model trained to mimic the big one's outputs) or a linear model over precomputed features, that scores the full inventory in a few milliseconds and keeps the top 500 or so. Its job is recall, not precision: it only needs to avoid discarding things the main ranker would have loved. The main ranker is the multi-task network described above, a shared trunk of a few hidden layers over the concatenated features feeding the five heads, sized so that scoring around 500 candidates fits the latency budget on accelerated hardware.

The value model sits on top, and it is deliberately not learned. The score for each post is a weighted combination of the head predictions, for example P(like) times 1, plus P(comment) times 15, plus P(share) times 30, minus P(hide) times 100, plus a small dwell term. Worked through for one post with P(like) = 0.10, P(comment) = 0.02, P(share) = 0.01, and P(hide) = 0.005: the score is 0.10 + 0.30 + 0.30 - 0.50 = 0.20, and it is instructive that the rare hide signal at 0.5 percent probability outweighs the like term entirely, which is exactly the intent. The weights are a product decision reviewed by humans, not parameters fit by gradient descent, because they encode what the company means by value, comments over likes, penalties for hides, and they change through deliberate review when the product philosophy changes rather than drifting wherever engagement optimization would take them.

The multi-task ranker. One shared network feeds five prediction heads, and the value model combines them with human-reviewed weights, here P(like) × 1 + P(comment) × 15 + P(share) × 30 - P(hide) × 100 plus a dwell term, with integrity scores entering as dashed demotions.

Integrity integration

Separately trained classifiers score every post for clickbait, engagement bait, likely misinformation (via proxies such as fact-check matches), and borderline content that approaches policy lines without crossing them. These are trained on human-reviewed labels, not engagement, because engagement is precisely the signal that fails here: the content these classifiers target often engages exceptionally well. Posts that actually violate policy are removed upstream and never reach ranking; everything else enters the value model as demotions, multiplicative reductions applied to the score, rather than hard removals, which keeps the system debatable and tunable, since a demotion strength is a number that can be reviewed, measured, and adjusted per classifier. Transparency requirements push the same direction: a "why am I seeing this" feature must trace a ranking back to contributions, you follow this page, this post is popular among your friends, and an architecture of explicit heads, weights, and demotions can produce that explanation, where a single end-to-end engagement score could not.

Offline evaluation

Each head is evaluated separately on a time-split holdout, training on two weeks and validating on the following days, with AUC and normalized entropy per head. AUC is the probability that a random positive outranks a random negative; normalized entropy is the model's log loss divided by the log loss of always predicting the base rate, lower being better. Worked example for the like head: at a 5 percent base like rate the constant predictor's log loss is 0.199 nats, so a model achieving 0.179 has a normalized entropy of 0.90, capturing about 10 percent of the available uncertainty beyond the base rate; the same model might post AUC 0.92 on likes but only 0.78 on hides, and that per-head breakdown is the diagnostic view, since the value model amplifies the hide head a hundredfold. Calibration per head is checked too, because the value model's weighted sum silently assumes the probabilities are comparable across heads, and a like head predicting double the true rate would distort every score it touches.

The serving architecture

At request time the funnel runs: roughly 2,000 candidates from inventory, the first-pass ranker prunes to about 500 in a handful of milliseconds using cached features, the feature store assembles the full feature vectors including session context, the main ranker scores all 500 across its heads in around 40 milliseconds batched, the value model applies weights and integrity demotions in well under a millisecond, and final feed rules run last: author diversity (no more than two consecutive posts from the same author), format mixing, and seen-post suppression. The budget closes at around 80 to 100 milliseconds for the ML path inside the 200 millisecond envelope. The two-stage shape is the load-bearing decision, since one heavy pass over 2,000 candidates would cost four times the main-ranker compute for candidates that mostly had no chance, and the first-pass ranker buys that back at the cost of a managed recall risk, which is itself monitored by measuring how often the main ranker's top posts would have been pruned by the first pass.

The ranking funnel. A cheap first pass prunes 2,000 candidates to 500, the feature store feeds the multi-task main ranker, the value model combines head predictions with dashed integrity demotions, and diversity rules shape the final feed.

Online evaluation and operations

Offline metrics gate iteration, but launches are decided online, and the two regularly disagree. A model can win offline by predicting clicks better and lose online because what it surfaces more of is clickbait-adjacent content that drives short-term interactions and long-term fatigue; offline evaluation also scores against logged data shaped by the old policy, so it systematically underestimates how behavior changes when the ranking changes. The online metrics that decide launches are therefore broader and slower: sessions per user and time in app read carefully rather than maximized blindly, meaningful interaction rates such as comments and reshares among real connections, hide and report rates as guardrails that can veto a launch on their own, and direct user surveys ("is this post worth your time?") as the only signal that approximates value without passing through engagement at all. Experiments run for weeks because feed changes have lagged effects, and the deepest ones are read against long-term holdouts.

Operationally the models retrain daily or continuously, since affinity and content distributions drift fast. A new model is validated by shadow scoring, running it on live traffic and logging its scores without serving them, which catches feature-pipeline mismatches and score-distribution shifts before any user sees a ranking from it, and promotion proceeds through a small percentage rollout with automated rollback triggers on guardrail metrics. Monitoring covers the inputs and outputs continuously: feature distributions against training snapshots, per-head prediction means against realized rates as a standing calibration check, and score distributions per content type, where a sudden shift usually means an upstream pipeline change nobody announced. The session store and feature logs get the same scrutiny as the model, because in practice most quality regressions in systems like this arrive through the data path rather than the weights.

Follow-up questions

  • Why multi-task instead of one engagement model? Interactions carry different meanings and wildly different base rates, and one binary target collapses them into mush. Separate heads keep rare strong signals like hides from drowning, share statistical strength through the trunk, and give the value model levers to weigh.
  • Why are the value weights not learned? They define what the product means by value, which is a judgment, not a statistic. Learning them would re-derive engagement maximization with extra steps; reviewing them as a product decision keeps the objective inspectable and deliberately changeable.
  • How do you fight position bias? Log position and train with it as a feature fixed to a constant at serving, and keep a small randomized traffic slice as a nearly unbiased evaluation set. Ignoring it trains the model to agree with the previous ranker rather than with users.
  • What is training-serving skew and one concrete guard? Features computed differently offline and online, such as affinity over 90 days in training and 30 in serving, degrading quality with no visible error. The guard: log serving-time feature values and train only on those logs.
  • Why can a model win offline and lose online? Offline evaluation scores against data generated by the old policy and counts proxies, not value. A model that better predicts clicks can shift the feed toward content that erodes sessions and trust, which only guardrails, surveys, and long experiments reveal.
  • Why demote integrity-flagged content instead of removing it? Violating content is removed upstream. For the borderline rest, demotion strengths are tunable, measurable numbers compatible with transparency and appeal, while silent removal of non-violating content is a heavier policy with no gradations.
