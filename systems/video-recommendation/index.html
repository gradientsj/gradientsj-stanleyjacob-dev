Design a video recommendation system

Systems design · Machine learning systems · Dec 2025

The YouTube homepage answers a strange question many times per second: out of billions of videos, which few dozen should this particular person see right now, with no query to go on? Recommendation drives the majority of watch time on large video platforms, which is why the design question is an interview staple, and it is a generous question for a candidate because it contains every major theme of production ML in one system: retrieval at impossible scale, learned ranking, biased training data, cold start, and a feedback loop that will quietly eat the product if nobody names it.

The organizing idea is the funnel, and stating it with numbers up front frames everything after. No model can score billions of items per request, so the system works in stages of increasing cost on shrinking sets: candidate generation reduces billions to a few thousand with very cheap math, ranking scores those few thousand, of which a couple hundred survive, with a model that can afford rich features, and re-ranking shapes the final few dozen with business and diversity rules. Each stage exists so the next one can afford to be smarter, and most design decisions below are about which stage a given piece of intelligence belongs in.

Scope and requirements

The surface is the signed-in homepage feed of a platform with two billion users and a corpus of several billion videos growing by hundreds of hours per minute. The system returns roughly 30 ranked videos per request, personalized from watch history, subscriptions, and context such as device, time, and language, within a backend budget of about 200 milliseconds at the 95th percentile. Peak traffic is on the order of a few hundred thousand homepage requests per second. In scope are candidate generation, ranking, re-ranking, and the training loop; out of scope are search (query-driven), the up-next panel (a sibling system sharing most parts), ads, and the integrity systems that decide what is eligible to recommend at all, though re-ranking consumes their outputs.

Framing the problem as a machine learning task

The candidate generation stage is framed as extreme-scale retrieval: learn representations such that, given a user, the system can fetch the few thousand videos most likely to engage them without scoring the whole corpus. The ranking stage is framed as supervised prediction: given a user-video pair, predict the probability of click and the expected engagement and satisfaction conditioned on impression, trained from logged behavior. Treating the whole thing as one classification problem over billions of classes fails on arithmetic, and treating it as pure collaborative filtering, the classic insight that people who watched the same things in the past will watch the same things next, fails alone on freshness and cold start, so the production framing uses learned retrieval plus learned ranking, with collaborative signal living inside the learned representations.

Data and labels

Training data is the impression log: for each video shown, whether the user clicked, how long they watched, whether they liked, shared, or hit "not interested," all keyed by position on the page. Implicit labels dominate by volume, and the strongest simple label is watch time conditioned on impression rather than raw clicks, because clicks alone teach the model that misleading thumbnails are excellent content. Explicit signals, likes and survey responses, are scarce but anchor what satisfaction means. Position bias infects all of it, since items shown first get engaged more for being first, so training either includes position as a feature that is neutralized at serving time, or weights examples by estimated position propensity, and a small slice of randomized orderings keeps those estimates calibrated. The deeper bias is that logs only contain outcomes for videos the previous system chose to show, which is the feedback loop hazard treated in its own right below.

Features and the feature store

Three feature families feed the models. User features include watch and search history aggregates, topic affinities, subscription lists, and context like device and hour. Video features include topic, language, duration, age, channel statistics, and engagement rates such as click-through and average completion. Cross features describe the pair, for example how often this user watched this channel in the last month, and they are the expensive, high-value family that only the ranking stage can afford, precisely because the candidate set is small by the time ranking runs.

These features live in a feature store, which is the system that computes each feature once and serves identical values to offline training and online inference. It earns its definition with the failure it prevents: training-serving skew, where a feature is computed one way in the training pipeline and a subtly different way in the serving path, a seven-day average offline versus a six-day average online, and the model quietly underperforms with nothing erroring. The discipline that goes with the store is logging the exact feature values used at serving time and training on those logged values rather than recomputing history.

Model architecture

Candidate generation runs several sources in parallel, because different sources are good at different things and the union feeds ranking. The workhorse is two-tower retrieval, worth defining carefully since it is the load-bearing idea. Two neural networks are trained jointly: a user tower that maps user features to an embedding, a learned vector summarizing taste, and an item tower that maps video features to an embedding in the same space, with training pushing the dot product of genuinely engaged (user, video) pairs high and sampled non-engaged pairs low. The towers never look at each other's raw inputs, and that independence is the entire trick: every video's embedding can be precomputed offline and loaded into an ANN index, an approximate nearest neighbor structure that finds the highest-dot-product vectors while examining only a sliver of the corpus, so at request time the system computes one user embedding and pulls the top few thousand videos in milliseconds. Alongside it run cheaper sources: recent uploads from subscribed channels, popular-in-region lists that need no personalization, and a fresh-content source that gives new videos a lane into the funnel before they have any engagement history.

The ranking model is where capacity goes. A deep network, commonly with multi-task heads, meaning one shared body whose several output heads predict click probability, expected watch time, like probability, and predicted survey satisfaction simultaneously, scores each of the couple thousand candidates with full cross features. The heads are blended into a single ordering score with tuned weights, and the weighting is a product decision wearing a model's clothes: watch time alone famously rewards clickbait and rabbit holes, so satisfaction-flavored heads get deliberate weight to pull the blend toward content users are glad they watched, which is the fix the field converged on after watch-time-only optimization showed its failure mode. A plain logistic regression or GBDT over the same features is the baseline to name first, and the neural ranker justifies itself by learning feature interactions the baseline cannot.

Offline evaluation

Candidate generation is measured with recall@K against held-out future behavior: take a user's actual next-watched video from a time slice after the training window, and check whether it appears in the generator's top K. If the held-out watch shows up in the top 2,000 candidates for 62 of 100 test users, recall@2000 is 0.62, and a generator change that lifts it to 0.66 is doing its one job better, getting the right items into the pool. Ranking is measured with AUC, the probability that a randomly chosen positive example outscores a randomly chosen negative: with positives scored 0.9 and 0.4 and negatives scored 0.7 and 0.2, three of the four positive-negative pairs are ordered correctly, so AUC is 0.75. Watch-time heads use regression error, and calibration is checked so that predicted probabilities match observed rates, since the blend assumes the heads mean what they say. Splits are strictly time-based, training on the past and evaluating on the future, because random splits leak tomorrow's engagement statistics into today's features and flatter every model. The unhappy fact to state plainly is that offline gains often vanish online: offline evaluation can only test against what the old policy logged, so a model that would have shown different videos is evaluated on the narrow region where the policies overlap, which is why offline metrics gate experiments rather than ship them.

The serving funnel

A homepage request fans out to the candidate sources in parallel, the two-tower ANN lookup beside subscriptions, regional popularity, and the fresh lane, returning a merged, deduplicated pool of about 2,000 videos in roughly 30 milliseconds. The ranker then scores the pool with batched feature-store lookups in about 50 milliseconds, and the top couple hundred by blended score pass to re-ranking, which applies the rules a scoring model cannot see: diversity across channels and topics so the page is not one channel's wall, recency mixing, demotion of borderline content flagged by integrity systems, and deduplication against recently shown items. About 30 videos render. The latency budget closes at roughly 100 milliseconds of model-path work inside the 200 millisecond envelope, and the funnel arithmetic, billions to 2,000 to 200 to 30, is what makes each stage's model affordable at its position.

The funnel: parallel candidate sources, with two-tower retrieval over an ANN index doing the heavy reduction from billions, merge into about 2,000 candidates; the multi-task ranker keeps a couple hundred; re-ranking rules shape the final 30 for the page.

Cold start, exploration, and the feedback loop

Cold start arrives on both sides of the marketplace. A new user has no history for the user tower, so the system leans on context, language, region, device, and any onboarding topic picks, blended with popularity, and the page improves within a session as the first few watches become features. A new video is invisible to engagement-based sources by construction, so it enters through content features in the item tower, topic, language, channel history, and through a deliberate exploration budget: a small percentage of impressions reserved for serving videos the system is uncertain about to a random slice of plausibly matched users, gathering the engagement signal that no amount of modeling can conjure from nothing.

Exploration is also the antidote to the named hazard of this design, the feedback loop: the model trains on what it showed, shows what it scored highly, and therefore never learns about the videos it never showed, so errors of omission become permanent and the catalog ossifies around early winners. The exploration slice breaks the loop by paying a small, measured engagement cost today for unbiased signal tomorrow, and the logged randomness doubles as the data that makes off-policy evaluation and position-bias estimates trustworthy. A platform that skips this line item does not avoid the cost; it pays it invisibly in a feed that slowly narrows.

The training loop: logs feed the feature store, the trainer publishes versioned models, and serving writes new impressions back into the logs. The dashed exploration path is the deliberate randomness that keeps the loop from only ever learning about its own past choices.

Online evaluation and operations

Models graduate through A/B tests on long-horizon metrics: daily active users, total satisfied watch time, survey satisfaction, and retention measured over weeks, because short-term engagement lifts can cash out long-term trust. Experiments hold back a small long-term holdout group from a change for months to observe slow effects that two-week tests miss. The deployment path is conservative since the homepage is the product: a new ranker first runs on shadow traffic, scoring real requests without affecting users while its score distributions and latency are compared against the incumbent, then ramps through small percentage slices with automated rollback on metric regressions, all enabled by the model registry keeping every version addressable.

Steady-state operations revolve around freshness and skew. Rankers retrain daily or continuously since engagement features move fast; the two-tower model retrains on a similar cadence, with item embeddings re-batched into the ANN index every few hours and the index versioned together with its tower, because mixing a new user tower with an old item index quietly corrupts retrieval geometry. Monitoring watches prediction distributions per head, feature null rates and distribution drift, candidate source overlap, exploration slice health, and end-to-end funnel latency, with the score histogram alarm usually firing before any business metric moves. Periodic audits check the page-level outcomes the per-item models cannot see, topic concentration and channel diversity drift, which is where re-ranking rules get retuned.

Follow-up questions

  • Why a funnel instead of one great model? Arithmetic: scoring billions of items with a cross-feature ranker per request is computationally impossible, while two-tower dot products against a precomputed ANN index are nearly free. The funnel spends compute where the candidate set is small enough to afford it.
  • Why can the ranker use cross features when the towers cannot? Two-tower retrieval depends on item embeddings being precomputable without knowing the user, which forbids user-item interaction features in the towers. After candidate generation shrinks the set to thousands, per-pair features become affordable, which is exactly the ranker's advantage.
  • How do you stop watch time from rewarding clickbait? Multi-task heads that predict satisfaction signals, surveys, likes, "not interested" rates, get real weight in the blended score, and bounce-heavy watch patterns are labeled as the negative signal they are. The blend weights are revisited with survey-based A/B results, not set once.
  • What is the exploration budget actually buying? Unbiased signal on new and under-shown videos, calibrated position-bias estimates, and protection against the feedback loop that otherwise locks the catalog to early winners. The cost is a small, measured dip in short-term engagement on the exploration slice.
  • Why do offline wins evaporate online? Offline evaluation replays logs collected under the old policy, so it can only judge a new model where the two policies overlap, and it cannot observe how users would react to genuinely different pages. Offline metrics gate which models earn an A/B test; only the test ships them.
  • What breaks if the feature store is skipped? Training-serving skew: offline pipelines and online services drift into computing the "same" feature differently, the model underperforms with no error anywhere, and the failure is found months later by someone diffing feature values by hand. One computation, two consumers, logged at serving time.
