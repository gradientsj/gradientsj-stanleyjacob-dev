Design a stock exchange

Systems design · Reservations and money · Nov 2025

A stock exchange accepts buy and sell orders for listed securities, matches them into trades, publishes the resulting prices to the world, and hands the trades off for clearing and settlement. It is the rare interview question where the answer is not a web architecture at all: the core of a modern equities venue is a single-threaded program walking an in-memory data structure in microseconds, surrounded by machinery whose entire job is to feed that program a perfectly ordered stream and to broadcast what it did. Interviewers ask it to see whether a candidate can recognize when the standard playbook of caches, queues, and eventual consistency is the wrong playbook.

Exchanges are special because their requirements are absolute rather than statistical. Matching must complete in microseconds, fairness rules are regulatory obligations rather than aspirations, the same inputs must always produce the same trades, and every message ever received must be reconstructible years later. The design below works from those constraints to the architecture they force, with the order book, the sequencer, and the market data fabric as the load-bearing pieces.

Scope and requirements

Functionally the venue handles limit orders, which are instructions to buy or sell at a stated price or better, and market orders, which execute immediately at the best price currently available, plus cancels and modifications. It matches orders under price-time priority, the fairness rule stating that a better price always trades first and that at the same price the order that arrived earlier trades first. It publishes market data, both the trades and the changing shape of the book, and it forwards executed trades to clearing, the post-trade process that nets obligations and settles money against shares, which this design acknowledges and leaves to its batch world.

The non-functional requirements are the unusual part. Matching latency must sit in the tens of microseconds, determinism must be total in the sense that replaying the same input stream yields bit-identical trades, the audit trail must capture every inbound message in its exact processed order, and availability failures must never manifest as lost or reordered orders. Throughput is high but, as the sizing shows, not the truly hard part.

Sizing the problem

A large national exchange processes billions of order messages per day; take 10 billion as the round number. The trading day is six and a half hours, or 23,400 seconds, so the average is about 427,000 messages per second, but exchange traffic concentrates brutally at the open, the close, and news moments, so the system must absorb peaks of ten times the average, around 4 million messages per second across all symbols. The per-symbol picture is what shapes the design: the flow spreads over thousands of listed symbols, and even the hottest symbol on its hottest day peaks at a few hundred thousand messages per second.

Now compare that with what one core does. A simple in-memory operation, an array index, a compare, a pointer chase that stays in cache, costs on the order of 100 nanoseconds or less, so one core executes several million such operations per second even with bookkeeping, and matching a single order is only a handful of them. One thread can therefore match every message the busiest symbol produces, with an order of magnitude to spare. Memory tells the same story: a million resting orders at roughly 100 bytes each is 100 MB, so the books for the entire market fit comfortably in one machine's RAM. Those two facts, one thread suffices per symbol and everything fits in memory, decide the whole architecture.

The order interface

Members connect through gateways speaking a session protocol, FIX or a terser binary equivalent, but the logical message set is small and worth writing out. Every acknowledgment carries the sequence number the venue assigned, which is the thread that ties the entire system together.

NEW_ORDER   { "client_order_id": "c-118", "symbol": "ACME", "side": "buy",
              "type": "limit", "qty": 300, "price": 10.02 }
→ ACK       { "order_id": 884213, "seq": 90417723 }

CANCEL      { "order_id": 884213 }
→ CANCELED  { "order_id": 884213, "seq": 90417951 }

EXECUTION   { "order_id": 884213, "fill_qty": 200, "price": 10.02,
              "trade_id": 7741180, "seq": 90418002 }    // pushed to both counterparties

The high-level architecture

Client gateways terminate member sessions and run pre-trade risk checks: buying power, which is the funds or credit a member has available to cover what it is ordering, price collars that reject orders absurdly far from the market, and per-member rate limits. Everything that passes flows into the sequencer, and from there the sequenced stream fans out to the matching engines, the audit log, and onward to market data.

Risk-checked orders pass through the sequencer, which stamps a single global order on every message; the engines consume the sequenced stream by symbol range, the audit log records it verbatim, and every book change flows to the market data publisher. The dashed replay path is how a recovering engine catches up.

The order book, worked through

The order book is the central data structure: for each symbol, two sides of resting limit orders, the bids waiting to buy and the asks waiting to sell. Each side is a sorted set of price levels, and each level holds a first-in-first-out queue of orders, which is precisely how price-time priority becomes a data structure: the sort gives price priority, and the queue gives time priority within a price. Near the touch, the best prices where all the action is, flat arrays indexed by price tick beat fancier trees because they stay in cache.

# one matching thread per symbol; no locks anywhere in this code
def on_market_buy(book, qty):
    while qty > 0 and book.asks:
        level   = book.asks.best()            # lowest ask price first
        resting = level.queue[0]              # earliest order at that price first
        fill    = min(qty, resting.qty)
        emit_trade(price=level.price, qty=fill, maker=resting.order_id)
        resting.qty -= fill; qty -= fill
        if resting.qty == 0: level.queue.popleft()
        if not level.queue:  book.asks.drop_best()

A worked match makes the mechanics concrete. Suppose ACME's ask side rests at 200 shares at 10.02, 250 at 10.03, and 500 at 10.04, with bids at 10.01 and 10.00 below. A market buy for 300 shares walks the ladder: it takes all 200 at 10.02, clearing that level entirely, then takes 100 of the 250 resting at 10.03. Two trades print, 200 at 10.02 and 100 at 10.03, the partially filled resting order keeps its queue position with 150 remaining, and the book's best ask is now 10.03, so the spread has widened from one cent to two. A limit buy at 10.03 for the same 300 shares would have produced identical fills, while a limit buy at 10.02 would have taken the first 200 and then rested at 10.02 as the new best bid.

A market buy for 300 walks the ask side in price-time order: step 1 takes all 200 at 10.02 and clears the level, step 2 takes 100 of the 250 at 10.03, and step 3 prints two trades. The book is left with 150 shares at a new best ask of 10.03.

One thread per symbol, and the sequencer that makes it possible

The design move interviewers wait for is the shape of the matching engine: single-threaded per symbol and entirely in memory. The sizing already showed one core has throughput to spare, and going parallel would buy nothing while costing everything that matters here. Locks and cross-core coordination would dominate the microsecond budget, and worse, they would introduce scheduling nondeterminism, where the same two orders might match differently depending on which thread won a race. Sequential processing of an already-ordered input stream eliminates locks, keeps the book hot in one core's cache, and makes the engine a pure function from input sequence to trades. The lineage is the LMAX architecture, which demonstrated millions of transactions per second on a single thread feeding through pre-allocated ring buffers, and the discipline that comes with it: no garbage-collection pauses on the hot path, no system calls, no wall-clock reads inside matching, with even time arriving as sequenced tick messages so replays see identical inputs.

The component that makes "already-ordered" true is the sequencer: it receives every risk-checked inbound message and stamps it with a monotonically increasing sequence number, and from that point the sequence is the definition of what happened first, regardless of network jitter upstream. This single decision turns the cluster into a replicated state machine, the arrangement where deterministic replicas consuming the same ordered commands necessarily hold identical state. Any replica of the matching engine, live or warm standby, that has consumed sequence number N has exactly the same book as every other replica at N, so failover is not a recovery procedure so much as a promotion: the standby is already correct, and if a replica must start cold, it loads the latest snapshot and replays the sequenced log forward, arriving at provably the same state including the same trades. The sequenced log doubles as the complete audit trail the regulator requires, one stream serving consensus, recovery, and compliance at once. The sequencer itself is kept almost logic-free, stamp and forward, precisely so it can be made redundant with a hot standby and fast failover without dragging business state along.

Market data and fairness

Every change to the book, an add, a cancel, an execution, becomes an incremental market data message, and periodic snapshots of the full book let late joiners and gap-recovering consumers resynchronize without replaying the day. Inside the facility, the feed travels over UDP multicast, a network mode where the switch delivers one transmitted packet to all subscribers simultaneously, so no recipient is first because the fabric itself does not play favorites; fairness comes from physics rather than from policy. Lost packets are detected by sequence gaps and repaired from a retransmission service or the next snapshot. The public internet gets conflated feeds, which coalesce rapid updates and publish the latest state at intervals, trading completeness for bandwidth that retail consumers can afford.

The same fairness logic explains why exchanges sell rack space. Colocation puts member servers in the exchange's own data center with deliberately equalized cable lengths, so that no participant buys an advantage the venue did not publish a price for; latency from the matching engine to every colocated rack is engineered to be identical to within nanoseconds. Control-plane actions ride the same machinery as orders: a circuit breaker, the rule that halts trading in a symbol or the whole market when prices move too far too fast, is implemented as a halt command flowing through the sequencer, so every replica and every feed sees the halt at the same sequence number and the state remains deterministic even while the market is being stopped.

Scaling, failures, and operations

Scaling is by symbol partitioning. Symbols are independent, so the market shards cleanly across engine instances, the busiest symbols get the most isolated placement, and only control messages fan out everywhere. Gateways scale horizontally with member count, and the market data tier scales by adding multicast groups and feed handlers. The sequencer is the one component that cannot be parallelized within a partition, which is why it stays minimal, runs on the fastest path available, and is measured in nanoseconds; venues that outgrow a single global sequencer give each engine partition its own, accepting that cross-symbol ordering is then only approximate, which trading semantics permit.

Failure handling falls out of the replicated state machine. An engine crash promotes a standby that is already at the same sequence number, and the gap, if any, is replay rather than reconstruction, with clients seeing elevated latency for milliseconds rather than lost orders. A sequencer failure fails over to its standby with a strict rule that no message is delivered to engines until its sequence stamp is durable on the standby, since a stamp that could be forgotten is no ordering at all. Gateways shed sessions to their peers. After the close, the system changes character entirely: executed trades feed the clearinghouse, obligations are netted so each member settles only its net position per symbol, and settlement completes at T+1, meaning shares and cash legally change hands one business day after the trade. The microsecond world and the batch world meet in a file, and both sides reconcile it line by line, because an exchange's last invariant is that nobody ever disagrees about what traded.

Follow-up questions

  • Why is the matching engine single-threaded? One core already exceeds the busiest symbol's peak message rate by an order of magnitude, and parallelism would add lock overhead and scheduling nondeterminism, breaking both the latency budget and the requirement that identical inputs produce identical trades.
  • How do replicas stay exactly identical? Same sequenced input plus deterministic code: no wall-clock reads, no randomness, no iteration over unordered structures, with time itself delivered as sequenced tick messages. A replica at sequence N is bit-identical to any other at N.
  • What happens if an engine dies mid-burst? A standby consuming the same stream is already at the same state and is promoted; a cold start loads a snapshot and replays the sequenced log. Nothing is lost because the log, not the engine's memory, is the source of truth.
  • How is fairness physically enforced? The sequencer defines arrival order at a single point, colocation equalizes cable lengths so no member is closer, and multicast delivers market data to all subscribers in the same instant.
  • What data structure holds the book? Per side, price levels in a structure sorted by price with a FIFO queue per level; near the touch, flat arrays indexed by tick outperform trees because the hot path stays in cache.
  • Where does clearing fit? Downstream and batch: trades flow to the clearinghouse, obligations are netted per member, and settlement happens at T+1. The exchange's responsibility ends at an immutable, sequenced record of what traded.
