Design a real-time gaming leaderboard

Systems design · Data pipelines and observability · Oct 2025

A leaderboard is the scoreboard for an entire player base. Players earn points each match, and the game shows three views of the result: the global top 10, the player's own rank among tens of millions ("you are #8,432,019"), and how their friends are doing, all updated within seconds of a match ending, because the product moment is closing the results screen and seeing your number move. Interviewers like the question because it has a famous right answer, the Redis sorted set, and the interview is really about whether the candidate understands why it is right, what it costs in durability, and what happens when one machine no longer holds the data.

The deceptive part is the rank query. Top 10 is easy in any database; "my exact rank among 26 million" at thousands of queries per second is the operation that quietly rules out the default relational design and pulls a specialized ordered structure into the center of the system. The walkthrough below sizes the problem, shows why the naive approach fails, and then builds outward from the sorted set to durability, ties, seasons, friends, and sharding.

Scope and requirements

Functionally: record points per player per match, serve the global top 10, serve any player's exact current rank, and serve a friends view that ranks a player among their friend list. Scores accumulate over a season, a competitive period of one to a few months, after which the board resets and history is archived. The leaderboard must be server-authoritative, meaning the score comes from the game server's own record of the match rather than anything the client claims, since a leaderboard players can write to directly is a list of cheaters sorted by audacity.

Non-functionally: updates should be visible within about a second, rank reads should return in single-digit milliseconds at the median, and a score accepted from a finished match must never be lost even if the serving layer dies, because players notice missing points faster than they notice almost any other defect. Slight staleness in ranks is fine; losing writes is not.

Sizing the problem

Take 25 million daily active players, each finishing about 10 scored matches per day. That is 250 million score updates per day, and dividing by 86,400 seconds gives roughly 2,900 writes per second on average, with evening peaks plausibly five times that, around 15,000 per second. Reads are larger: every match end fetches the player's rank and the top 10, plus menu visits and friend views, so a 10 to 1 read ratio gives about 29,000 reads per second on average and well over 100,000 at peak. The season's member count is bigger than the daily count: with monthly actives around 26 million, the board holds 26 million entries, and each entry is a player ID plus a score plus ordering structure overhead, which lands near 100 bytes in practice, so the whole board is about 2.6 GB. That number matters because it says one machine's memory can hold the entire ordered structure, which makes the simple architecture viable.

Why the relational instinct fails

The natural first design is a scores table with an index on score, ORDER BY score DESC LIMIT 10 for the top, and a rank query like the one below for position. The top-10 query is fine, since it reads ten entries off the end of the index. The rank query is the problem: counting how many players score higher than you requires walking or aggregating the index from the top to your position, and for a median player that is 13 million index entries examined to produce one integer. At tens of thousands of rank queries per second the database does trillions of entry visits per day to answer a question whose answer changes constantly, and caching barely helps because every write shifts ranks below it. Maintaining a materialized rank column makes reads cheap and writes ruinous instead, since one score update can shift millions of ranks. The structural need is a data structure that keeps members ordered and can answer "how many are above this one" in logarithmic time, and that is exactly what the centerpiece provides.

-- the read that breaks the relational design at scale
SELECT COUNT(*) + 1 AS rank
FROM scores
WHERE season_id = 7 AND points > (SELECT points FROM scores
                                  WHERE season_id = 7 AND player_id = 8231991);

The Redis sorted set

A Redis sorted set is a collection of unique members, player IDs here, each carrying a float score, kept permanently ordered by that score. Internally it pairs a hash table, for finding any member's score in constant time, with a skip list, which is a linked list augmented with express lanes: each member appears in the bottom lane, half of them in a lane above, a quarter in the lane above that, so a search drops from the top lane downward and reaches any position in O(log N) hops, and by recording how many bottom-lane members each express link skips, the structure counts a member's position during the same descent. The operations map one-to-one onto the product: ZINCRBY board 30 player:8231991 adds 30 points, ZREVRANGE board 0 9 WITHSCORES returns the top 10, and ZREVRANK board player:8231991 returns the exact rank, each in O(log N), which at N of 26 million is about 25 hops, microseconds of work. A single Redis node comfortably serves over 100,000 such operations per second, and with the whole board at 2.6 GB, the entire global leaderboard fits on one node with an enormous margin, which is the punchline of the sizing arithmetic.

The architecture around it

Durability is the part that needs direct treatment, because Redis is best operated as cache-grade storage: its persistence options either lose a second or so of writes on a crash or cost latency, and a leaderboard should not be the system that discovers the edge cases. So the sorted set is treated as a fast, rebuildable view, not the system of record. The game server reports a finished match to a score service, which appends the score event to a durable log (Kafka), and the event then flows both to the sorted set via ZINCRBY and to a relational scores table that is the lasting record. The score service applies events idempotently, deduplicating by match ID, so a retried report cannot double-count. If the Redis node dies, a replica promotes, and in the worst case the board is rebuilt by streaming the scores table back in: 26 million entries written in pipelined batches at around 200,000 per second is about 130 seconds, two minutes and change of degraded leaderboard reads served from the database's top-10 query while the rebuild runs.

Score events land in a durable log before anything fast happens; the sorted set serves every rank read, the database is the record that survives, and the dashed path is the two-minute rebuild that makes Redis safe to lose.

Ties, seasons, and friends

Two players on 1,500 points need a deterministic order, and the usual rule is that whoever reached the score first ranks higher. Encoding that into a single float uses a composite score: shift the points into the high bits and put a complemented timestamp in the low bits, complemented so that an earlier time produces a larger value. Redis floats hold 53 bits of integer precision, so giving 20 bits to points (up to about a million) leaves 33 bits for seconds within a season, which covers 272 years, more than enough. Concretely, with a season that started at time zero, a player reaching 1,500 points at second 86,400 stores 1,500 times 233 plus (233 minus 1 minus 86,400); a rival reaching 1,500 a day later stores a strictly smaller value, so the earlier player ranks above them forever until either scores again.

SEASON_BITS = 33  # seconds within season; points live above these bits

def composite(points: int, season_seconds: int) -> float:
    return points * 2**SEASON_BITS + (2**SEASON_BITS - 1 - season_seconds)

def points_of(score: float) -> int:
    return int(score) >> SEASON_BITS

composite(1500, 86_400)   # earlier scorer
composite(1500, 172_800)  # same points a day later: strictly smaller

Seasons are handled by naming, not migration: the board for season 7 lives at key board:s7, the season flip is just the score service starting to write board:s8, and the old key is archived to the database and expired. This makes resets instant and makes "last season's final standings" a cheap historical query rather than a careful in-place operation.

The friends view looks like it needs a personal sorted set per player, and the arithmetic says otherwise. Fetching a friends board on demand means one pipelined batch of a few hundred ZSCORE calls, each a constant-time hash lookup, so one round trip and well under a millisecond of server work, done only when someone actually opens the friends tab. Precomputing per-player friend sets instead means 26 million sorted sets averaging 200 members, around 5 billion entries and several hundred gigabytes of memory, updated on every score event by fanning out to every friend's set, hundreds of writes per match finish. On-demand wins by orders of magnitude at these read rates, and the precomputed design only earns its memory when friend boards are pushed continuously to clients or read vastly more often than they are written, which is not how a results screen behaves.

Beyond one node: sharding the board

Growth or blast-radius policy eventually forbids the single node, and there are two ways to split a sorted set. Range sharding assigns score bands to shards, top scores on shard one, mid scores on shard two, and keeps rank queries cheap because a player's rank is their rank within their shard plus the exact sizes of the shards above; its operational pain is that the bands breathe, since scores inflate over a season, so shards need rebalancing while live, and the top band is also the hot one. Hash sharding assigns players to shards by hashing the player ID, which balances load perfectly and never needs rebalancing; its pain is that no shard knows the global order, so global queries become scatter-gather. The top 10 stays cheap: query each of the three shards for its local top 10, merge the 30 candidates by score, and take 10, which is the second diagram. The exact global rank becomes a fan-out sum, my rank equals the count of better scores on every shard, computed with one ZCOUNT per shard, three small logarithmic queries here; at larger shard counts most systems soften the promise to "rank within a few thousand" or compute exact ranks a little less often and interpolate. Hash sharding with scatter-gather is the design I would pick, named pain and all, because rebalancing a live range-sharded leaderboard during a season is the kind of operation that generates war stories.

Step 1: each hash shard answers ZREVRANGE 0 9 for its local top 10. Step 2: the merger k-way merges the 30 candidates by score. Step 3: the global top 10 is served and cached for a second, since it changes slowly relative to read rate.

Scaling, failures, and operations

The serving layer scales in tiers. The score service is stateless and grows horizontally. Each Redis shard runs with a replica and automatic failover, and a failover loses at most a moment of applied events, which the log-driven applier simply re-applies idempotently, the same property that makes the full rebuild safe. The top 10 deserves its own tiny cache with a one-second TTL, because it is the single hottest read in the system and is identical for every player, so there is no reason to ask the sorted set 100,000 times per second for an answer that changes a few times a second. The database absorbs the write rate easily, 15,000 small inserts per second at peak partitioned by season, and serves history, profiles, and audits rather than hot ranks.

Cheating is an operational requirement, not an afterthought. Scores enter only from game servers, so the client has no write path to subvert, and the score service enforces sanity ceilings per match and per day, flags statistically implausible streaks for review, and keeps the append-only event log as the audit trail that lets an operator answer how any player reached any score. When a cheater is confirmed, their events are tombstoned and their board entry recomputed from the surviving history, which the event-sourced design makes a query plus a ZADD rather than a forensic reconstruction. The failure stories close neatly: a dead score service node is replaced behind the balancer, a dead Redis shard promotes its replica and reconciles from the log, a dead database follower is routine, and the one scenario that costs real product pain, losing both a shard and its replica, costs two minutes of rebuild from the system of record rather than any lost score.

Follow-up questions

  • Why is the rank query hard for a relational database? Rank is a count of everyone above you, which is an index walk proportional to your depth in the board, millions of entries for a median player, repeated tens of thousands of times per second. A skip list answers the same question in about 25 hops.
  • What exactly does the sorted set cost in memory? Around 100 bytes per member counting the hash entry, skip list node, and pointers: 26 million members is roughly 2.6 GB, which is why one node suffices for the whole player base.
  • What if Redis loses everything? Nothing is authoritative there: replay the scores table in pipelined batches at about 200,000 writes per second, roughly two minutes for 26 million entries, serving top-10 from the database meanwhile.
  • How do you order players with equal points? A composite float: points in the high bits, complemented season-seconds in the low bits, so earlier achievement ranks higher deterministically and the decode back to points is a bit shift.
  • Why not precompute every player's friends board? Five billion set entries and hundreds of writes fanned out per match finish, against one pipelined batch of ZSCORE calls when a friends tab actually opens. Precomputation only pays when friend views are pushed or read overwhelmingly more than scores change.
  • Range or hash sharding when one node is not enough? Hash: perfect balance and no live rebalancing, paying with scatter-gather top-K and fan-out rank sums. Range keeps ranks local but makes the operator re-band a live leaderboard as scores inflate, which is the worse trade in practice.
