Design similar listings on vacation rental platforms

Systems design · Machine learning systems · Dec 2025

On every listing page of a vacation rental platform there is a carousel near the bottom: "similar listings you might like." A traveler lands on a two-bedroom apartment in Lisbon, scrolls past the photos and reviews, and the carousel offers a handful of alternatives in the same neighborhood at a comparable price. It looks like a small widget, but at Airbnb's published numbers, item-to-item recommendation of this kind drives a meaningful share of all bookings, because it matches how people actually shop: not by typing better queries, but by hopping from one plausible option to the next until one fits. The design question is how to decide, given the listing being viewed, which other listings belong in those few slots.

The framing temptation is to define similarity by attributes: same city, same number of bedrooms, price within 20 percent. That baseline is worth building and worth beating, because attributes miss what sessions reveal. Two listings can differ on paper, a houseboat and a loft, and still be considered by exactly the same kind of traveler in the same shopping session, while two near-identical condos can serve completely different trips. The stronger definition is behavioral: two listings are similar if the same kinds of sessions consider them both, and the centerpiece of this design is a way to learn that similarity directly from click logs.

Scope and requirements

The product surface is the carousel on the listing page: given an anchor listing (the one being viewed), the user's search context (dates, number of guests), and a market of perhaps tens of thousands of listings, return around a dozen alternatives, ranked, within roughly 100 milliseconds of server time so the page does not wait on it. Assume a platform with about 5 million active listings worldwide and hundreds of millions of listing-page views per day. Out of scope: search ranking itself, pricing suggestions, and the booking flow. One requirement that distinguishes this from recommending movies must be stated early: most travelers arrive with dates, and a recommendation that is unavailable for those dates is worthless no matter how similar it is, so availability is a hard constraint in candidate generation rather than a feature the ranker may trade away.

Framing the problem as a machine learning task

The task splits cleanly in two. First, learn a representation of listings such that behaviorally similar listings are close together, which turns "find similar listings" into "find nearest neighbors." Second, rank the retrieved neighbors for this particular user and trip with a small supervised model predicting the probability that the traveler clicks (and ultimately books) the suggested listing. The alternative of training one end-to-end model to score every (anchor, candidate) pair is cleaner on paper, but pair scoring cannot be run against a whole market per page view, and a learned embedding space gives the retrieval step for free.

Embeddings from click sessions

The centerpiece technique is borrowed from language modeling. The skip-gram objective, the idea behind word2vec, learns a vector for every word by training a tiny model to predict which words appear near each other in real sentences; words used in similar surroundings end up with nearby vectors, which is why "hotel" and "motel" land close together. The transfer to listings is direct: a user's click session, the sequence of listings they viewed in one sitting, plays the role of a sentence, and each listing plays the role of a word. If thousands of sessions that contain the Lisbon apartment also contain a particular loft three streets away, the training pushes their vectors together, with no human ever defining what made them alike. Each listing gets a vector of perhaps 32 numbers, and the geometry of that space encodes neighborhood, style, and price band simultaneously, because those are exactly the things that co-occur in real shopping behavior. Training uses negative sampling, contrasting the listings that truly co-occurred against randomly drawn ones, and Airbnb's published refinement adds negatives drawn from the same market, since random negatives are usually from other cities and teach the model little beyond geography it already knows.

The second refinement from that work is the booked listing as global context. Sessions that end in a booking are the ones that encode satisfied intent, so during training the eventually-booked listing is treated as a neighbor of every listing in that session, not just the ones within a few clicks of it. Intuitively, every click in a converted session gets pulled slightly toward the thing the traveler finally chose, which tilts the whole space from "what people browse together" toward "what leads to bookings," and that tilt is the difference between a curiosity engine and a revenue engine.

New listings have no sessions yet, and the cold-start answer is simple and works: assign a new listing the average of the vectors of a few geographically nearest listings of the same property type and similar price band, for example the three closest comparable apartments. Location dominates real similarity in this domain, so the approximation is good enough to bootstrap impressions, and the listing earns its own trained vector in the next refresh once sessions accumulate.

Candidate generation

With every listing embedded, candidate generation is a nearest-neighbor lookup. Exact search over 5 million vectors per page view is unnecessary; an approximate nearest neighbor index, a data structure that returns almost-nearest vectors in well under a millisecond by organizing them into a navigable graph or partitioned cells, retrieves the top 200 neighbors of the anchor with recall in the high nineties. The index is pre-filtered by market, since a Lisbon anchor should pull Lisbon candidates and the embedding space already concentrates neighbors locally, and then the hard constraints apply: candidates must be available for the user's dates (checked against the availability calendar index), must fit the guest count, and must pass status filters such as not being the anchor itself or already booked. The date filter bites hard in high season, perhaps cutting 200 neighbors down to 80, which is exactly why retrieval starts wide and why availability runs here rather than in the ranker, where a soft penalty could let an unavailable listing slip through.

Ranking the candidates

The surviving candidates are scored by a small supervised model, a GBDT or compact neural net, predicting the probability of a click from the carousel, with features that are concrete and mostly relational to the anchor: the price ratio between candidate and anchor (a candidate at 1.1 times the anchor's price ranks differently than one at 2.5 times), embedding cosine similarity, distance to the anchor in meters, review score and review count, capacity fit against the guest count, and instant-book availability. Anchoring the features to the listing being viewed matters because the anchor is the best single statement of what this traveler wants right now, stronger in this moment than their long-term history.

Data, labels, and offline evaluation

Labels come from the carousel's own logs: an impression of a candidate that the user clicks is a positive, with a booking that follows counting as a stronger positive (weighted higher or modeled as a second task), and impressed-but-not-clicked candidates as negatives, logged with their position so position bias, the tendency of earlier slots to attract clicks regardless of fit, can be controlled by training with position and serving with it fixed.

Offline evaluation uses held-out sessions split by time, training embeddings and ranker on weeks 1 to 8 and evaluating on week 9, since random splits would let the model see the future of the very sessions it is tested on. The retrieval stage is measured by recall@K: for each held-out case where a user viewing anchor A went on to click listing L, check whether L appears in the top K neighbors of A. If the embedding index places the clicked listing in its top 50 for 62 percent of cases while the same-neighborhood-and-price heuristic manages 31 percent, the embeddings have roughly doubled the ceiling on what the ranker can ever show. The ranking stage is measured by the average position of the clicked or booked listing among scored candidates, where moving the eventual booking from average rank 9 to rank 4 is the kind of offline result that usually survives the online test.

The serving architecture

The serving path is short, which is part of the design's appeal. The listing page request arrives with the anchor ID, dates, and guest count; the anchor's vector is fetched and the ANN index queried for 200 neighbors within the market; hard filters check availability and capacity; the ranker scores the survivors with features fetched from the feature store; and a final diversity pass assembles the carousel. The budget is comfortable: about 1 millisecond for the ANN query, 10 to 20 for the availability checks done as one batched calendar lookup, 10 for feature assembly, and a few for scoring 80 candidates, landing well under the 100 millisecond envelope. Diversity earns its pass because nearest neighbors are sometimes too near: ten units in the same condo building are each excellent candidates and a terrible carousel together. A simple greedy rule fixes it, selecting candidates in score order while applying a penalty proportional to each candidate's maximum embedding similarity against the already-selected set, so the second unit from the same building drops below a strong option in the next neighborhood.

The serving path. The ANN index retrieves 200 behavioral neighbors of the anchor, hard availability and capacity filters cut them to around 80, the ranker orders them for this trip, and a diversity pass keeps the carousel from filling with near-duplicates.

The embedding training pipeline

The offline side runs nightly. Raw click logs are sessionized, grouping each user's views into sequences split on 30 minutes of inactivity and dropping trivial sessions with a single click, then the skip-gram trainer runs over the resulting corpus, on the order of hundreds of millions of sequences in a mature platform, with the booked-listing global context applied to converted sessions. The output is one 32-dimensional vector per listing, around 640 MB of floats for 5 million listings, small enough to hold in memory everywhere it is needed. The ANN index is rebuilt from the fresh vectors and swapped atomically with the version of the vectors it was built from, and that versioning discipline matters more than it looks: vectors from different training runs live in different coordinate systems even when trained on nearly the same data, so mixing yesterday's index with today's vectors produces silently wrong neighbors rather than an error. The cold-start path runs inside the same pipeline, assigning neighbor-average vectors to listings created since the last run so they enter the index immediately rather than waiting to be browsed.

The nightly training pipeline. Click logs become session sequences, the skip-gram trainer with booked-listing context produces versioned listing vectors, and the ANN index is rebuilt and swapped atomically. The dashed path bootstraps new listings with the average of nearby comparable listings.

Online evaluation and operations

The online metrics are carousel click-through rate and, more importantly, downstream booking rate attributed to carousel clicks, read through A/B tests against the incumbent. The offline-online gap shows up here in an instructive way: an embedding change can lift retrieval recall substantially while carousel click-through barely moves, which usually means the ranker, not retrieval, is the binding constraint, and that diagnosis is worth stating in an interview because it shows the stages are evaluated as a system rather than in isolation. Booking is the metric that decides launches, since click-through alone rewards eye-catching but unbookable suggestions.

Freshness on the supply side needs deliberate help. A new listing with a borrowed vector still has no reviews and no track record, so the ranker scores it timidly, and without intervention it stays unseen and never accumulates the sessions that would earn it a real vector. A small exploration boost for young listings, capped in impressions, breaks that loop, and the marketplace depends on it because hosts who get no visibility in their first month tend to leave. On the monitoring side, the things that rot quietly are the inputs: session volume per market, the fraction of listings whose vectors changed sharply between refreshes (a spike means a training problem rather than a market shift), ANN recall measured against exact search on a sample, and the share of carousels where fewer than 12 candidates survived the availability filter, which is the early signal that retrieval needs to widen in tight markets. Retraining cadence is nightly for embeddings and weekly for the ranker, with the usual discipline that the ranker trains on features logged at serving time so that offline and online disagree as little as possible.

Follow-up questions

  • Why learn similarity from sessions instead of attributes? Attributes describe what a listing is; sessions reveal what travelers treat as substitutes, which is the question the carousel actually answers. The attribute baseline roughly halves retrieval recall in the worked example above, and the gap is the behavior the attributes cannot see.
  • Why is the booked listing used as global context? It tilts the embedding space from co-browsing toward conversion: every click in a session that ended in a booking is pulled toward the booked listing, so neighborhoods in the space organize around what people choose, not just what they look at.
  • How do you handle a brand-new listing? Assign it the average vector of a few nearest listings with the same type and price band so it enters the index immediately, then give it a capped exploration boost so it accumulates the sessions that earn it a trained vector.
  • Why filter availability in candidate generation rather than rank with it? Because for a traveler with dates it is a hard constraint: a perfect listing that is booked for those dates has zero value, and a soft penalty in the ranker would still let it occupy a slot some fraction of the time.
  • Why version the embeddings and rebuild the index together? Different training runs produce different coordinate systems even on similar data, so vectors and index must come from the same run. Mixing versions degrades neighbors silently, with no error to catch it.
  • What changes for a viewer with no dates selected? The availability filter relaxes to general near-term availability, and the ranker leans more on the anchor-relative features. The system degrades gracefully because the anchor, not the user profile, carries most of the intent signal.
