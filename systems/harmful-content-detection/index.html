Design a harmful content detection system

Systems design · Machine learning systems · Dec 2025

Every large social platform runs an integrity system: the machinery that detects posts violating policy, whether that is graphic violence, hate speech, self-harm content, sexual material, or spam, and acts on them before they reach people. It is among the hardest ML system designs an interviewer can pose, and the difficulty is structural rather than algorithmic. The users producing the worst content actively adapt to evade detection, so the data distribution fights back. Violations are a sliver of total volume, so the class imbalance is severe. Errors harm people in both directions, since a missed violation can mean a person sees the worst image of their life while a false removal silences legitimate speech, and the policies themselves differ by category, language, and jurisdiction. No single model solves this; the design is a layered system with humans inside it.

The hard parts, and the places this walkthrough spends its time, are the operating point machinery that converts model scores into tiered actions, the human review loop that is part of the architecture rather than an afterthought, and the latency split between what must run synchronously at post time and what can follow minutes later.

Scope and requirements

The system watches every new post, text, images, video, and their combinations, on a platform handling on the order of one billion posts per day, roughly 12,000 per second on average and several times that at peak. It must score content against a set of policy categories, take automated action where confidence justifies it, route uncertain cases to human reviewers, and support appeals. Policy categories carry different stakes: imminent-harm categories such as credible threats and self-harm need the fastest path and the highest recall, while spam tolerates more error in both directions. A reasonable violation base rate to carry through the design is 0.1 percent of posts, which at a billion posts per day still means a million violating posts daily. In scope are detection, action, and the review loop; out of scope here are policy authoring itself, advertiser content, and live-stream moderation, which shares the models but has its own latency regime.

Framing the problem as a machine learning task

The core framing is multi-label classification per policy category over multi-modal inputs. Multi-label means each post receives an independent score for every category rather than one winner-take-all class, because a single post can be hateful and violent and spammy at once, and the downstream actions differ per category, so the system needs each probability separately. Multi-modal means the inputs span text, image, video, audio, and context such as the posting account's history, since modern violations live in the interaction between modalities: an innocuous photo with a sinister caption, or hateful text rendered inside an image to dodge text models.

Combining modalities raises the fusion question, and the two options deserve plain definitions. Early fusion combines the raw learned representations, feeding text and image embeddings into one model that scores the post jointly, which is the only way to catch meaning that exists between modalities. Late fusion runs a separate model per modality and combines their scores at the end, which is weaker on cross-modal meaning but much easier to operate: when a late-fusion system misfires, the per-modality scores show which component erred, and one model can be retrained without disturbing the others. A defensible production answer uses late fusion as the workhorse because its failures can be localized to a component, and adds an early-fusion multimodal model where cross-modal violations concentrate, with the framing alternative, a single end-to-end model for everything, rejected on operability grounds rather than accuracy ones.

Data and labels

Gold labels come from human review decisions: trained reviewers applying written policy to queued content, producing the per-category judgments that train and evaluate every model in the system. This supply is expensive, rate-limited, and noisy, since policy lines are genuinely contested and reviewer agreement on categories like hate speech is well below perfect, so the pipeline measures inter-reviewer agreement, double-reviews a sample to estimate label quality, and treats appeal outcomes, where a second reviewer overturns the first, as a direct measurement of label noise.

The class imbalance is the other defining property. At a 0.1 percent violation rate, a random training sample of a million posts contains a thousand positives, and a model can score 99.9 percent accuracy by calling everything clean, which is why accuracy never appears on an integrity dashboard. Training handles the imbalance by downsampling the clean class, keeping every labeled violation while sampling perhaps one clean post per hundred, and then reweighting so predicted probabilities remain calibrated, or equivalently by upweighting the rare positives in the loss. Evaluation must resist the same trap by reporting precision and recall per category rather than any pooled accuracy, and the labeled evaluation sets are stratified samples enriched with hard cases, kept frozen so quarter-over-quarter comparisons mean something. User reports supply a further label stream, abundant and very noisy, since most reported posts violate nothing, but useful as a candidate generator for review and as a weak training signal. Multilingual and cultural coverage is fundamentally a data problem: the categories exist in every language, while labels concentrate in a few, so the label budget is allocated by language and region deliberately, and cross-lingual transfer from multilingual encoders is the bridge while labeled data catches up. Coded language and dialect shift make a model trained on one region's data quietly wrong in another, which sliced metrics surface and only local labels fix.

Known-content matching

Before any classifier runs, the cheapest and most precise layer asks whether this content has been seen before, because much of the worst material is re-shared rather than new. Perceptual hashing computes a fingerprint of an image or video designed so that re-encoding, resizing, cropping, and watermarking produce the same or a nearby hash, in the style of PhotoDNA, and matching that fingerprint against databases of known violating media gives near-certain detection at microseconds of cost. Industry hash-sharing programs extend the database across platforms for the most severe categories. One step softer, embedding similarity, vector representations compared in an approximate nearest neighbor index, catches near-duplicates that survive heavier edits than hashing tolerates, at slightly lower precision. Together these layers remove a large fraction of total violating volume before a single expensive classifier runs, and they are the reason the classifiers can afford to be heavy: they only face content the cheap layers could not dismiss.

Model architecture

The classifier stack is tiered by cost. The synchronous tier, which runs while the user waits for the post to publish, contains the hash and similarity matchers plus a fast text model, a distilled transformer scoring all text fields in a few milliseconds, and a lightweight image classifier. The asynchronous tier runs within minutes of publication: full-size multimodal transformers, video models that sample and score frames with audio, and context models that incorporate account history and propagation patterns. Each category gets its own calibrated head, meaning a predicted 0.9 corresponds to roughly 90 percent of such posts being true violations, because everything downstream consumes these scores as probabilities and miscalibration silently corrupts every threshold. Blocking every post on the heavy models would add seconds of latency to every one of a billion daily posts to catch a fraction of a percent, which is why total synchronous screening is unaffordable and the design accepts a window of minutes during which a violating post may be live, with the synchronous tier sized to catch the known and the obvious instantly.

Offline evaluation

Per-category precision-recall curves are the core offline artifact. Precision is the share of flagged posts that truly violate; recall is the share of true violations that get flagged, and the curve traces the trade as the score threshold moves. Because thresholds drive actions with different costs, the numbers that matter are read at specific points: precision at the auto-remove threshold, where false positives mean wrongly silenced users, and recall at the review threshold, where false negatives mean harm reaching people. A small worked example grounds it: if the violence model at threshold 0.92 has precision 0.98 and recall 0.45, then of 1,000 true violations in a day's traffic it auto-removes 450, and those removals arrive with about 9 wrongful ones (450 correct removals being 98 percent of roughly 459 total). Calibration is checked alongside, by binning predictions and comparing predicted probability to observed violation rate per bin, and evaluation slices by language, region, and content type, since aggregate curves hide exactly the failures that matter. Validation splits are time-based, training on past months and evaluating on the most recent, because adversarial drift makes random splits flatter the model with yesterday's evasions.

The layered serving architecture

At post time, the synchronous gauntlet runs in tens of milliseconds: hash and similarity matching, then the fast text and image models. A known-database match acts immediately, removing the post before anyone sees it. The fast models' scores feed the threshold router, and the post publishes unless the synchronous tier itself crossed an action line. Minutes later the heavy asynchronous models deliver their richer scores to the same router, which can then remove, demote, or queue content that the fast tier let through. The router is the system's heart: per category, scores map to bands, and bands map to actions, auto-remove at high confidence, human review at middle confidence, demotion (reducing distribution while review catches up) below that, and nothing at the bottom.

The layered path: known-content matching acts instantly, fast synchronous models gate publication, heavy asynchronous models deliver richer scores minutes later, and the per-category threshold router fans out to tiered actions. The dashed return path is the system feeding itself labels.

The operating point machinery

Choosing the bands is reading points off the precision-recall curve and attaching costs to them, separately per category. Continuing the violence example: threshold 0.92 (precision 0.98, recall 0.45) defines the auto-remove band, because the platform has decided roughly two wrongful removals per hundred is the most user trust will bear for this category. Dropping to 0.60 raises recall to 0.75 at precision 0.80, and that band, 0.60 to 0.92, routes to human review: of the day's 1,000 true violations, the band catches another 300, and at 80 percent precision the queue receives about 375 posts to find them, which is exactly how review headcount gets sized from model quality. From 0.30 to 0.60 the score still carries signal but not enough to justify either removal or scarce reviewer time, so distribution is limited while the post awaits any stronger evidence, and below 0.30 nothing happens. Imminent-harm categories shift every line toward recall and route to specialist queues with response-time targets in minutes; spam shifts toward automation since both error types are cheap. The review queue itself is prioritized by expected harm, severity of the category times predicted reach of the post, because reviewing a borderline post heading for a million views matters more than a certain violation nobody will see. Reviewer decisions flow back as fresh gold labels, and appeals run as a second pass with separate reviewers, both correcting individual errors and measuring the system's false-removal rate from the inside.

One category's bands, numbered top to bottom: the highest scores remove automatically at audited precision, the middle band buys recall with reviewer time, the lower band limits distribution, and the rest pass. The dashed loop returns reviewer judgments as the next training set.

Online evaluation and operations

Two north-star metrics define success. Prevalence measures how much violating content users actually see, estimated by sampling viewed content and human-labeling the sample, expressed as violating views per ten thousand views; it is the user's experience of the system, and it weights a million-view miss a million times more than an unseen one. Proactive rate measures the share of actioned violations the system caught before any user report, which tracks whether detection leads harm or trails it. Around them sit per-category automated-action precision audited by sampling, review queue latency and backlog, appeal overturn rates as the running measure of wrongful action, and reviewer agreement. A/B tests of new models read these online metrics rather than offline curves, since a model that wins offline can lose on prevalence by shifting errors onto high-reach content.

Operations are dominated by adversarial adaptation. Evading users move from plain slurs to leetspeak and homoglyphs, render text inside images, recut and filter known videos, and develop coded language that means nothing to yesterday's training set, so retraining runs on a fast cadence, weekly or better for adversarial categories, with monitoring tuned to the signatures of evasion: prediction score distributions drifting downward per category, report rates rising while model catch rates fall, and clusters of near-duplicate content scoring just under thresholds. Hash databases update continuously as reviewers confirm new violating media. Feature and prediction monitoring guards the boring failure modes too, missing-modality rates, calibration drift, and per-language score distributions, because a feature pipeline that silently drops image embeddings for one language's traffic is indistinguishable from an evasion wave in the topline numbers until the slices are inspected.

Follow-up questions

  • Why not block every post until all models clear it? The heavy models add seconds of latency and run at a cost that a billion daily posts cannot pay synchronously, for a violation rate of 0.1 percent. The design accepts a minutes-long exposure window for content the cheap synchronous tier cannot catch, and shrinks that window with prioritized async scoring.
  • Why per-category thresholds instead of one global score? Because costs differ per category: a wrongful spam removal is trivial while a wrongful hate-speech removal silences someone, and a missed self-harm post is a different magnitude of harm than a missed spam post. Each category's precision-recall curve is read with its own cost model.
  • Early or late fusion for multimodal posts? Late fusion as the backbone, because per-modality scores localize failures and components retrain independently; an early-fusion multimodal model added specifically for the cross-modal violations late fusion structurally misses, like benign images with weaponized captions.
  • How does the system handle a 0.1 percent positive rate in training? Downsample the clean class aggressively, then reweight or recalibrate so output probabilities stay meaningful, and never report accuracy: per-category precision and recall on a frozen, enriched evaluation set are the only numbers that move decisions.
  • What keeps the human review budget from exploding? The band boundaries: review receives only the score range where reviewer judgment changes outcomes, the queue is prioritized by severity times predicted reach, and known-content matching keeps re-shared material out of the queue entirely. Review capacity is a sized, budgeted component, not an overflow drain.
  • How do you know the system is winning against adversaries? Prevalence trending down and proactive rate trending up, sliced by category and language. Model-level metrics can look stable while coded language erodes real coverage, which is why the sampled, human-labeled prevalence estimate is the metric of record.
