SLOs, SLIs, and error budgets

Notes · reliability · Jun 2026

"Is the service healthy?" is a useless question until you make it numeric. SLIs, SLOs, and error budgets are the vocabulary that turns reliability from a feeling into a measurement you can act on. I put all of this into practice in my Uptime & SLO Monitor.

SLI, SLO, target

  • An SLI (indicator) is a measured number: the fraction of successful requests, or the p95 latency.
  • An SLO (objective) is the target for an SLI: "99.9% of requests succeed over 30 days."
  • An SLA is the contractual version with consequences; the SLO is the internal goal you actually engineer toward.

The error budget

The insight that makes SLOs useful: if the target is 99.9%, then 0.1% of failure is not just tolerated, it is budgeted. 

error_budget = 1 − SLO        (e.g. 0.1% over the window)
budget_consumed = error_rate / error_budget
budget_remaining = 1 − budget_consumed

That budget is a shared currency. Plenty of budget left? Ship faster, take risks. Budget nearly spent? Freeze features and stabilize. It replaces "should we deploy?" arguments with a number both dev and ops agree on.

Burn rate

Burn rate is how fast you are spending the budget, relative to spending it exactly evenly over the window. A burn rate of 1 exhausts the budget precisely at the end of the window; a burn rate of 14 means you would burn the entire 30-day budget in about two days.

Multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerting

Alerting on raw burn rate is either too twitchy or too slow. The Google SRE approach pairs a long and a short window and fires only when both exceed a threshold. The long window confirms the budget is genuinely burning; the short window makes the alert fire quickly and, just as importantly, reset quickly once the incident is over. A high burn rate pages a human; a slow leak only files a ticket.

Good alerting wakes you only when the budget is actually in danger. Everything else is noise, and noise is how on-call rotations burn out.
