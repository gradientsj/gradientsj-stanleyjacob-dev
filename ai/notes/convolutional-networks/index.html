How convolutional networks see images

Notes · convolutional neural networks · Jun 2026

Convolutional networks were the architecture that made deep learning work on images, and they are still the backbone of a lot of vision today, including the kind of classifier behind my visual defect inspection project.

Convolution

Instead of connecting every pixel to every neuron, a convolution slides a small filter across the image and reuses the same weights everywhere. That gives two things for free: translation equivariance (a feature is detected wherever it appears) and a huge reduction in parameters compared to a dense layer.

Pooling and receptive fields

Pooling and strided convolutions downsample the feature maps, so deeper layers cover a larger receptive field of the original image. Early layers fire on edges and textures; deeper layers compose those into parts and whole objects.

Going deep with residuals

AlexNet showed in 2012 that a deep CNN trained on a GPU could crush prior vision methods. Naively stacking more layers then stopped helping, until ResNet added residual (skip) connections that let gradients flow through very deep networks, making hundreds of layers trainable.

CNNs vs vision transformers

Vision transformers split an image into patches and apply attention, and now match or beat CNNs given enough data. CNNs still win on data efficiency and bring a useful built-in bias toward locality, so the two are often combined rather than treated as rivals.

For most practical image tasks, a pretrained CNN or ViT plus a little fine-tuning gets you most of the way, which is exactly the recipe in the defect-inspection project.
