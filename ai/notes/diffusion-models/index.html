How diffusion models generate images

Notes · diffusion generative models · Jun 2026

Diffusion models generate data by learning to reverse a process that gradually destroys it. The idea is counterintuitive but powerful: rather than trying to map noise to an image in a single leap, which is a hard and unstable problem, you break it into many small and easy steps. During training the model only ever has to remove a small amount of noise, and during generation it applies that one modest skill hundreds of times, turning pure noise into a coherent sample. This decomposition into many gentle steps is a large part of why diffusion produces such high-quality and diverse images.

There are two processes to keep separate in your head. The forward process is fixed and only adds noise, so it requires no learning at all. The reverse process is the one that is learned, and it removes noise one step at a time. The entire model is a single network trained to predict the noise present in a partially corrupted input, and everything else in the method is bookkeeping arranged around that one prediction.

The forward process

The forward process takes a clean sample and adds a small amount of Gaussian noise repeatedly, over a fixed number of steps, according to a schedule that sets how much noise enters at each step. After enough steps the sample is statistically indistinguishable from pure noise, having lost all of its structure. A convenient property of Gaussian noise is that these steps compose in closed form, so you can jump directly to the noise level of any step in a single operation: scale the original by the square root of the cumulative signal and add noise scaled by its complement. That closed form is precisely what makes training efficient, because you never have to simulate the whole chain to get a training example.

The reverse process

The reverse process undoes one noising step at a time. Instead of predicting the slightly cleaner image directly, the standard formulation trains the network to predict the noise that was added, which turns out to be an easier and better-scaled target. Given that prediction, a simple formula recovers the mean of the previous, less noisy state, and a small amount of fresh noise is added back at every step except the last. The network is conditioned on the timestep so that it knows how much noise it is currently supposed to remove, since the right amount to subtract changes dramatically between an almost-clean image and near-pure noise.

loss = mean( (eps - eps_theta(x_t, t))**2 )   # predict the added noise

The training objective

Training is strikingly simple, which is one of diffusion's great practical advantages. You pick a random training image and a random timestep, use the closed-form forward step to corrupt the image to that noise level, and ask the network to predict the noise that was added. The loss is just the mean squared error between the predicted and the actual noise, averaged over many images and timesteps. There is no adversarial game as in a GAN and no separate discriminator that can collapse, which is a large part of why diffusion training is so stable and reproducible.

Sampling: DDPM and DDIM

Generation starts from pure Gaussian noise and repeatedly applies the learned denoiser. The original DDPM sampler takes many small stochastic steps, which is faithful to the math but slow, often requiring hundreds or even a thousand network evaluations to produce one image. DDIM reformulates the reverse process so that it is deterministic and lets you skip steps, trading a little quality for a large speedup, so a good sample can come from twenty to fifty steps instead. Sampler design remains an active area precisely because each step is a full forward pass through a large network, so halving the step count nearly halves the cost.

Conditioning and classifier-free guidance

Unconditional diffusion produces plausible images but offers no control over what they contain. To condition generation on a text prompt, the prompt is encoded and fed into the denoiser through cross-attention, so the denoising at every step is steered toward the description. Classifier-free guidance sharpens that control: the model is trained to denoise both with and without the prompt, and at sampling time the two predictions are extrapolated apart, which amplifies the prompt's influence at some cost to diversity. A single guidance-scale knob then lets you trade faithfulness to the prompt against variety in the output.

Latent diffusion

Running diffusion directly on high-resolution pixels is expensive, because every one of the many steps has to operate on a large array. Latent diffusion, the approach behind Stable Diffusion, first compresses images into a much smaller latent space with a pretrained autoencoder, runs the entire diffusion process in that compact space, and only decodes back to pixels at the very end. This cuts the cost by a large factor and is what made high-resolution text-to-image generation practical on commodity hardware. The same recipe of diffusing in a compressed learned space now appears for audio, video, and other modalities.

Step by step

  1. Fix a noise schedule and precompute the cumulative product of the alphas.
  2. Forward: jump any image to noise level t in one closed-form step.
  3. Train the network to predict the noise that was added, using a mean-squared-error loss.
  4. Reverse: each step subtracts the predicted noise and adds a little back.
  5. Sample by starting from pure noise and denoising from the last step down to the first.
import numpy as np

T = 1000
betas = np.linspace(1e-4, 0.02, T)            # noise schedule
alphas = 1.0 - betas
alpha_bar = np.cumprod(alphas)

def q_sample(x0, t, noise):                    # forward: jump to noise level t
    return np.sqrt(alpha_bar[t]) * x0 + np.sqrt(1 - alpha_bar[t]) * noise

def training_loss(model, x0):
    t = np.random.randint(T)
    noise = np.random.randn(*x0.shape)
    xt = q_sample(x0, t, noise)
    return ((model(xt, t) - noise) ** 2).mean()    # predict the noise

def p_sample_step(model, x, t):                # reverse: one denoising step
    eps = model(x, t)
    mean = (x - betas[t] / np.sqrt(1 - alpha_bar[t]) * eps) / np.sqrt(alphas[t])
    return mean + (np.sqrt(betas[t]) * np.random.randn(*x.shape) if t > 0 else 0)

def sample(model, shape):
    x = np.random.randn(*shape)                # start from pure noise
    for t in reversed(range(T)):
        x = p_sample_step(model, x, t)
    return x

Complexity (time and space)

Cost is dominated by sampling: generating one image takes the number of denoising steps times the cost of a single forward pass, so DDPM at a thousand steps is roughly twenty to fifty times more expensive than DDIM at twenty to fifty steps. Training cost scales with network size and dataset like any deep model, but each step is cheap because the target, the added noise, is free to compute. Latent diffusion reduces both training and sampling by working on arrays that are often eight times smaller along each spatial dimension, which is roughly a sixty-four-fold reduction in the number of elements processed per step.

Worked example

The forward closed form scales the original signal by the square root of the cumulative alpha and the noise by its complement. As the timestep grows, the signal fades to almost nothing and the input becomes essentially pure noise, which is the state sampling starts from:

import numpy as np
T = 1000
betas = np.linspace(1e-4, 0.02, T)
alpha_bar = np.cumprod(1 - betas)

for t in [0, 50, 200, 600, 999]:
    sig = np.sqrt(alpha_bar[t])
    noise = np.sqrt(1 - alpha_bar[t])
    print(f"t={t:4d}  signal={sig:.3f}  noise={noise:.3f}")

# t=   0  signal=1.000  noise=0.010
# t=  50  signal=0.985  noise=0.173
# t= 200  signal=0.810  noise=0.586
# t= 600  signal=0.160  noise=0.987
# t= 999  signal=0.006  noise=1.000

Follow-up questions

  • Why predict the noise instead of the clean image? Predicting the added noise is an easier, better-scaled regression target and empirically trains more stably than predicting the image directly.
  • Why is diffusion more stable to train than a GAN? It optimizes a plain regression (mean squared error on the noise) with no adversarial min-max game and no discriminator that can collapse.
  • DDPM versus DDIM? DDPM uses many small stochastic steps for high quality but is slow; DDIM is deterministic and skips steps for far fewer network evaluations.
  • What does classifier-free guidance do? It extrapolates between the prompt-conditioned and unconditioned predictions to strengthen adherence to the prompt, at some cost to diversity.
  • Why run diffusion in a latent space? Diffusing in a compressed latent instead of raw pixels cuts the per-step cost dramatically, which is what makes high-resolution generation affordable.
