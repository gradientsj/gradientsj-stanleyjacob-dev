What it takes to train an LLM

Notes · training large language models · Jun 2026

Training a large language model is, at its core, teaching a network to predict the next token in a sequence. That single objective, repeated over an enormous amount of text, is enough to produce models that write code, answer questions, and translate, because predicting the next token well turns out to require absorbing grammar, facts, and patterns of reasoning. The conceptual recipe is short, but almost all of the actual difficulty is engineering: assembling and cleaning the data, fitting the model across many machines, and keeping a run that lasts days or weeks numerically stable.

It helps to separate the pipeline into stages, because each has its own failure modes and the cost of a mistake grows with scale. First the data is gathered and filtered, then it is turned into tokens, then a loss is defined and optimized, and finally the pretrained model is adapted to follow instructions. A flaw early in that chain can quietly waste a great deal of compute before the loss curve reveals anything is wrong, which is why careful, incremental validation matters so much.

Data is the real lever

The single biggest influence on a model's quality is its data, not its architecture. Pretraining corpora are assembled from web crawls, books, code, and curated sources, then aggressively cleaned: deduplicated so the model does not simply memorize repeated passages, stripped of boilerplate and low-quality text, and filtered for harmful content. The mixture matters as much as the volume, since adding code tends to improve reasoning and adding many languages improves multilingual ability. Because a model can only ever learn what is present in its data, this curation is where a large share of the real work, and the competitive advantage, actually lives.

Tokenization

Models do not read characters or whole words; they read tokens, which are subword units produced by a tokenizer. Byte-pair encoding is the usual choice: it starts from individual bytes and repeatedly merges the most frequent adjacent pair into a new symbol, building a fixed vocabulary of common fragments. This strikes a useful balance, since frequent words collapse to a single token while rare words split into a handful of pieces, and because it can always fall back to raw bytes, nothing is ever truly out of vocabulary. Vocabulary size is itself a trade-off: a larger vocabulary shortens sequences and makes attention cheaper, but it enlarges the embedding and output projection layers.

The objective

At every position the model outputs a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary, and training minimizes the cross-entropy between that distribution and the token that actually came next. Cross-entropy rewards placing high probability on the correct token and penalizes confident mistakes heavily, which is exactly the pressure you want. Because of causal masking, every position is a separate prediction, so one forward and backward pass over a length-n sequence yields n training signals at once. The loss is often reported as perplexity, the exponentiated cross-entropy, which can be read intuitively as the effective number of tokens the model is choosing between at each step.

Optimization

The optimizer of choice is AdamW, which adapts the step size for each parameter using running averages of the gradient and its squared magnitude, and applies weight decay separately from that adaptive update. The learning rate is never constant: a short warmup ramps it up to avoid early instability, after which a cosine schedule decays it smoothly toward a small final value. Two further safeguards are standard at scale, namely gradient clipping, which caps the global gradient norm so a single bad batch cannot blow up the weights, and mixed precision in bf16, which halves memory and speeds up matrix multiplies while keeping a numerically safe range. Tuning these together is the difference between a loss curve that descends cleanly and one that diverges hours into the run.

Scaling laws

How big a model, how much data, and how much compute should be combined is governed by scaling laws, empirical power-law relationships between loss and scale. The influential Chinchilla result showed that many large models had been undertrained, and that for a fixed compute budget it is better to train a somewhat smaller model on far more data, with parameters and tokens scaled together at roughly twenty tokens per parameter. The practical takeaway is that model size and dataset size should grow in tandem, and that a giant model fed too little data simply wastes its own capacity.

Parallelism and the systems problem

No single accelerator can hold a frontier model, so training is spread across many of them in complementary ways. Data parallelism replicates the model and splits the batch, averaging gradients across replicas; tensor parallelism splits individual weight matrices across devices; and pipeline parallelism places different layers on different devices and streams micro-batches through them. Gradient accumulation lets the cluster reach a very large effective batch size without holding it all in memory at once. Orchestrating these so the hardware stays busy, and recovering gracefully from the inevitable node failures, is a substantial distributed-systems problem in its own right.

After pretraining

A freshly pretrained model is good at continuing text but does not yet follow instructions or behave the way a user expects. Supervised fine-tuning on curated instruction-and-response pairs teaches the desired format and tone. After that, preference optimization aligns the model with human judgments: classic RLHF trains a reward model from human comparisons and then optimizes the policy against it with reinforcement learning, while the simpler Direct Preference Optimization skips the separate reward model and optimizes the preference objective directly. These post-training stages use comparatively little compute but disproportionately shape how the finished model feels to use.

Step by step: byte-pair encoding

  1. Start with the text as a list of individual characters.
  2. Count every adjacent pair of symbols.
  3. Merge the single most frequent pair everywhere into one new symbol.
  4. Record the merge and repeat until you reach the target vocabulary size.
  5. To tokenize new text later, apply the learned merges in order.
from collections import Counter

def get_pairs(tokens):
    return Counter(zip(tokens, tokens[1:]))      # adjacent symbol pairs

def merge(tokens, pair):
    out, i = [], 0
    while i < len(tokens):
        if i < len(tokens) - 1 and (tokens[i], tokens[i + 1]) == pair:
            out.append(tokens[i] + tokens[i + 1])   # join the merged pair
            i += 2
        else:
            out.append(tokens[i])
            i += 1
    return out

def train_bpe(text, num_merges):
    tokens, merges = list(text), []
    for _ in range(num_merges):
        pairs = get_pairs(tokens)
        if not pairs:
            break
        best = max(pairs, key=pairs.get)          # most frequent pair
        tokens = merge(tokens, best)
        merges.append(best)
    return tokens, merges

Complexity (time and space)

A useful rule of thumb is that training costs about 6ND floating-point operations, where N is the number of parameters and D the number of training tokens, splitting into roughly two for the forward pass and four for the backward pass per parameter per token. Memory on each device must hold the parameters, their gradients, and the optimizer state, and since Adam keeps two extra values per parameter, that optimizer state often dominates and is commonly sharded across devices. The naive tokenizer trainer shown here is about O(merges times corpus length); production tokenizers use smarter counting to scale to very large corpora.

Worked example

Byte-pair encoding builds up frequent fragments from characters. Trained on a tiny repetitive corpus, the first merges assemble the common piece "low" before moving on to "est" inside the repeated "newest":

toks, merges = train_bpe("low low low lower lower newest newest newest widest", 6)
print(merges)
# [('l', 'o'), ('lo', 'w'), (' ', 'low'), ('e', 's'), ('es', 't'), (' ', 'n')]
# it first builds the frequent fragment 'low' from characters,
# then starts assembling 'est' from the repeated 'newest'.

Follow-up questions

  • Why next-token prediction rather than something more elaborate? It is a self-supervised objective that needs no labels and, applied at scale, forces the model to learn grammar, facts, and reasoning as a side effect.
  • What does the Chinchilla result say? For a fixed compute budget, scale tokens and parameters together (about 20 tokens per parameter); many earlier models were too large for their data.
  • Why AdamW instead of plain SGD? Per-parameter adaptive steps converge faster on the noisy, ill-conditioned loss surfaces of large models, and decoupled weight decay regularizes without distorting that adaptive step.
  • What is perplexity? The exponential of the cross-entropy loss, read as the effective number of tokens the model is choosing among at each position.
  • How do RLHF and DPO differ? Both align to human preferences; RLHF trains a separate reward model and optimizes with reinforcement learning, while DPO optimizes the preference objective directly, which is simpler and more stable.
